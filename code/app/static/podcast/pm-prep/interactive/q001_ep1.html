Measuring Redfin's Hot Home: Start Here, Not There

Before you pick a metric, you need to understand what the feature actually claims — and why that changes everything.

"How do you measure success of the Hot Home feature in Redfin?"

Listen while you work through it
3 decisions · ~8 min

Redfin's Hot Home feature places a badge on listings predicted to sell quickly. It sounds like a straightforward product metrics question — but there's a hidden layer that most PMs miss in the first 30 seconds.

The way you answer this reveals something about how you think about prediction features vs regular product features. Let's find out where your instincts take you.

Before defining any metrics — what's the first thing you'd want to know about the Hot Home feature?

Hot Home makes a prediction: this home will sell quickly. That is fundamentally different from a feature like a saved search or a price estimate. A recommendation feature can succeed by making users feel good. A prediction feature succeeds only if the prediction is correct.

This distinction changes the entire measurement framework. The primary metric is not engagement — it's predictive accuracy: the percentage of homes labelled Hot that actually sold within the predicted window, versus homes not labelled Hot. Without this, every other metric is measuring noise.

You've established that accuracy matters. Now — whose success are you measuring? The feature serves multiple stakeholders.

The stakeholder tension is real. Sellers want their home to be Hot — it signals desirability and can drive urgency among buyers. If Redfin widens the criteria to label more homes as Hot (to please more sellers), accuracy drops. Buyers who trust the badge will act on false signals, tour homes that don't sell quickly, and slowly lose trust in the feature.

So you end up with two utility metrics: buyer tour-to-offer rate on Hot Home listings (did the badge help buyers act on real opportunities?), and seller satisfaction with listing status (do sellers feel represented fairly?). These must be tracked separately — a single blended metric would mask the tension.

You're building the measurement framework. What's the metric most likely to tell you the feature is failing — before it shows up in transaction data?

Trust decay is the lagging failure signal that looks fine in weekly dashboards right up until it collapses. Buyers don't announce when they stop trusting a badge — they just stop acting on it. The early signal is in repeat behaviour: users who acted on Hot Home badges last month and whether they're acting on them again this month. A declining repeat action rate, before any change in aggregate CTR, is your canary.

Key takeaways
01
For prediction features, measure accuracy of the prediction before engagement with it — engagement without accuracy is a vanity metric that masks a failing product.
02
Separate stakeholder success metrics — features that serve buyers and sellers can satisfy one group at the cost of the other. Blending them hides the conflict.
03
Measure trust decay through repeat behaviour, not just current engagement — trust erodes slowly then breaks suddenly, and it shows up in cohort behaviour before aggregate metrics.
