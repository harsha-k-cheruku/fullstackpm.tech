Businesses & Marketplaces from First Principles

The shared lens every company note uses - so patterns become comparable across Meta, Google, Amazon, Nvidia, DoorDash, and Walmart.

0TL;DR

  • Every business does exactly two things: create value (solve a problem / make something abundant) and capture value (a slice of what it created).
  • Durable winners add a flywheel (a self-reinforcing loop) and a moat (why it cannot be copied).
  • The dominant internet pattern is Aggregation Theory: own the demand/user relationship, commoditize the suppliers.
  • Opportunity lives in fragmentation: markets with many small players, high search/trust costs, and no aggregator yet.

1The two questions every business answers

Strip any company to its irreducible core and it answers just two questions:

  1. Value creation - what problem does it solve, or what does it make abundant/cheap/accessible that used to be scarce/expensive/hard? (No value created = no business.)
  2. Value capture - how does it keep a slice of the value it created? (Value created but not captured = a charity or a commodity.)
The gap that is everything

A great business creates a LOT of value and captures a durable slice. Commodities create value but capture almost none (competed to marginal cost). Monopolies capture a lot (sometimes more than they create). The art is a wide, defensible gap between value created and price - and a moat that keeps competitors from closing it.

2The universal 9-point lens

Every company note in this folder answers these nine, in order. Same lens = comparable patterns.

  1. Founding bet / core axiom - the irreducible truth about the world it exploits.
  2. Value creation - what it makes abundant, cheap, or accessible.
  3. Value capture - the monetization mechanism (rake, ads, subscription, toll, margin).
  4. The flywheel - the self-reinforcing loop that compounds with scale.
  5. The moat - why it cannot be copied (see the moat taxonomy).
  6. What it aggregated / commoditized - whose demand it owns, whose supply it made interchangeable.
  7. Unit economics shape - marginal cost, take-rate, CAC vs LTV, gross margin.
  8. Failure modes / vulnerabilities - what could break the flywheel or breach the moat.
  9. The pattern it exemplifies - the transferable lesson.

4The moat taxonomy (why it cannot be copied)

  • Network effects - value rises with users; late entrants face an empty product.
  • Economies of scale - the biggest player has the lowest unit cost.
  • Switching costs - it hurts (time/money/risk) to leave.
  • Brand - trust/identity that commands a premium ("Google it").
  • Proprietary data / process - a compounding asset rivals cannot reproduce.
  • Counter-positioning - incumbents are structurally unable to respond.
  • Regulatory / distribution capture - default placement, licenses, spectrum.

5Aggregation theory (the internet's master pattern)

Ben Thompson, distilled

When distribution is free and transactions are frictionless, power flows to whoever owns the demand (the user relationship), not whoever owns the supply. Aggregators win by delivering a superior experience that pulls in users at zero marginal cost; suppliers are then forced to commoditize themselves to reach those users. Google aggregates intent; Meta aggregates attention; Amazon aggregates consumption. Own demand -> suppliers compete to serve you -> you take the margin.

The strategic implication: the scarce resource online is demand/attention, not supply. Whoever controls the user relationship controls the economics.

6Finding fragmented surfaces (where to build)

Opportunity concentrates where value is created but no one has aggregated it yet. Signals of a fragmented, attackable surface:

  • Many small players, no dominant aggregator - demand is scattered and unowned.
  • High search + trust costs - buyers struggle to find/verify good suppliers.
  • Offline / manual workflows not yet moved online or automated.
  • Underserved long tail - SMBs or niches the incumbents ignore.
  • A verification/judgment gap - AI can now generate, but someone must certify quality (see ../future-of-work/).
The build thesis (ties to your Future-of-Work note)

Aggregate a fragmented surface by owning the demand + the trust + the workflow; let cheap AI supply the labor. The fragmented SMB/seller-enablement layer - many small sellers, high complexity, no trusted analytics aggregator - is exactly this kind of surface.

  • Meta (Facebook + Instagram) - attention -> advertising; social network effects
  • Google (Alphabet) - aggregating intent; the auction
  • Amazon - the retail flywheel + marketplace + AWS
  • Nvidia - picks-and-shovels + CUDA lock-in
