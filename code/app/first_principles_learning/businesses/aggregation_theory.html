Aggregation Theory: Own the Demand, Tax the Flow

The load-bearing value-capture model of the internet era. Own the customer relationship, and the suppliers commoditize themselves fighting to reach you - after which you tax the whole flow, thinly but hugely.

Formalizes the synthesis behind Distribution vs Depth · explains Amazon, Google, Meta, DoorDash · term coined by Ben Thompson · Captured 2026-07-11

0TL;DR

  • The move: build a curated/personalized experience -> own the demand (the customer/attention) -> suppliers must come to you to reach anyone -> supply commoditizes -> you dictate terms.
  • The causation is one-way: owning demand is the lever; "suppliers face more competition" is the consequence, not the cause.
  • Capture: a small tax x enormous volume x ~zero marginal cost = the biggest, most durable slice. A toll booth, not a stickup.
  • Asymmetry: the consumer usually wins (surplus - better/cheaper/curated); the supplier gets squeezed. But the consumer still pays - in attention, data, and lock-in.
  • The ratchet: take rates start small to grow the flywheel, then rise as lock-in deepens (enshittification).
  • It is ONE of four patterns: aggregate demand / own scarce supply / enable producers / invert via membership.
  • Streaming (AVOD/SVOD) is aggregation theory live: AVOD taxes attention, SVOD taxes dollars, hybrid does both - and it is quietly rebundling cable.

1The core mechanism

Aggregation Theory (Ben Thompson) explains why the internet's biggest winners captured value. The classic chain:

1. Build a superior, curated, personalized EXPERIENCE (the demand magnet)
2. -> OWN THE CUSTOMER / ATTENTION (the demand relationship = the asset)
3. -> suppliers now MUST come to you to reach any customer
4. -> suppliers become interchangeable = SUPPLY COMMODITIZES
5. -> you set the terms and TAX THE FLOW (thin cut, gigantic volume)
The one correction most people get wrong

It is not "expose customers to more options, therefore negotiate harder." That gets the causation backwards. The lever is owning the demand relationship. Once you own the customer, the supplier's "more competition" problem is the downstream consequence - they are now one of thousands fighting for your users, so their pricing power evaporates. Own demand first; supply commoditizes itself second.

Contrast the old world: pre-internet, value accrued to whoever controlled distribution + supply (the newspaper's printing press, the studio's theaters, the retailer's shelf). The internet made distribution free, so control shifted to whoever owns demand. That is the entire regime change.

2Why it works now (and not before)

Aggregation is a child of friction death / zero marginal cost. Three collapses had to happen first:

  • Zero cost of distribution - you can serve one more user for ~$0, so you can aggregate global demand, not just local.
  • Zero cost of transactions - you can intermediate billions of tiny interactions profitably.
  • Zero cost of supply onboarding - suppliers (drivers, sellers, creators, listings) can be added at ~$0, so supply is effectively infinite and therefore cheap.
Why this hands power to demand

When distribution and supply both go to ~zero cost, the only scarce thing left is attention / demand. Scarcity migrates to demand - so value does too. (This is the "scarcity migrates" principle from the abundance note, applied to industry structure.)

3The value-capture math

Your instinct - "consolidate the most value in small chunks" - is exactly right. The formula:

Value captured  =  (small take rate)  x  (enormous volume)  x  (~zero marginal cost)
AggregatorPer-unit cut (looks small)Summed (is huge)
Visa / Mastercard~1.5-3% per swipeA tax on ~all of commerce

The genius: take a cut nobody revolts over

Each participant pays a slice small enough that leaving is not worth it, while the aggregator owns the sum. Because marginal cost is ~zero, almost the entire tax drops to the bottom line. A toll booth on a road everyone must use - not a robbery of any one traveler.

4Who wins, who pays (the asymmetry)

The subtle part your synthesis should correct: the consumer usually is not the one squeezed - the supplier is. The aggregator's weapon is delighting the consumer.

PartyWhat they getWhat they pay
ConsumerBig surplus - better, cheaper, curated, convenientAttention, data, lock-in (not always $)
AggregatorThe largest, most durable value poolMust keep the experience great (its only moat)
Supplier / producerReach they could not get aloneMargin, the customer relationship, pricing power
Why suppliers accept the thin slice

A small slice of a huge pie beats 100% of nothing. They trade margin for distribution. The ones who refuse flee to enablers (Shopify/Stripe) to own their own demand - the counter-move (see §9). And "if you are not paying, you are the product" is just this asymmetry stated from the consumer side: the consumer is delighted because they are the inventory being sold to advertisers/suppliers.

5The take-rate ratchet

The take rate is not fixed - it ratchets up. This is the time-dynamic your model was missing.

  • Phase 1 - grow: keep the cut small, subsidize both sides, win the market. Suppliers and users pile in.
  • Phase 2 - lock in: switching cost rises (reviews, ranking, habit, data). Sides can no longer leave cheaply.
  • Phase 3 - extract: raise the cut, add ads, add fees, degrade the free experience. The "small pie" shrinks for the supplier.
Enshittification

Amazon's all-in 3P take (referral + FBA + ad spend to be visible) is now estimated near ~50%, not 15%. App stores hold 30%. This is enshittification (systems-thinking + incentives): a maturing aggregator extracts more and quality decays - which is precisely what opens the door for the next specialist or enabler. The ratchet contains its own counter-force.

6The four value-capture patterns

Aggregation is powerful but it is one of four ways to capture value. Knowing which game you are in is the whole point.

PatternYou own...Margin shapeExamples
Aggregate demandThe customer / attentionThin cut x huge volumeGoogle, Meta, Amazon, DoorDash, Spotify
Own scarce supply (bottleneck)An input nobody can route aroundFAT margin per unitNvidia, TSMC, ASML, Visa's network
Enable producers (arms dealer)The tools producers use to escape aggregatorsSmall % of producer successShopify, Stripe, AWS
Invert via membershipThe relationship; give away the goods marginProfit from the FEE, not the productCostco, Amazon Prime
Your synthesis, placed

What you described - "consolidate value in small chunks while producer and consumer get a thin slice" - is precisely the aggregate-demand quadrant. It is the dominant internet pattern, but the fattest margins often sit in the bottleneck quadrant (own scarce supply), and the most defensible often in enablement (help others own their demand).

7Case study: streaming and the VOD models

Streaming is aggregation theory playing out live - and the acronyms are just which side of the flow you tax.

ModelStands forHow you payWhat it taxesExamples
AVODAdvertising-based Video On DemandFree; you watch adsAttentionYouTube free, Tubi, Netflix/Disney ad tiers
SVODSubscription Video On DemandFlat recurring fee, (usually) no adsDollars + habitNetflix, Disney+, HBO Max, Apple TV+
TVODTransactional Video On DemandPay per title (rent/buy)Per-transactionAmazon/Apple movie rentals
FASTFree Ad-Supported Streaming TVFree, linear "channels" that just playAttention (lean-back)Pluto TV, Samsung TV Plus, Roku Channel

Why this is the same freemium funnel as Spotify

  • AVOD = the free top of funnel (Spotify's free tier) - monetize attention, widen reach, capture non-payers. Pure aggregation: own the eyeballs, sell them.
  • SVOD = the paid tier (Spotify Premium) - monetize dollars + recurring habit, higher ARPU, lower churn.
  • Hybrid (Netflix/Disney adding ad tiers) - the realization that you want both: AVOD to widen the funnel, SVOD for margin. Own the demand, monetize it two ways.
The unbundling -> rebundling paradox, live

Streaming unbundled cable (pick your services a la carte). Now there are too many services, each fragmenting the catalog - so consumers want a bundle again, and FAST/AVOD is quietly rebuilding ad-supported linear "channels." Cable, reincarnated. Value oscillates between bundling and unbundling as friction and economics shift - a paradox worth its own flag.

The streaming twist: nobody fully owns the supply

Unlike Google (owns the index) or Amazon (owns the marketplace), streamers must make or license the content - so, like Spotify with labels, content cost caps margin, which is why everyone is racing to owned originals + ads. Aggregation is strongest when supply is other people's free output (YouTube), weaker when you must fund the supply yourself (Netflix).

8Spot it / build it / defend against it

Spot an aggregator

  1. Who owns the demand relationship (the default place users start)?
  2. Is supply commoditized - are suppliers interchangeable and price-taking?
  3. Is value captured as a thin cut of huge volume at ~zero marginal cost?
  4. Is the experience (curation/personalization) the moat, not owning assets?

Build one

  • Win the consumer experience first (that is the demand magnet) - subsidize the hard side to solve cold-start.
  • Make onboarding supply ~free so supply floods in and commoditizes.
  • Grow the flywheel with a small take; raise it only once switching cost is deep.

Defend against one (if you are a supplier)

  • Own your own demand - direct relationship, brand, email/community (the Shopify/DTC escape).
  • Differentiate off the commodity axis - taste, identity, trust (the depth play).
  • Become a bottleneck - own a scarce input the aggregator cannot route around.

9Limits & counter-moves

Where aggregation weakens or breaks

1. You must fund your own supply. If supply is not other people's free output, content/inventory cost caps margin (Netflix, Spotify).

2. Arm-the-rebels enablers. Shopify/Stripe/Substack let producers own their demand - structurally anti-aggregator.

3. Regulation. Own too much demand and you become a gatekeeper - antitrust, app-store rules, DMA.

4. A new surface. AI agents may re-intermediate discovery ("agent, find/buy/play X"), aggregating the aggregators - the next surface war (see Amazon / Spotify).

5. The ratchet's own backlash. Over-extraction (enshittification) degrades the experience - the one thing the moat depends on - opening the door to the next entrant.
