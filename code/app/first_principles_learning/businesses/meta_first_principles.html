Meta (Facebook + Instagram) from First Principles

A business built directly on a psychology axiom: humans are obligately social, attention is scarce, and attention converts to money through an ad auction.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/businesses · Same 9-point lens as its siblings · See The shared lens · Numbers approximate, framed as of ~2023-2024.

0TL;DR

  • Core axiom: humans are obligately social (psychology axiom #4) and attention is finite. Meta manufactures social connection at zero marginal cost, then sells the resulting attention to advertisers.
  • Value creation: it makes connection, self-expression, and distribution/reach abundant and free for ~3.9B+ people (family daily actives). Anyone can now broadcast to the world.
  • Value capture: ~98% of revenue is advertising - a real-time auction that prices access to your attention and targets it with behavioral data. Total revenue ~$135B+ (2023).
  • Moat: direct + social network effects, social-graph switching costs, a behavioral-data flywheel, and the Instagram + WhatsApp acquisitions that closed off the two biggest escape routes.
  • Shape: near-zero marginal cost per user, ~80% gross margin, ARPU that varies ~10x by geography (US/Canada richest). It aggregates attention and commoditizes creators and publishers.
  • Vulnerabilities: platform/attention shifts (TikTok), Apple's ATT privacy change (a ~$10B/yr hit), antitrust, teen churn, and Reality Labs burning ~$15-18B/yr on an unproven bet.

1Founding bet / core axiom

Strip Meta to bedrock and it rests on two irreducible truths about people and the world:

  1. Humans are obligately social. This is not a preference, it is wiring. Belonging, status, comparison, and reciprocity are survival-grade drives (this is exactly psychology axiom #4 in ../psychology/). A tool that lets people see and be seen by their tribe taps a bottomless need.
  2. Attention is scarce and monetizable. Each person has a fixed ~16 waking hours. Whoever captures a durable share of those hours holds something advertisers will pay dearly to reach.
The founding bet

Build the thing humans cannot not use (a place to be social), give it away free so distribution is unlimited, and monetize the byproduct - attention - via advertising. The genius is that the product and the raw material are the same thing: human attention, freely given.

Psychology tie-in

Meta does not just serve the social drive, it engineers around the variable-reward loop. The pull-to-refresh feed, the unpredictable like/comment/tag, and the notification badge are a slot-machine schedule (intermittent reinforcement) delivering dopamine on an unpredictable timetable. Predictable rewards habituate; variable ones compel. That is why the feed is infinite and the counts are always one tap away.

2Value creation (what it makes abundant)

Meta took things that were scarce, expensive, or gate-kept and made them free and universal:

Used to beMeta makes it
Staying in touch required proximity, phone calls, lettersPassive, continuous awareness of hundreds of people at zero effort
Self-expression / an audience required media gatekeepersAnyone can broadcast photos, video, opinion to the world for free
Reach / distribution was owned by TV, radio, newspapersA local baker or a global brand can reach exactly its audience
Finding your people (niche communities) was hardGroups, Pages, DMs collapse the cost of coordination to ~zero

The genuinely new thing is cheap, targeted distribution for everyone. Before Meta/Google, a small business could not affordably reach 500 local dog owners. Now it can, for a few dollars. That is real value created for ~10M+ advertisers, most of them small.

Why the value is real, not just extractive

The critique "it is just addiction" undersells it. A grandparent seeing a grandchild's photos, a diaspora staying connected, a niche seller finding customers - these are real surpluses. Meta creates a lot of value. The interesting question is always how durable a slice it captures, and at what social cost.

3Value capture (attention -> the ad auction)

The product is free. The users are not the customer - the advertiser is. Roughly 98% of revenue is advertising (the rest is trivial hardware / Reality Labs). The mechanism:

  1. Harvest attention + behavioral data. Every scroll, like, dwell-time, click, and (historically) off-platform activity feeds a profile of interests and predicted actions.
  2. Auction each impression. When you open the app, an ad auction runs in milliseconds. Advertisers bid, but Meta does not simply pick the highest bid - it ranks by expected value = bid x estimated action rate (will you click/convert) + estimated ad quality. Better prediction lets Meta charge more while showing you ads you are likelier to want.
  3. Target precisely. Advertisers pick audiences by demographics, interests, behaviors, lookalikes, and custom lists. Precision is the product Meta actually sells - the ability to waste fewer impressions than TV ever could.
  4. Price by outcome. Billing is typically CPM (cost per thousand impressions) under the hood, but advertisers optimize toward CPC or cost-per-action. Better targeting -> higher conversion -> advertisers bid more -> higher effective CPM.
The capture insight

Meta does not sell ad space. It sells prediction - the probability that a given human will take a given action. The scarce, defensible asset is not the pixels, it is the model that ranks 3.9B people by likelihood-to-convert. That is why more data literally equals more revenue per user.

4The flywheel

Meta runs two interlocking loops - a user loop and a money loop - that feed each other.

The loop

More users -> more engagement + behavioral data -> better targeting/prediction -> higher ad ROI -> more advertisers bidding -> more revenue per impression -> reinvest in product, R&D, and acquisitions -> more/stickier users -> (repeat).

Two sub-loops make it especially hard to stop:

  • Social network loop (user side): each new user makes the network more valuable to existing users (your friends are here), which pulls in more users. Classic direct network effect.
  • Data/auction loop (money side): more engagement -> more signal -> better prediction -> a denser auction -> advertisers get more ROI and cannot afford to leave -> funds more product that drives more engagement.

Acquisitions are the accelerant, not the core: buying Instagram (~$1B, 2012) and WhatsApp (~$19B, 2014) plugged the two loops into the fastest-growing new attention pools before they could become rivals.

5The moat

Several moats stacked, which is why frontal assaults keep failing:

  • Direct network effects: a social app with no friends is worthless. A new entrant faces the cold-start problem; Meta faces it for no one.
  • Social switching costs: your identity, photos, friend graph, groups, and history live here. Leaving means abandoning your social capital, and coordinating a whole graph to move at once is nearly impossible.
  • Data moat: a decade-plus of labeled behavioral data trains targeting models a new rival cannot reproduce at any price - the data only exists because the users are already here.
  • The acquisition moat: Instagram closed the "young / visual / mobile-native" escape route; WhatsApp closed the "private messaging" one. Meta bought its two most dangerous futures. This is the loop-closing move that antitrust now attacks.
  • Scale economics: R&D, infra, and ad-tech costs are spread over ~3.9B users, so per-user cost is trivially low - a would-be competitor cannot match the feature velocity.
Where the moat is thin

Network effects lock in a graph, not attention. TikTok proved you do not need your friends to be entertained - an algorithmic content feed can steal hours without touching the social graph. Meta's graph moat did not protect its time-spent moat. That is the crack Reels was a defensive response to.

6What it aggregated / commoditized

Straight out of aggregation theory (see the lens, section 5): own demand, commoditize supply.

  • Aggregated: human attention. Meta owns the demand side - the eyeballs and the relationship with them - at a scale nobody else touches outside Google.
  • Commoditized creators: influencers and publishers depend on Meta's feed for reach, but the algorithm decides who is seen. No single creator has leverage; they are interchangeable inputs competing to fill the feed for free.
  • Commoditized publishers/news: media companies became suppliers of content that keeps users scrolling, while Meta kept the ad dollars. The publisher's reader relationship was intermediated away - the reason the news industry's ad economics collapsed.
The aggregator's leverage

Because Meta owns demand and suppliers are fragmented and interchangeable, it dictates terms: it can down-rank news, throttle organic reach to sell "boosts," or change the feed overnight - and suppliers must adapt because that is where the audience lives. Owning demand is the whole game.

7Unit economics shape

The economics of bits: build once, serve billions.

  • Near-zero marginal cost per user: one more user costs a sliver of server + bandwidth. Revenue from that user is close to pure contribution margin.
  • High gross margin: roughly 80% gross margin on the ads business - characteristic of zero-marginal-cost software.
  • ARPU varies ~10x by geography. Advertisers pay for purchasing power, so the same minute of attention is worth far more in the US than in India.

Ad load is the throttle

Revenue = users x time-spent x ad load (ads per unit of content) x price-per-ad. Meta can dial revenue up by raising ad load, but too many ads degrades the experience and pushes users away - a self-limiting lever. Long-run growth must come from better prediction (higher price per ad) and new surfaces (Reels, messaging ads), not just cramming in more ads.

The counterweight is Reality Labs: the metaverse/AR bet burns roughly $15-18B per year with negligible revenue, a deliberate drag on otherwise stellar margins in search of the "next platform" that would free Meta from Apple's and Google's OS control.

The structural lesson from ATT

Meta built its money loop on data it collected across a device it does not own. Apple owns the OS; when Apple changed one setting, Meta lost billions. An aggregator that rents its distribution/data layer from a platform owner is one policy change from a haircut. Owning the graph did not save it - it did not own the pipe.

9The pattern it exemplifies

Meta is the canonical stack of attention -> advertising layered on social network effects, riding zero marginal cost distribution - textbook aggregation theory on the demand side.

The transferable lesson

If you can (1) build on an irreducible human drive so people cannot not use the product, (2) give it away free so distribution is unlimited, and (3) monetize the byproduct (attention/data) rather than charging the user, you get a business with near-infinite reach and enormous margins. The moat is the network + the data flywheel; the recurring risk is that you may not own the platform your distribution rides on.

  • Same family as: Google (aggregates intent instead of attention, same ad-auction capture) and, more loosely, any free ad-funded feed.
  • The generalizable move: find a scarce, monetizable resource (here, attention) generated as a byproduct of a free service people are compelled to use, then build a prediction machine to price it.
  • The cautionary half: engineered variable-reward loops create engagement and backlash in equal measure - regulatory, reputational, and generational risk grow with the very mechanism that drives the numbers.
