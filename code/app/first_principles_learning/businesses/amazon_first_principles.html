Amazon from First Principles

Three stacked businesses in one flywheel: (a) 1P retail, (b) the 3P Marketplace, (c) AWS. The retail loses cushion; ads and AWS pay the bills.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/businesses · Applies the shared 9-point lens · The shared lens

0TL;DR

  • Amazon is not one company - it is three stacked businesses: 1P retail (Amazon buys and resells), the 3P Marketplace (other sellers sell, Amazon rakes), and AWS (cloud infrastructure utility).
  • The core axiom: customers will always want lower prices, more selection, and faster delivery. Bezos said invest in what stays constant, and reinvest cash flow rather than protect margin.
  • Retail runs on razor-thin margin plus enormous scale and a negative working-capital cycle (collect from buyers before paying suppliers) that self-funds growth.
  • The real profit engines are the hidden high-margin layers: 3P seller fees + FBA + advertising, and AWS. These subsidize cheap retail.
  • The flywheel (the Bezos napkin): lower prices -> more customers -> more sellers/selection -> better cost structure -> even lower prices. Growth compounds itself.
  • 3P advertising is the same pattern as Google/Meta: aggregate demand, then charge suppliers for visibility on your shelf.

1Founding bet / core axiom

Bezos built the whole company on things that do not change. Ask what customers will want in ten years and the answer is boring and permanent: lower prices, more selection, faster delivery. Nobody will ever wake up wanting fewer choices, higher prices, and slower shipping. So you can safely pour a decade of investment into those axes and know the effort keeps paying off.

The Bezos inversion

Most companies optimize for reported earnings and defend margin. Amazon inverted this: maximize long-run free cash flow per share, and reinvest it into the constants (price, selection, speed) instead of dropping it to the bottom line. Thin margin is a feature, not a bug - it is deferred market share.

The second axiom is cash flow, not accounting profit. Amazon spent roughly its first two decades near breakeven on purpose, plowing every dollar of cash back into fulfillment centers, price cuts, and new bets. The bet: a company that owns the customer relationship at massive scale can monetize later on its own terms.

2Value creation

Each of the three businesses makes something abundant that used to be scarce or expensive.

BusinessWhat it makes abundant / cheap
1P retailSelection + convenience + low price. One store with near-infinite shelf, delivered to your door, often cheaper than local retail.
3P MarketplaceInstant distribution + trust + logistics for any small seller. A garage brand gets Amazon's traffic, checkout, reviews, and (via FBA) same-scale delivery.
AWSTurns capex infrastructure into on-demand opex utility. Servers, storage, and databases become a metered tap you turn on in minutes instead of a data center you build in months.

The AWS insight is the sharpest: Amazon took its own hard-won, capital-heavy infrastructure competence and repackaged a fixed cost as a variable service. A startup no longer needs a million dollars and six months to launch - it rents compute by the hour. That is genuine value creation: it lowered the barrier to building software for the entire economy.

3Value capture

This is where the three businesses diverge sharply - and where the popular picture of Amazon is usually wrong.

The hidden gem

Advertising is the quiet miracle. Because Amazon owns the point of purchase intent (shoppers already searching to buy), an ad on Amazon converts far better than a display ad. Sellers already commoditized on the shelf now pay Amazon a second time just to be visible. This is the same "charge suppliers for attention" mechanic as Google and Meta - but attached directly to the buy button.

Follow the profit

For long stretches, North America + International retail operating income was roughly breakeven or negative, while AWS supplied the majority of total operating income. Ads increasingly stack on top. Retail is the customer-acquisition and demand-aggregation machine; ads and AWS are where the money is actually made.

4The flywheel

The famous napkin sketch Bezos drew describes a self-reinforcing loop with no obvious starting point:

lower prices -> more customers -> more traffic -> more sellers/selection -> better cost structure (scale) -> lower prices

  1. Lower prices pull in more customers.
  2. More customers mean more traffic and demand on the site.
  3. More demand attracts more third-party sellers, which expands selection.
  4. More selection + volume improves the customer experience and drives more scale.
  5. Scale lowers unit cost (fulfillment, buying power), which funds still-lower prices. Loop repeats.
The second, financial loop

Bolted onto the growth flywheel is a cash loop: generate free cash flow -> reinvest into fulfillment, price, and new bets -> grow the base -> generate more cash flow. The negative working-capital cycle (see section 7) makes the flywheel partly self-funding - growth throws off cash instead of consuming it.

Crucially, the 3P Marketplace supercharges the loop: Amazon gets more selection without paying for the inventory, and each 3P sale is higher-margin than a 1P sale. Sellers do the assortment expansion for free; Amazon captures the rake and the ad dollars.

5The moat

  • Economies of scale - the largest buyer and shipper in retail has the lowest unit cost. Hard to out-price the low-cost operator.
  • Logistics / fulfillment network - hundreds of fulfillment centers, sortation, and last-mile delivery are a multi-decade, tens-of-billions capex moat. You cannot rent same-day delivery into existence.
  • Prime switching costs / lock-in - once you pay the annual fee and lean on fast free shipping (plus video, music, etc.), you default to Amazon for every purchase. The subscription pre-commits your future demand.
  • 3P network effects - more buyers attract more sellers attract more selection attract more buyers. A classic two-sided marketplace loop.
  • AWS switching costs + scale - once your app is built on AWS-specific services, migrating is painful and risky. Meanwhile AWS's scale lets it keep cutting prices, the same supply-side moat as retail.
Stacked moats

Amazon is unusual because it stacks nearly every moat type at once: scale economies (retail + AWS), network effects (3P), switching costs (Prime + AWS), and a proprietary logistics process. Breaching one does not breach the others.

6What it aggregated / commoditized

In the language of Aggregation Theory (see the shared lens): Amazon aggregates consumption / demand. It owns the relationship with the buyer - the place people go to search for and buy things.

What it commoditizes is the supply side: sellers and brands. To reach Amazon's aggregated demand, a brand must compete on Amazon's shelf, price against every rival on the same page, accept Amazon's rules, and - increasingly - buy Amazon's ads to be seen at all. The seller is interchangeable; the demand is scarce and Amazon owns it.

The seller's trap

Once Amazon is your main channel you are dependent on it, yet you compete against every other seller (and sometimes against Amazon's own private-label products) on your own product page. You created the value; Amazon captures a widening slice via fees + FBA + ads. This is aggregation working exactly as designed - painful for suppliers, great for the aggregator.

7Unit economics shape

  • Razor-thin retail margin - 1P operating margin is low single digits at best. The model only works at enormous volume.
  • Negative working capital / cash conversion cycle - Amazon collects cash from customers immediately but pays suppliers on ~30-60 day terms. It effectively uses supplier money to fund operations and growth. A negative cash conversion cycle means growth generates cash.
  • High-margin ads + AWS subsidize retail - the loss-leader retail acquires and locks in customers; the profit is harvested elsewhere. This cross-subsidy is the whole financial trick.
  • FCF over EPS - Amazon guides and thinks in free cash flow, not earnings per share. Depreciation-heavy investment can suppress reported profit while cash generation stays strong.
  • 3P is margin-accretive - each 3P unit carries a fee/ad rake with no inventory risk, so shifting mix toward 3P lifts blended margins even as headline retail stays thin.
The mental model

Picture retail as a giant, low-margin funnel that captures and holds demand, financed partly by suppliers' float, with two high-margin taps - advertising and AWS - drilled into the side. The funnel does not need to be profitable; it just needs to keep the taps flowing.

8Failure modes / vulnerabilities

  • Regulatory / antitrust - the sharpest risk. Owning the marketplace, competing on it with private label, AND charging sellers for visibility invites self-preferencing claims. Forced separation of these roles would fracture the flywheel.
  • AWS competition - Microsoft Azure and Google Cloud are well-funded and growing fast. Price competition or a share of AI-workload wins shifting to rivals would erode the main profit engine.
  • Logistics cost - fuel, labor, wages, and last-mile expense are structurally rising. Fast delivery is capital- and labor-intensive; margin pressure is constant.
  • Seller discontent - as total take (fees + FBA + ads) climbs toward a large share of a seller's revenue, sellers seek other channels (Shopify, TikTok Shop, direct). If the supply side revolts, selection suffers.
  • Thin retail margin - with so little cushion, a demand shock, a bad capex cycle, or an over-build of fulfillment capacity can quickly turn retail loss-making (as it did during over-expansion periods).

9The pattern it exemplifies

Amazon is the canonical stack of three business "physics": economies of scale + the flywheel + aggregation-then-monetize-suppliers-with-ads. Retail wins on scale; the flywheel makes growth compound; the marketplace and ads monetize the aggregated demand.

The transferable lesson

Own the demand at scale first, even at breakeven, then monetize the suppliers who need to reach that demand. Amazon's 3P advertising is structurally identical to Google and Meta's model: aggregate demand/attention, then charge suppliers for visibility. Amazon just attached it to the moment of purchase, where intent is highest and ads convert best.

Build takeaway

The most durable position is to own a demand relationship and let suppliers commoditize themselves to reach it. Bonus points if you can layer a second, higher-margin business (ads, a utility like AWS) on top of a thin-margin demand-aggregation core. The thin core is the moat; the layers are the profit.

10Glossary

TermMeaning
FlywheelAmazon's self-reinforcing loop: lower prices -> more customers -> more sellers/selection -> scale -> lower prices.
1P (first-party)Amazon buys inventory and resells it; it owns the goods and the margin/risk.
3P (third-party)Independent sellers sell on Amazon's Marketplace; Amazon takes a fee instead of owning inventory. ~50% of units.
FBA (Fulfillment by Amazon)3P sellers ship inventory to Amazon, which stores, packs, and delivers it (often Prime-eligible) for a fee.
Take rate / rakeThe percentage of each 3P sale Amazon keeps (referral fee ~8-15%, plus FBA fees).
Cash conversion cycleTime between paying suppliers and collecting from customers. Amazon's is negative - it collects first, pays later, so growth funds itself.
FCF (free cash flow)Cash generated after capex. Amazon optimizes for FCF per share, not accounting EPS.
Prime lock-inAnnual subscription (fast free shipping + media) that pre-commits members' future purchases to Amazon - a switching-cost moat.
Utility computingSelling compute/storage as a metered, on-demand service (AWS) - turning capex infrastructure into opex.
Negative working capitalOperating on suppliers' float: customer cash arrives before supplier bills are due, financing growth.
Aggregation theoryOwn the demand relationship, commoditize suppliers, and take the margin. Amazon aggregates consumption.
