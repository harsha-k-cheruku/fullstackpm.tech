The Build Playbook

Personal strategy: what to build, given domain knowledge but little proprietary data (yet).

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/build-playbook · Captured 2026-07-11 · Cashes out ../future-of-work/, ../economics-of-ai/, ../businesses/

0TL;DR

  • You are not moat-poor. You have 3 of the 4 moats (domain judgment, distribution, trust). The 4th (proprietary data) is the one you MANUFACTURE by shipping.
  • The highest-leverage move: encode your tacit domain judgment into agents + shared skills - convert knowledge (trapped in your head) into an asset (runs without you, improves for free).
  • Build where you are the cheap verifier - tasks where your domain knowledge makes checking the output cheap for you and expensive for everyone else.
  • Shipping mints the data: usage generates the labeled examples + eval sets that become your durable moat.
  • One line: own the judgment, rent the intelligence, and let shipping mint the data.

1The reframe: you are conflating "raw data" with "moat"

"I don't own much proprietary data" is true - but that is only ONE of the four moats from the Economics of AI note, and it is the only one you build by shipping rather than the one you need to start. Most builders have the opposite problem (data but no distribution or trust). Your starting hand is actually stronger.

The core correction

A moat is not just proprietary data. It is domain judgment + distribution + trust + data. You already have the first three. The move is not "go get data" - it is "use what you have to start the loop that generates the data."

Hidden asset you already have

You have been accumulating proprietary data without naming it: the WBR metric glossary, the qualify-IB logic, manager lookups, SQL query assets, "settled data definitions." That curated, hard-won institutional logic IS proprietary data. Nobody outside your org can reproduce it. It is a moat wearing work clothes.

3The highest-leverage move

Do this

Encode your tacit domain judgment into agents + shared skills.

Right now your most valuable asset - knowing how launches really work, what a real qualified inbound is, why the AOP number was wrong, what a good WBR looks like - lives in your head and dies when you are busy. Encoding converts that knowledge (illiquid, trapped in you) into an asset (runs without you, improves as the models under it improve). This is exactly what Caddie already is: encoded judgment. You have started; the move is to do it deliberately and repeatedly.

4The cheap-verifier rule (what to build)

The filter

Build agents for tasks where YOUR domain knowledge makes verification cheap for you and expensive for everyone else.

From the Future-of-Work note: value concentrates where generation is cheap but verification is hard. An outsider can prompt an LLM to "analyze launches," but they cannot tell if the answer is right - you can, instantly. Your judgment is the eval function. Build where you are the judge; that is your comparative advantage made concrete.

5The data flywheel (how you mint the missing moat)

You do not buy the 4th moat - you generate it by shipping:

Encode judgment -> ship a tool -> people use it
        ^                                   |
        |                                   v
   better tool  <- eval set + feedback <- usage data
   (proprietary)     (what they ask,
                      what's right/wrong)

Every question a manager asks Caddie, every answer you correct, every metric definition you lock in = labeled examples only you own. After six months of use, that eval set + those definitions ARE the moat - and they compound while you sleep (the appreciating-asset principle from Economics of AI).

6The ranked build menu (your world)

Ordered by leverage-per-effort. Start at 1 and 2 - they build 3-5 for free and start the flywheel with the distribution + trust you already have.

  1. Judgment-encoding analyst agents (started - Caddie). One per recurring analytical job: WBR-prep, launch-quality QA, target-vs-actuals, coaching briefs. Moat: your metric logic. Verifier: you.
  2. Skills, not just agents (DRY). Factor repeated knowledge - the metric glossary, qualify-IB rules, SQL patterns - into shared skills many agents reference. Your knowledge base as code: the most durable, most reusable, hardest-to-copy asset.
  3. A "definitions / source-of-truth" agent. The thing that ended the AOP-vs-WBR confusion. Encodes what each metric really means so nobody relitigates. Pure encoded judgment; huge org value; zero external data needed.
  4. Workflow / pipeline agents (the WBR scaffolder pattern). Automates a process, not a task. High stickiness = distribution moat.
  5. A verification / QA agent (the critic, kept separate from the doer). Checks other analysts' numbers against your locked definitions. This one IS your judgment, productized - the purest expression of your edge.

7Asset-inventory checklist (revisit quarterly)

Tick these as your moats grow - it turns the abstract strategy into a scoreboard:

  • Have I encoded at least one new piece of tacit judgment into an agent/skill this quarter?
  • Is my repeated knowledge factored into shared skills (DRY), not copy-pasted across agents?
  • Do I have a single source-of-truth for my metric definitions that agents reference?
  • Is at least one tool in front of real users (using my distribution)?
  • Am I capturing usage (questions asked, answers corrected) as a growing eval set?
  • Is there a separate verifier checking the doer (not self-checking)?
  • Am I building things that improve for free as the underlying models improve?
  • Am I pricing/positioning for outcomes + trust, not raw output volume?

8Synthesis

The one-line strategy

You don't lack a moat - you lack the habit of encoding. Convert your domain judgment into agents + shared skills, ship them to the audience and trust you already own, and let usage manufacture the proprietary data you don't yet have. Own the judgment, rent the intelligence, and let shipping mint the data.

This maps straight onto the businesses capstone: be a deep wrapper, not a thin one - own the domain + workflow + trust, and let cheap intelligence supply the labor. That is the durable position the first principles point to.
