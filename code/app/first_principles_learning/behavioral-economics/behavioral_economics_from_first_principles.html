Behavioral Economics from First Principles

What you get when the predictably-irrational human mind meets decisions about money, time, and risk.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/behavioral-economics · Captured 2026-07-11 · Builds on ../psychology/

0TL;DR

  • Behavioral Economics = Psychology x Economics: the study of how real humans decide, versus the rational-actor ideal.
  • Loss aversion, present bias, time preference, herding, and animal spirits are its core concepts; behavioral finance covers the market/crowd ones.
  • Incentives & moral hazard are the odd ones out - they belong to Information Economics / Contract Theory, and assume rational actors with bad information/incentives, not buggy cognition.
  • Behavioral problems are fixed with nudges/framing; incentive problems are fixed by redesigning the contract.

1What it is (the umbrella)

These concepts are what you get when you take the messy, predictably-irrational human mind (psychology) and point it at decisions about money, time, and risk (economics). That intersection is Behavioral Economics, and its market-facing cousin is Behavioral Finance. A few items predate behavioral econ and have older homes (noted below).

3The four families

  1. Time & impatience - Present Bias, Time Preference. How humans value the future (badly). Present bias is the behavioral version; time preference is the older classical version of the same phenomenon.
  2. Risk & value - Loss Aversion. How we evaluate gains vs losses under uncertainty (Prospect Theory). The single most important idea in behavioral econ.
  3. Social & crowd dynamics - Herding, Animal Spirits. What happens when individually-irrational moods propagate through a market or group. Behavioral econ meeting finance and macro.
  4. Information & incentives - Incentives, Moral Hazard. The odd one out: from Information Economics / mechanism design. Assumes rational actors who behave badly because information is asymmetric and incentives are misaligned. (Moral hazard = "I take more risk because someone else bears the cost.")

4The distinction worth internalizing

Two attacks on the rational actor

Behavioral economics (loss aversion, present bias, herding, animal spirits): humans are predictably irrational - their cognition deviates from the rational model.

Information economics (incentives, moral hazard, adverse selection): humans may be perfectly rational, but asymmetric information and misaligned incentives still produce bad outcomes.

They are taught together but break the model from different angles - which changes the fix (see below).

5First-principles tie-in

Notice the through-line to the psychology axioms: loss aversion, present bias, time preference, herding, and animal spirits are all economic mechanisms that fall out of axiom #6 (reward + loss aversion), #5 (emotion first), and #4 (social). Behavioral economics is, quite literally, "the mechanisms of human psychology, applied to money" - the same axiom -> mechanism pattern used for LLMs and agents.

6How to use it

  • Cognition problem (bias) -> fix with a nudge / reframe: default options, loss-framing, social proof, commitment devices, reducing friction.
  • Information / incentive problem -> fix by redesigning the contract/incentives: align rewards with outcomes, add skin-in-the-game, reduce asymmetry (transparency, monitoring).
  • Diagnostic question: "Is the person confused/impulsive (behavioral) or responding rationally to a bad incentive (information)?" The answer tells you which lever to pull.

7Glossary

TermMeaning
Prospect TheoryModel of how people value gains/losses relative to a reference point; losses loom larger.
Hyperbolic discountingWe discount near-future rewards far more steeply than distant ones - the math of present bias.
Time preferenceThe rate at which we prefer good now over good later.
HerdingCopying the crowd's actions rather than one's own information.
Animal spiritsKeynes's term for the emotion-driven confidence that moves markets.
Moral hazardTaking more risk because someone else bears the cost.
Adverse selectionBad outcomes from hidden information before a deal (e.g. only sick people buy insurance).
Principal-agent problemAn agent acting for a principal has different incentives/information; core of moral hazard.
NudgeA change to choice architecture that steers behavior without removing options.
