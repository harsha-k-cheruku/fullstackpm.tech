Need 5: Financial Security

Store, move, and grow money. An ancient safety need served today by invisible rails that skim a few basis points off nearly every dollar that moves.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/human-needs · Captured 2026-07-11 · See The needs framework · #1 Visa, #2 Mastercard / Stripe

0TL;DR

  • The need splits into three jobs: move money (payments), store money (banking), grow money (investing). Payments has the strongest moat, so most of this note lives there.
  • Trust is the real currency. Money only works if both sides believe the value will clear. Every mechanism - a bank, a card network, a stablecoin - is fundamentally selling trust plus settlement.
  • Visa and Mastercard are near-monopoly toll roads. They own the rails in a four-party card model and take a tiny cut (roughly 0.1-0.15% network fee) of an enormous volume - Visa alone clears on the order of 13-15 trillion USD per year.
  • Tiny percentage x gigantic volume = extraordinary margins. Visa runs a roughly 65% operating margin; Mastercard is similar. This is arguably the most durable moat on earth - a two-sided network effect that has compounded for 60+ years.
  • Stripe attacks from a different angle: not new rails, but a developer-first software layer on top of Visa/MC that owns the integration and the SMB/online-business relationship. Blended pricing about 2.9% + 30c.
  • The frontier is embedded finance - every SaaS product becoming a fintech - while stablecoins and instant-settlement networks (FedNow, RTP) poke at the rails themselves.

1The need as an axiom

Sitting in Maslow tier 2 (safety), financial security is the want to store value, move value, and grow value with confidence that it will still be there and still be accepted tomorrow. It is older than money itself: grain stored in a temple granary, clay tablets recording debts in Mesopotamia, tally sticks, gold on deposit with a goldsmith who issues a receipt that itself starts circulating as paper money.

Strip it to the bone and the axiom is: a human holds value now and needs to (a) keep it safe over time, (b) transfer it to someone else, or (c) make it larger. The caveman traded a spear for a pelt (moving value); the billionaire wires a fund (moving value at scale). Same job.

Trust is the core currency

Money is a shared fiction that only functions because everyone trusts everyone else to honor it. So every financial mechanism is really a trust-and-settlement machine: a bank promises your deposit is safe; a card network promises the merchant will get paid; a clearing house promises the two ledgers will reconcile. Whoever is trusted to sit in the middle and guarantee settlement captures the value. Fraud is the tax on broken trust, and managing it is a core cost of the business.

2The demand flow

Moving money along the "payment" branch of the need follows a repeatable pipeline. Understanding it shows exactly who gets paid and why:

NEED -> Transaction trigger -> Rails/Network -> Trust/Fraud check -> Settlement -> Fees split
  • Transaction trigger: a buyer wants a good/service and reaches for a payment instrument (a card, a wallet, a bank transfer).
  • Rails/Network: the instrument routes the request over a shared network (Visa, Mastercard, ACH, RTP).
  • Trust/Fraud: the network and banks authorize - is the card real, is there money, is this fraud? This risk-check is the value-add.
  • Settlement: the actual movement of funds between banks, usually a day or more after authorization (batch settlement).
  • Fees split: everyone in the chain takes a slice; the biggest slice (interchange) goes to the buyer's bank.

The four-party card model (the thing to actually understand)

A card swipe looks instant, but five parties are involved. This structure is why the whole industry is shaped the way it is:

PartyWho they areRole in the flow
CardholderThe buyerPresents the card; wants convenience and protection.
Issuing bank (issuer)The buyer's bank (e.g. Chase)Issued the card; approves the transaction; fronts the money; earns interchange.
MerchantThe sellerWants the sale; pays a merchant discount fee to accept cards.
Acquiring bank (acquirer)The merchant's bank / processorConnects the merchant to the network; deposits funds; earns a markup.
NetworkVisa / Mastercard in the middleRoutes the message between issuer and acquirer; sets interchange rules; takes a small network fee. Owns no money, only the switch.

Money flow (simplified): the cardholder buys; the acquirer asks the network; the network asks the issuer; the issuer approves and later sends funds (minus interchange) to the acquirer (minus its markup) to the merchant. So on a 100.00 USD sale a merchant might net roughly 97.50-98.00 after all fees.

The key insight

The network (Visa/MC) never lends money and never takes credit risk. It just runs the switch in the middle and charges a tiny toll for the guaranteed routing. That is why it is such a clean, high-margin business - the banks carry the risk and the balance sheet; the network carries only the rails.

3#1 Visa - how it wins

Visa is the toll booth on a huge fraction of the world's consumer spending. It processes on the order of 200+ billion transactions a year across roughly 4+ billion cards and something like 13-15 trillion USD of payments volume. It does not issue cards, does not lend, does not carry consumer credit risk. It sells one thing: the trusted network that connects every issuer to every acquirer.

The two-sided network effect

This is the whole moat in one sentence: cardholders carry Visa because merchants accept it, and merchants accept Visa because cardholders carry it. Each new cardholder makes the network more valuable to merchants, and each new merchant makes it more valuable to cardholders. That flywheel has compounded since the 1960s and is now effectively impossible to replicate - you cannot bootstrap a new global network from zero because neither side will join before the other.

The economics

  • Take rate: Visa keeps only a sliver - roughly 0.10-0.15% of volume as its own net revenue (the fat interchange slice goes to issuing banks, not Visa).
  • But the volume is colossal: a tiny percentage of ~14 trillion USD is ~30+ billion USD of revenue.
  • The cost to run the switch barely grows with volume, so margins are extraordinary: Visa posts roughly a 65% operating margin and net margins north of 50%.
  • Almost zero marginal cost per extra transaction - the rails are already built, so each incremental swipe is nearly pure profit.
The most durable moat on earth

Visa combines a two-sided network effect, near-zero marginal cost, a 60-year head start, regulatory entrenchment, and no balance-sheet risk. It is a near-monopoly toll on money movement itself. Warren Buffett-style investors love it precisely because the moat widens every year the world goes more cashless - the need is ancient and Visa collects a micro-toll on more of it every day.

4#2 Mastercard + Stripe (two different challengers)

Mastercard - the same model, the #2 network

Mastercard is essentially Visa's mirror image: the same four-party model, the same two-sided network effect, the same take-rate economics, just at a somewhat smaller scale (roughly two-thirds of Visa's volume). Together the two form a global duopoly over card rails. Mastercard runs comparable ~55-60% operating margins. There is little to differentiate strategically - it is a duopoly precisely because a second network is useful for redundancy and negotiating leverage, but a third would add nothing.

Stripe - the genuinely different angle

Stripe does not compete on rails. It rides on top of Visa/MC. Its insight was that for online and SMB businesses, the hard part was never the network - it was the integration. Accepting a card historically meant a merchant account, a gateway, a processor, and weeks of paperwork. Stripe collapsed all of that into seven lines of code.

  • Developer-first: Stripe won engineers, not procurement departments. Clean APIs, great docs, instant onboarding. The developer became the buyer.
  • It abstracts the rails: a merchant integrates Stripe once and never thinks about issuers, acquirers, or networks again - Stripe orchestrates all of it underneath.
  • It owns the integration layer, which means it owns the SMB/online-business relationship. That is a different, softer moat than Visa's, but a real one: switching payment providers means re-plumbing your checkout.
  • It expands upward: billing, invoicing, fraud (Radar), issuing, treasury, lending - Stripe sells the whole financial stack to businesses, not just the swipe.
Two moats, two layers

Visa owns the rails (the network layer). Stripe owns the integration (the software layer on top). They are not really competitors - Stripe pays Visa/MC on nearly every transaction it processes. The interesting tension is who captures more of the merchant's fee over time: the toll road, or the software wrapping it.

6Unit economics (follow the fee on a 100 USD sale)

Here is where every slice goes on a typical 100.00 USD US credit-card purchase. The numbers are approximate and vary by card type and region, but the shape is the point:

SliceWho takes itApprox amount
InterchangeIssuing bank (buyer's bank)~1.50-2.00 (about 1.5-2.0%) - the biggest slice
Network fee (assessment)Visa / Mastercard~0.13 (about 0.13%) - tiny per swipe
Acquirer / processor markupAcquiring bank + processor~0.30-0.50
Merchant netsThe seller~97.50-98.00
  • Interchange is the fat part and it goes to the buyer's bank, not the network. This is why banks push rewards cards - they are funded by interchange.
  • The network take is measured in basis points (~13 bps). It looks trivial per transaction and is - the magic is entirely in the volume.
  • Volume x tiny-percentage = huge: 0.13% of 14 trillion USD is roughly 18 billion USD of pure-ish network revenue. The whole business is a rounding error charged a hundred billion times.
  • Stripe's blended price is about 2.9% + 30c per transaction. Out of that ~2.9%, most flows through to interchange + network + acquiring; Stripe keeps a spread of maybe 0.5-1.0% for the software, orchestration, and fraud tooling it layers on top.
Why the model is so profitable

Interchange is a percentage of an ever-growing pile of consumer spending; the network's cost to route the message does not grow with the dollar amount. A 10,000 USD purchase costs Visa the same to switch as a 10 USD one, but pays ~1,000x more in percentage fees. That decoupling of revenue (percent of value) from cost (per message) is the engine.

7How it is playing out

  • Embedded finance - every SaaS becomes a fintech. Shopify, Toast, and countless vertical-SaaS tools now bundle payments into their software. Because payments monetize at ~2-3% of volume, adding a payment layer can be more profitable than the software subscription itself. The refrain across the industry: "every software company will become a fintech."
  • Software eats the integration layer; networks keep the rails. Stripe, Adyen, and the SaaS platforms are steadily capturing the merchant relationship and the fee spread on top - but they still pay Visa/MC underneath. The rails endure; the layer above them consolidates.
  • The stablecoin threat to rails. This is the one thing Visa genuinely watches. If businesses can settle in USDC in seconds for a fraction of a cent, the card networks' cross-border and B2B premium erodes. Visa and Mastercard are hedging by building stablecoin settlement into their own networks - co-opting the threat rather than fighting it.
  • The SMB embedded-finance boom. Square, Stripe, and neobanks are wrapping payments, lending, payroll, and banking into one dashboard for small businesses that historically had none of it. The SMB relationship - long ignored by big banks - is the hottest battleground.
  • Real-time bank rails (FedNow/RTP) nibble at the edges for bill-pay and payroll, where speed matters more than card rewards - but consumer credit-card habits (rewards, protection, float) keep the card networks sticky.

8The pattern it exemplifies

This need showcases two of the most powerful business patterns on the board, stacked on top of each other:

Pattern 1: A toll on money movement + network effects

Sit in the middle of a transaction everyone must make, take a tiny percentage, and let a two-sided network effect (buyers and sellers pulling each other in) make you un-dislodgeable. The toll compounds automatically as the world transacts more. Visa and Mastercard are the textbook case; this is among the most durable moats that exists.

Pattern 2: Software wrapping the rails to own the SMB relationship

When the underlying utility (the rails) is a locked-up duopoly, the opportunity moves up a layer: wrap the rails in software so good that you own the customer relationship and capture the fee spread. Stripe, Square, and Adyen do this. The lesson generalizes far beyond payments - when you cannot own the utility, own the integration layer on top of it.

9Glossary

TermMeaning
Payment railsThe underlying networks that move money between banks (Visa, Mastercard, ACH, RTP, FedNow).
InterchangeThe fee paid by the merchant's side to the cardholder's issuing bank on each transaction - the largest slice of card fees (~1.5-2%).
Four-party modelThe card structure: cardholder, issuing bank, merchant, acquiring bank, with the network switching in the middle.
Network effectWhen each new user makes the product more valuable to every other user; two-sided when two groups (buyers/sellers) pull each other in.
Basis points (bps)Hundredths of a percent. 100 bps = 1%. Network take rates are quoted in single-digit-to-teens bps.
Embedded financeFinancial services (payments, lending, banking) built directly into non-financial software, so any SaaS becomes a fintech.
AcquirerThe merchant's bank/processor that connects the seller to the card networks and deposits their funds.
IssuerThe cardholder's bank that issues the card, approves transactions, fronts the money, and earns interchange.
SettlementThe actual transfer of funds between banks that reconciles a transaction, usually a day or more after authorization.
Take rateThe percentage of transaction volume a middleman keeps as its own revenue (Visa's is ~0.10-0.15%).
