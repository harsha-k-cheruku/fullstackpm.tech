The Need for Mobility

Move people and things from A to B - and the modern twist: access instead of ownership.

Need 10 of 13 · The needs framework · Captured 2026-07-11

0TL;DR

  • Mobility is ancient - freedom of movement and moving goods is a permanent need; the modern innovation is on-demand access replacing ownership.
  • #1 Uber wins as a two-sided local marketplace: it owns no cars, but owns the liquidity (riders + drivers) and the network effect - plus a super-app spanning rides, Eats, and freight.
  • #2 Tesla attacks the same need from the opposite direction: own the machine (vertical integration + software + autonomy), not rent the ride. Lyft is the rides-only US #2.
  • Autonomy is the wildcard - robotaxis (Waymo, Tesla) could remove the driver, flipping Uber's marketplace into an asset-heavy fleet game.
  • Same need, two mechanisms: access (marketplace) vs ownership (vertical integration).

1The need as an axiom

The desire to move - yourself, your family, your goods - is as old as legs. Freedom of movement underlies trade, work, escape, and opportunity; a person who cannot move is a person whose options collapse. For most of history the mechanism was ownership (a horse, a car) or public infrastructure (rail, bus). The 2010s introduced a genuinely new mechanism: mobility on demand - summon a ride in minutes without owning the asset.

The modern twist

The need never changed - "get me from A to B." What changed is the answer to "must I own the vehicle to satisfy it?" Smartphones + GPS + payments made access viable at scale, and that reframed a century-old ownership market into a marketplace.

2The demand flow for mobility

A ride travels the standard pipeline - but with a hard real-world logistics core in the middle:

NEED -> Trip trigger -> Request/Discovery (open app) -> Match to supply (nearest driver)
     -> Fulfillment (the physical ride) -> Payment (in-app) -> Retention (habit, price, ETA)
  • The scarce input is liquidity - enough drivers nearby that wait times are short, and enough riders that drivers stay busy. Thin liquidity = the whole thing fails.
  • Matching + routing is the hard technical middle - real-time supply/demand balancing, surge pricing, ETAs.
  • Whoever owns the demand relationship (the app the rider opens) controls the economics; drivers become interchangeable supply.

3#1 Uber - the two-sided marketplace

Uber's insight: you do not need to own cars to own mobility - you need to own the match. It is an aggregator of local transport supply.

  • Two-sided local network effects. More riders -> more driver earnings -> more drivers -> shorter waits + lower prices -> more riders. Density is the flywheel (same physics as DoorDash).
  • Asset-light. Drivers bring the capital (their cars); Uber provides the demand, the software, the trust (ratings), and the payment rail - and takes a cut.
  • The super-app. Rides, Uber Eats, and freight share drivers, users, and data - cross-subsidizing liquidity and raising switching costs. One app, many mobility jobs.
  • Global scale + brand. "Get an Uber" became a verb - the brand moat of the default.
The nuance from DoorDash's note applies here too

Mobility network effects are local, not global - liquidity in Chicago does nothing for a rider in Miami. Uber must win each city's density separately, which is why the moat is really "a thousand local moats" and why competitors (Lyft, DiDi) can survive regionally.

4#2 Tesla (own the machine) & Lyft (rides-only)

Tesla serves the identical need from the opposite pole: instead of renting you a ride, it sells you the vehicle - and bets the future on vertical integration + software.

  • Own the whole stack: the car, the battery, the software, the charging network, increasingly the insurance. Margin captured at every layer.
  • The car as a software platform: over-the-air updates, and Full Self-Driving (FSD) sold as high-margin software optionality on top of the hardware.
  • The autonomy bet: if self-driving works, Tesla could run its own robotaxi network - owning both the machine and the ride, collapsing Uber's marketplace advantage.

Lyft is the pure-play US #2 in ridesharing - the same marketplace model as Uber but rides-only, no Eats/freight, no global sprawl. It survives on being a credible alternative (driver/rider multi-homing keeps it alive) but lacks Uber's scale and super-app cross-subsidy.

5Other players / how it is evolving

  • Waymo (Alphabet) - the autonomy leader by safety record; a driverless robotaxi service already live in several cities. The clearest existence-proof that the driver can be removed.
  • DiDi, Grab, Ola - regional #1s (China, SE Asia, India) proving mobility is won market-by-market; Grab is itself a super-app (rides + food + payments).
  • Micromobility - bikes/scooters (Lime, Bird) for the short-trip slice.
  • The ownership-vs-access debate - most miles are still driven in owned cars; ridehail is a small (if high-profile) share of total mobility. Ownership is far from dead.
  • The robotaxi threat - autonomy is the industry's single biggest swing factor (see below).

(Numbers are approximate and directional.) Uber's model is the classic marketplace: brutal to bootstrap (chicken-and-egg liquidity), then leverages once dense. Tesla's is the classic hardware model: heavy capex, but with a software call-option (FSD) bolted on that could re-rate the whole business if it pays off.

7How it is playing out

  • Autonomy is the wildcard. If robotaxis scale, the "driver" - Uber's whole supply side - disappears. That could help Uber (cheaper rides, own the demand + fleet partnerships) or hurt it (whoever owns the autonomous fleet, e.g. Waymo/Tesla, owns the ride end-to-end and skips the marketplace). It flips the mechanism from asset-light to asset-heavy.
  • Consolidation + super-apps. Rides + food + payments bundle to deepen liquidity and switching costs (Uber, Grab).
  • EV transition reshapes the cost base of every mile.
  • Regulation - gig-worker classification (are drivers employees?) directly threatens the asset-light economics.
The mechanism-shift to watch

Ridehail replaced ownership with a marketplace. Autonomy could replace the marketplace with a fleet. The need ("A to B") is fixed; the winning mechanism has already changed once this decade and may change again. Hold the need constant and ask: what new tech serves it better next?

8The pattern it exemplifies

Mobility is the cleanest head-to-head of the two great ways to serve a physical need:

  • Access / marketplace (Uber): own the demand + the match, let others supply the asset, take a rake. Wins on liquidity + network effects, but the moats are local and the supply side is contestable.
  • Ownership / vertical integration (Tesla): own the machine and the software end-to-end, capture margin at every layer, bet on a technology (autonomy) to leap ahead. Wins on integration + software optionality, but is capital-heavy.
One line

The same need - move from A to B - is being fought over by an asset-light aggregator (own the match) and an asset-heavy integrator (own the machine), with autonomy as the technology that could decide which mechanism wins the next decade.

9Glossary

TermMeaning
Two-sided marketplaceA platform matching two groups (riders + drivers) and taking a cut.
LiquidityEnough supply + demand nearby that matches happen fast; the lifeblood of a local marketplace.
Take rateThe share of the fare the platform keeps after paying the driver.
Asset-lightOwning the demand/software but not the physical assets (cars).
Vertical integrationOwning multiple layers of the stack (car + software + charging + service).
Autonomy / FSDSelf-driving capability; removes the human driver from the loop.
RobotaxiAn autonomous vehicle offering rides with no driver.
Contribution marginRevenue per ride minus the direct costs of that ride.
Super-appOne app bundling many services (rides, food, payments) to deepen lock-in.
Multi-homingUsers/drivers using several apps at once, weakening any one platform's lock-in.
