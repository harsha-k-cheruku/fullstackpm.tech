Human Needs from First Principles

The axiom layer under every business: what humans permanently want, and how today's mechanisms arrange themselves to serve it.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/human-needs · Captured 2026-07-11 · Builds on ../psychology/ · Feeds the 13 need notes

0TL;DR

  • Needs are the irreducible layer; companies are the mechanisms that currently serve them best. Needs are stable for millennia; the mechanisms churn every generation.
  • Maslow's hierarchy is a useful map (physiological -> safety -> belonging -> esteem -> self-actualization) but is not strictly hierarchical and misses a few durable needs.
  • A refined list gives ~13 durable "jobs that never change" - from sustenance to knowledge to status to compute.
  • Every need is served through the same demand flow: need -> trigger -> discovery -> trust -> decision -> fulfillment -> payment -> retention.
  • Watching the #1 and #2 company per need shows how the whole board is playing out - and the same handful of patterns recur everywhere.

1Needs are axioms; companies are mechanisms

The cleanest way to understand "why does this company exist" is to invert it: a company is whatever currently serves a durable human need best. The need came first and will outlive the company. Amazon is not an axiom - "people want selection, convenience, and low prices" is the axiom, and Amazon is the current mechanism. Replace Amazon and the need remains.

The meta-principle, again

Needs (axioms) -> the best mechanism to serve them (companies). When technology changes, the need stays constant but a new mechanism can win. Study the need to understand why any business exists, and to see what is permanent versus what is temporary.

2Maslow's hierarchy + an honest critique

Maslow (1943) proposed five tiers, usually drawn as a pyramid:

  1. Physiological - food, water, warmth, rest, sex.
  2. Safety - security, health, shelter, money, stability.
  3. Belonging / love - relationships, connection, community.
  4. Esteem - status, recognition, respect, identity.
  5. Self-actualization - growth, mastery, meaning, creativity.
Where Maslow is wrong / incomplete

Not strictly hierarchical: people pursue belonging and esteem while still hungry; a starving artist chases meaning over safety. The tiers are simultaneous drives, not a strict ladder.

Missing needs: it under-weights knowledge/understanding (Maslow later added "cognitive" and "aesthetic" needs), convenience/time-saving (a meta-need modern commerce is built on), and entertainment/stimulation.

Culturally narrow: "self-actualization" at the top reflects an individualist lens; other cultures rank community/belonging higher.

Still: as a first-order map of durable demand, it is excellent - just treat it as overlapping drives, not a queue.

4The demand flow chain

Every need is satisfied by traveling the same pipeline. Understanding it explains who captures the value:

NEED -> Trigger -> Discovery/Search -> Evaluation/Trust -> Decision -> Fulfillment -> Payment -> Retention
  • Aggregators own Discovery + Trust (Google owns search/intent; Amazon owns evaluation via reviews).
  • The strongest businesses own the most steps end-to-end (Apple, Amazon), capturing margin at each.
  • Whoever owns the demand relationship (the top of the chain) forces suppliers below to commoditize - this is aggregation theory (see ../businesses/).
Why this matters for reading the board

For any need, ask: who owns Discovery? who owns Trust? who owns Fulfillment? The answers tell you who has power and why - and each need note traces exactly this.

6Why the mapping shifts (needs constant, mechanisms churn)

The need never moves; the winning mechanism does - whenever a new technology serves the same need better:

  • Knowledge: encyclopedias -> Google -> now answer-engines (OpenAI). Same need, three mechanisms in one lifetime.
  • Entertainment: broadcast TV -> cable -> Netflix -> TikTok. The need for stimulation is constant.
  • Connection: letters -> phone -> Facebook -> ephemeral/messaging. Belonging never changed.
  • Money: cash -> cards -> digital rails -> embedded finance. Financial security is ancient.
The reader's takeaway

To predict the future of any industry, hold the need fixed and ask: what new technology now serves it better? That is where the next mechanism (and the next #1) comes from. This is the whole game of watching the board.
