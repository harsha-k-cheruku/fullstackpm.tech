Need: Compute

The enabling meta-need beneath all modern services: every other need is now served through software, and software runs on compute.

Need note 13 of 13 · Captured 2026-07-11 · See The needs framework · Ties to ../ai/ (the Bitter Lesson) and ../economics-of-ai/

0TL;DR

  • Compute is not a Maslow need directly - it is the enabling meta-need. Nobody wakes up craving FLOPs; they crave food, connection, money, answers. But every modern mechanism serving those needs now runs on compute.
  • It is a layered stack: chips -> servers/datacenters -> cloud -> platforms -> apps. Each layer has its own #1, and compute flows UP the stack to serve every other need.
  • Two different #1s in the same stack: Nvidia is #1 at the chip/accelerator layer (~90%+ of AI training silicon, locked in by CUDA); AWS is #1 at the cloud/utility layer (~30% of a ~$300B/yr cloud market).
  • The real bottleneck sits under Nvidia: TSMC fabricates nearly all leading-edge chips, and ASML is the sole maker of the EUV lithography machines TSMC needs. Monopoly stacked on monopoly.
  • AI turned compute into the raw fuel of intelligence. The Bitter Lesson (../ai/) says general methods that scale with compute win - so demand for compute is now demand for capability itself.
  • The economics are picks-and-shovels + toll road: Nvidia runs ~70%+ gross margin selling the shovels; clouds rent the shovels by the hour; the new binding constraint is increasingly power/energy, not chips.

1The need as an axiom (an enabling meta-need)

Every other note in this series states a need that a caveman would recognize: eat, shelter, connect, be seen, understand, move, earn. Compute is different. No human has a direct, innate need for compute. You cannot eat a GPU. So why does it belong on the list of durable needs?

Because it is the substrate. In 2026, almost every mechanism that serves a real need is software running on compute:

  • Food delivery (DoorDash) is a dispatch and matching problem solved by servers.
  • Search / knowledge (Google) is an index and ranking system running across millions of machines.
  • Connection (Meta) is a social graph and a recommendation model served from datacenters.
  • Money (Visa) is a network authorizing ~65,000+ transactions per second on redundant compute.

So compute is an enabling meta-need - like electricity a century ago. Nobody wants volts; they want light, refrigeration, and machines. Nobody wants FLOPs; they want the services compute makes possible. When a need becomes universally served through software, the substrate that software runs on becomes a need of its own - a derived, second-order need that nonetheless everyone depends on.

Why AI makes this the defining need of the era

For decades, compute was infrastructure - important but boring plumbing. AI changed that. The Bitter Lesson (see ../ai/) is the observation that, over 70 years of AI research, general methods that simply scale with more compute have consistently beaten clever hand-crafted methods. Learning and search beat human-encoded knowledge, every time, once enough compute arrives.

The consequence: compute became the raw fuel of intelligence. More compute -> bigger models -> more capability. That converts compute from a cost center into the primary input of the most valuable product of the decade. Demand for compute is now, quite literally, demand for capability. See ../economics-of-ai/ for how that reshapes the cost structure of every software business.

That is why compute earns a seat at the bottom of the needs table: it is the enabling layer under needs 1 through 12, and AI has made it the scarcest, most contested resource on the board.

2The demand flow (the stack)

Unlike consumer needs, compute is not bought via a discovery-and-trust funnel. It is consumed as a layered utility stack. Each layer buys from the one below and sells to the one above, and value (and margin) is captured at each rung:

PHYSICS -> Fab -> Chips -> Servers/Datacenter -> Cloud -> Platforms -> Apps -> NEED

silicon wafers   the accelerators   racks + power + cooling   rent by the hour   models/DBs/APIs   the service you use

The key structural insight

Compute flows up the stack. A wafer becomes a chip, a chip becomes a server, servers become cloud capacity, cloud runs a model, the model powers an app, the app serves a hungry / lonely / curious human. The dollar the consumer spends on any modern service is partly a payment, passed down the stack, for compute. That is what makes it a meta-need: it sits under all the others.

3#1: Nvidia AND AWS (two layers, both #1)

Compute has no single #1 because it is a stack, not a market. The two most powerful positions sit at different layers, and both are dominant in their own layer. It is a common error to compare them directly - they are not competitors, they are neighbors on the ladder.

Nvidia - the chip / accelerator layer

Nvidia designs the GPUs (accelerators) that do the massively parallel math AI training and inference require. It holds an estimated ~90%+ share of AI training silicon. In its data-center segment it went from roughly $15B/yr (FY2023) to a run-rate above $100B/yr by 2025 - one of the fastest revenue expansions in corporate history, briefly making it the most valuable company on Earth (~$3T+ market cap).

The moat is not just the chip - it is CUDA, Nvidia's software layer. Nearly every AI framework (PyTorch, TensorFlow) is optimized for CUDA. Fifteen-plus years of libraries, tooling, and developer muscle memory are built on it. A competitor can match the raw silicon and still lose, because the software ecosystem is on Nvidia. This is a classic lock-in via the complements: they do not just sell hardware, they own the platform the industry writes against.

AWS - the cloud / utility layer

Amazon Web Services invented the modern cloud (2006) and remains #1 with ~30% share of a global cloud infrastructure market worth roughly $300B+/yr and growing ~20% annually. AWS turns capital-heavy compute into a metered utility: instead of buying servers, you rent compute on demand by the second, and give it back when done.

That is the electricity analogy made real - AWS is the power company of compute. Its moat is scale (it can amortize datacenters across millions of customers), switching costs (your data, your architecture, your egress fees all live there), and breadth (200+ services). AWS is also Amazon's profit engine: it is a minority of revenue but the majority of Amazon's operating income.

Why both are #1 - and why that is the whole point

Nvidia sells you the engine. AWS rents you the car by the hour. You can buy Nvidia chips and run them yourself, or rent Nvidia chips inside AWS - in fact AWS is one of Nvidia's largest customers. They are stacked, not opposed. The compute need is served by a chain of monopolists and oligopolists, each dominant at its own rung. Reading this note means learning to see the layer, not just the logo.

4#2: AMD, Azure/GCP, and TSMC (the bottleneck)

Each layer has its own challenger - and one player under Nvidia is arguably more of a chokepoint than Nvidia itself.

AMD - the chip challenger

AMD is the credible #2 in accelerators (the MI300/MI350 line) and already beat Intel in CPUs on merit. Its problem is not silicon - the raw specs are competitive - it is CUDA. AMD's answer is ROCm, an open software stack, but it lags years behind in maturity and adoption. AMD's data-center AI revenue is measured in single-digit billions versus Nvidia's $100B+. The lesson: in compute, the software moat outlasts the hardware lead.

Azure and GCP - the cloud challengers

Microsoft Azure (~24% share) is the strong #2 in cloud, boosted enormously by its OpenAI partnership - it became the default place to rent frontier-model compute. Google Cloud (~12%) is #3, differentiated by its own TPU silicon and deep data/AI tooling. Together AWS + Azure + GCP are the cloud oligopoly, controlling roughly two-thirds of global cloud infrastructure. This is the classic shape of a capital-intensive utility: a handful of giants, because only they can afford the datacenters.

TSMC - the true bottleneck under Nvidia

Here is the twist that most people miss. Nvidia does not manufacture its own chips - it is fabless. It designs; someone else fabricates. That someone is almost always TSMC (Taiwan Semiconductor), which produces an estimated ~90% of the world's leading-edge chips (the most advanced process nodes). Nvidia, AMD, Apple, and Google all queue up for TSMC's capacity.

The chokepoint hiding under the famous name

If Nvidia is the picks-and-shovels of the AI gold rush, TSMC is the only mine that produces the steel. When people say Nvidia is the bottleneck, they are one layer too high. The real physical constraint is leading-edge fab capacity, and TSMC has a near-monopoly on it - concentrated on one island, which is also why it is one of the most geopolitically sensitive companies on Earth. The lower the layer, the harder it is to replace, and the more the real power tends to sit.

5Other players and how the stack is evolving

  • Hyperscalers building their own silicon. The biggest cloud buyers hate paying Nvidia's ~70% margin, so they are designing their own chips. Google has TPUs (custom AI accelerators, now several generations deep). Amazon has Trainium (training) and Inferentia (inference), plus Graviton ARM CPUs for general workloads. Microsoft has Maia. The strategy: in-house silicon for your own huge internal demand, to escape the Nvidia toll. It is vertical integration aimed straight at the highest-margin layer.
  • ASML - the monopoly beneath TSMC. Go one layer lower than the fab. To etch leading-edge chips you need Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) lithography machines, and ASML (Netherlands) is the only company on Earth that makes them. Each machine costs ~$150M-$400M. So the stack of chokepoints reads: apps depend on cloud, cloud on Nvidia, Nvidia on TSMC, TSMC on ASML. A monopoly (ASML) under a near-monopoly (TSMC) under a near-monopoly (Nvidia).
  • The training-vs-inference split. Two very different compute workloads. Training is building the model - a huge, one-time, capital-intensive burst on the biggest clusters. Inference is running the trained model to answer each user query - smaller per call, but happening billions of times a day, forever. Training is a spike; inference is a river. As AI products scale to real usage, inference is becoming the larger and faster-growing slice of total compute demand.
  • Power / energy as the new constraint. The binding limit is shifting from chips to electricity. A single large AI datacenter can draw hundreds of megawatts - the load of a small city. Hyperscalers are now signing deals for dedicated power, including nuclear (SMRs and restarted plants). The sentence you increasingly hear: "we have the chips, we cannot get the power." Energy is becoming the layer under the layer.

6Unit economics

The economics differ sharply by layer - and that difference is the whole story of who captures value.

Layer / playerGross margin (approx)Shape of the business

ASML (litho)~50%Tiny unit volume, enormous price per machine, monopoly pricing.

App / model layerHighly variableWhere inference cost eats the margin (see ../economics-of-ai/).
Capex vs opex - the split that matters

The AI compute buildout is capex-heavy: hyperscalers are collectively spending on the order of $200B-$300B+/yr on datacenters and chips. Two distinct spend shapes drive it (from ../economics-of-ai/):

Training = capex. A large frontier model can cost tens to hundreds of millions of dollars in compute to train - a big, lumpy, up-front bet. Inference = opex. Every query a user makes has a marginal compute cost that recurs forever. This flips the classic software model: traditional software had ~zero marginal cost per user, but AI software pays real compute per request. That single fact reshapes the margin structure of the entire application layer.

The falling cost curve. Counterbalancing it all: the cost per unit of compute (and per token of inference) has been falling fast - often several-fold per year - as chips improve, models get more efficient, and competition bites. Cheaper compute expands demand rather than shrinking spend (Jevons paradox): as it gets cheaper, we use dramatically more of it.

7How it is playing out

  • The AI compute buildout. The defining capex event of the decade. Datacenter construction, chip orders, and power contracts are all racing. Nvidia's order book stretches out quarters in advance; whole GPU allocations are spoken for before they are made.
  • In-housing silicon. Every hyperscaler is designing chips (TPU, Trainium, Graviton, Maia) to reduce dependence on Nvidia's margin. Expect the accelerator layer to slowly fragment - Nvidia stays dominant on frontier training, but custom silicon eats the more predictable internal and inference workloads.
  • The cloud oligopoly holds. AWS / Azure / GCP keep ~two-thirds of the market. Barriers to entry (datacenters, chips, power, and now regulatory scale) are too high for new entrants; the fight is share-shifting among the three, driven largely by who has the best AI story.
  • Energy becomes the bottleneck. The constraint migrates from chips to megawatts. Power availability, grid interconnect queues, and long-term energy deals (including nuclear) are now first-order strategic questions for compute providers.
  • Inference demand explodes. As AI moves from demos to embedded features in billions of daily interactions, inference volume - the river, not the spike - becomes the dominant driver of sustained compute demand, and the layer where cost discipline matters most.
The reader's takeaway

Hold the need fixed - "every service runs on software, and software runs on compute" - and the whole board resolves into a stack of chokepoints. To find where power sits, keep asking: who is the supplier one layer down that everyone else cannot replace? Follow that question down and you arrive at TSMC, then ASML, then physics and power.

8The pattern it exemplifies

Compute is the cleanest example of three overlapping patterns at once:

  • Picks-and-shovels. In a gold rush, sell shovels. In the AI gold rush, Nvidia sells the shovels (chips) and TSMC forges the steel. You do not need to know which AI app wins to profit from all of them needing compute.
  • Toll road. The cloud is a toll booth on compute - rent by the hour, take a cut of everything that runs. Every app in needs 1-12 pays the toll, passed up from the consumer's dollar.
  • The layered stack where each layer has its own #1. Unlike most needs (one dominant mechanism), compute is served by a ladder of dominant players - Nvidia (chips), AWS (cloud), TSMC (fab), ASML (litho) - each near-monopolistic at its own rung. The general rule: the lower the layer, the harder to displace, and often the more concentrated the power.
The one-line version

Compute is the meta-need everyone else depends on: the enabling substrate under all twelve real needs, structured as a stack of chokepoints, and - since the Bitter Lesson - the raw fuel of intelligence itself. Own a layer nobody can route around, and you tax the entire modern economy.

9Glossary

TermMeaning
ComputeRaw processing capacity - the ability to run calculations. Measured in FLOPs (floating-point operations per second) and, in practice, in chips, servers, and megawatts.
GPU / acceleratorA chip built for massively parallel math. Originally for graphics, now the workhorse of AI training and inference. Nvidia dominates this layer.
CUDANvidia's proprietary software platform for programming its GPUs. The ecosystem lock-in (not the chip alone) is Nvidia's deepest moat.
Cloud / utility computingRenting compute on demand by the second instead of owning servers. Turns capex into metered opex; the electricity model applied to computers.
HyperscalerA giant operator of global-scale datacenters (AWS, Azure, GCP, plus Meta). Big enough to design their own chips and buy their own power.
Fab (foundry)A semiconductor fabrication plant that turns chip designs into physical silicon. TSMC is the dominant leading-edge fab; Nvidia is fabless and depends on it.
FablessA chip company that designs but does not manufacture, outsourcing fabrication to a foundry (e.g. Nvidia, AMD, Apple).
Training vs inferenceTraining = building the model (huge, one-time, capex spike). Inference = running the model per query (small each, but billions of times, forever - opex river).
Capex / opexCapital expenditure = big up-front asset spend (datacenters, chips, training runs). Operating expenditure = ongoing running cost (inference per query, power).
ASIC / TPUASIC = Application-Specific Integrated Circuit, a chip custom-built for one job. Google's TPU, Amazon's Trainium/Inferentia are AI ASICs made to escape Nvidia's margin.
EUV lithographyExtreme Ultraviolet lithography - the technique for printing leading-edge chip features. ASML is the sole maker of EUV machines, the monopoly beneath TSMC.
