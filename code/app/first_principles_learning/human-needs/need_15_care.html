Need: Care

Childcare, eldercare, mental health, caregiving - the least aggregated, most human, most trust-and-labor-intensive need on the whole board.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/human-needs · Need 15 · See The needs framework · Ties to ../future-of-work/ human-premium invariants

0TL;DR

  • Care is a permanent, universal need - caring for the young, the old, the sick, and the struggling is as old as the species and never goes away. But it is labor-intensive, high-trust, high-liability, and emotional, which makes it resist both automation and aggregation.
  • It is the archetypal fragmented, still-offline market: a ~$600B+ US spend (childcare ~$60B, home/eldercare ~$150B+, nursing/senior facilities ~$250B+, mental health ~$100B+) with no dominant aggregator. That absence is the whole point.
  • The hard step is finding and trusting a caregiver. Care.com aggregated the discovery step, Bright Horizons sells employer-sponsored centers, and BetterHelp/Teladoc + Headspace/Calm scaled the scarce therapist digitally.
  • Digital mental health scaled fastest because it is the least physical. Childcare and eldercare stay stubbornly human, local, and offline - you cannot ship a hug or a diaper change over an API.
  • AI is arriving as a supplement (triage, admin, scheduling, companionship apps), not a replacement - the human-premium invariants (trust, physical presence, accountability, empathy) still bind.
  • This is the ANTI-pattern to the attention and marketplace giants: a need where labor is the product, margins are thin, and software economics mostly do not apply.

1The need as an axiom

Care sits across three Maslow tiers at once - physiological (a baby must be fed, an elder must be bathed), safety (the vulnerable must be kept from harm), and belonging (to be cared for is to be loved and attended to). It is not a luxury add-on; it is the substrate that keeps dependent humans alive and functioning.

The axiom is simple and unbreakable: every human is, at both ends of life and often in the middle, dependent on another human's care. Infants cannot self-feed. The frail elderly cannot self-transfer from bed to chair. The person in a mental-health crisis cannot self-regulate. Someone has to show up.

The defining property of this need

Care is labor-intensive, high-trust, high-liability, and emotional. Those four properties are exactly the ones that resist automation and aggregation. You are handing another person your child, your parent, or your mind - the stakes make trust non-negotiable and physical presence usually mandatory. That is why care is the LEAST software-like need on the board.

Contrast it with knowledge (answers can be copied infinitely at zero marginal cost) or entertainment (one film serves a billion people). Care does not copy. One caregiver serves a handful of people at a time, in person, over long relationships. The unit of production is a human hour, and human hours do not scale like bits.

2The demand flow

Care follows the same pipeline as every need, but the weight sits heavily on two steps that software normally commoditizes: trust and physical presence.

NEED -> Trigger (a baby, an aging parent, a crisis)
     -> Find a trusted caregiver  (the HARD step)
     -> Vet / trust  (background check, references, chemistry)
     -> Ongoing relationship / fulfillment  (in person, repeated)
     -> Payment  (out of pocket, or insurance for health/therapy)
     -> Retention  (a good caregiver is kept for years)
  • The trigger is a life event, not a browsing impulse: a birth, a diagnosis, a parent's fall, a mental-health emergency. Demand is inelastic and urgent when it hits.
  • Discovery is the bottleneck. Unlike buying a product, you cannot judge a caregiver from a listing - you need references, a background check, and a trial period. This is where aggregators try to add value, and where they mostly fail to fully own the relationship.
  • Fulfillment is physical and repeated - the same person, in the home or center, day after day. There is no drop-ship equivalent.
  • Payment is fragmented: childcare and non-medical eldercare are largely out of pocket; therapy and skilled nursing route partly through insurance and Medicare/Medicaid.
  • Retention is naturally sticky but relationship-bound - families keep a trusted nanny or aide for years, but the loyalty is to the human, not the platform.
Why the platform rarely owns the relationship

In most needs, whoever owns discovery + trust owns the customer. In care, the trust transfers to the individual caregiver, not the marketplace. Families meet a nanny on Care.com and then pay her directly off-platform for years. This disintermediation is structural, and it is why no care aggregator captures the value that a Google or an Amazon captures in its category.

3#1 the fragmented incumbents - how they (partly) win

There is no single #1 in care the way Walmart is #1 in groceries. Instead there are category leaders, each of which wins by solving one slice of the demand flow - usually the discovery step.

Care.com - aggregating the discovery step

Care.com is a two-sided marketplace matching families to caregivers (nannies, babysitters, senior caregivers, pet care, housekeeping). It has tens of millions of registered members and is the closest thing to a national aggregator. It solves find a caregiver by putting supply and demand in one searchable place, adding background checks and reviews to help the vetting step.

Its business model is subscription (families and/or caregivers pay a monthly fee for access) plus payment processing - notably not a large take rate on the ongoing labor, precisely because that labor moves off-platform. IAC acquired Care.com in 2020 for about $500M. Revenue is a few hundred million dollars per year - respectable, but a rounding error next to a marketplace giant like Airbnb or DoorDash, despite care being a far larger underlying market. That gap is the fragmentation tax.

Bright Horizons - employer-sponsored childcare centers

Bright Horizons operates ~1,000+ physical childcare centers, mostly as an employee benefit sold to large employers (it runs the on-site or near-site daycare for big companies and hospitals). Revenue is roughly $2.5B/year. It wins by anchoring demand through the employer (stable, pre-paid enrollment) and by owning the physical fulfillment - the actual building, staff, and licensing. This is care done as real estate + labor + B2B contracts, and it is capital- and labor-heavy by nature.

BetterHelp / Teladoc and Headspace / Calm - scaling the scarce therapist

Mental health is the one sub-category where software economics partly work, because talk therapy and wellness content are the least physical form of care. BetterHelp (owned by Teladoc) is the largest teletherapy platform, matching clients to licensed therapists over video/chat, with revenue around $1B/year. It scales the scarce therapist by removing geography and the commute.

Headspace and Calm go further: they turn wellness into pure software - meditation and mental-fitness content that serves millions at near-zero marginal cost, funded by consumer subscriptions and increasingly by employer/health-plan contracts. Calm and Headspace each reached valuations around $2B in their peak. This is the exception that scales - and it scales precisely because it stops being one-to-one human care and becomes one-to-many content.

The pattern inside the pattern

Every care leader wins the ONE step that can be partly digitized (discovery for Care.com, the employer relationship for Bright Horizons, delivery-over-video for BetterHelp, content for Calm) and leaves the irreducibly human part - the in-person hours, the trust, the liability - to individual humans. Nobody has aggregated the whole flow, because the core of it cannot be aggregated.

The absence IS the finding

In groceries there is Walmart. In search there is Google. In care there is... a phone book. The market is enormous (>$600B in the US alone) yet the largest pure-play, Care.com, touches only a sliver of the dollars. No company owns the demand relationship end-to-end, because trust and physical presence keep leaking out of every platform back to the individual human. Care is the clearest living proof that not every large market can be aggregated.

5Other players / evolution

Several forces are reshaping care - but none of them dissolve its core human property.

  • The caregiver shortage. Demand for direct-care workers is projected to grow faster than almost any other US occupation, while turnover in home care and childcare runs high (often 40-60% annually) because the work is hard and the pay is low. Supply, not demand, is the binding constraint.
  • Aging demographics - a decades-long tailwind. The US 65+ population is heading past 80 million by 2040, and the 85+ cohort (the heaviest care users) is the fastest-growing age group globally. Every developed country faces the same curve. Demand for eldercare is going straight up for the next 30 years.
  • Employer benefits as a channel. Childcare, eldercare navigation, and mental-health benefits are increasingly bought by employers (Bright Horizons, Care.com's enterprise arm, Lyra, Spring Health, Modern Health). The employer becomes the aggregator of demand even when it cannot aggregate supply.
  • AI as a SUPPLEMENT, not a replacement. AI is landing on the parts of care that are information work, not human work: triage and symptom-checking, scheduling and matching, care-plan documentation, insurance paperwork, and companionship/check-in apps for isolated seniors. It reduces the admin drag on human caregivers and extends their reach - but it does not lift the elder into the bath or hold the crying child. The human hour remains the product.
  • Regulation and liability. Licensing (staff-to-child ratios, nursing-home standards), background-check mandates, and malpractice/negligence liability all raise the cost of scaling and entrench local, accountable operators. Regulation is a moat for incumbents and a barrier to any would-be aggregator.

6Unit economics

The economics of care are the mirror image of software - and that single fact explains almost everything about the market's shape.

DimensionCare (labor)Software (bits)
Marginal cost of one more customerAnother human hour - roughly constant, does not fallNear zero
Gross marginThin - often 10-30% for agencies/facilities after wages70-90%
Cost driverWages (60-80% of revenue)R&D, then copies for free
ScalingLinear - 2x customers needs ~2x staffExponential - 2x customers, ~same cost
MoatTrust, licensing, local densityNetwork effects, data, switching cost

Labor is the cost, and labor does not scale like software. A daycare with twice the children needs roughly twice the staff and twice the licensed square footage. A home-care agency's revenue is capped by how many aide-hours it can staff. There is no operating leverage; margins stay thin and stubborn.

Marketplace take rates are low. Care.com cannot charge a 20% take on a nanny's wage the way DoorDash takes ~25-30% of a food order, because the caregiver and family simply transact directly after the first meeting. So platforms retreat to subscription fees for access - a much smaller slice of a much bigger pie.

Why care resists the software-margin playbook

The venture playbook - build a platform, aggregate a fragmented market, take a cut of every transaction, ride 80% gross margins to a monopoly - keeps failing in physical care. The transaction is a human relationship that walks off the platform, the margins are wage-bound, and liability punishes recklessness. Investors have burned billions trying to "Uber for X" the caregiving world and mostly learned that care is not Uberizable.

The exception: mental-health apps. Headspace and Calm DO get software margins, because they turned care into one-to-many content. Even teletherapy (BetterHelp) is only partly software - the therapist hour is still one-to-one and wage-bound, so its margins sit between pure content and pure labor. The rule holds: the more physical and one-to-one the care, the less it scales.

7How it is playing out

Watching the board, a clear ordering emerges - sorted by how physical the care is.

  • Digital mental health scaled fastest because it is the least physical. Video therapy, then app-based meditation, then employer mental-health platforms (Lyra, Spring Health) all grew quickly - especially post-2020 when remote care normalized. This is where the software money went, and it went there because it could.
  • Childcare and eldercare remain stubbornly human and offline. Despite decades of startups, most children are still cared for by a local center, a family member, or a nanny found by word of mouth; most elders are cared for at home by family or an agency aide, or in a local facility. The internet added a discovery layer on top but did not change the physical core.
  • Demographics keep driving demand up while the caregiver shortage keeps supply tight - so prices rise (US childcare and senior-care costs have outpaced general inflation for years) without any single winner consolidating the market.
  • Employers and health plans are quietly becoming the aggregators of demand (buying benefits in bulk) even as supply stays hopelessly fragmented. That is the most likely shape of any future consolidation: aggregate the payer, not the caregiver.

8The pattern it exemplifies

Care is the ANTI-pattern of this whole collection. Most need notes show a need being aggregated and automated into the hands of one or two giants. Care shows the opposite: a need where the forces of aggregation and automation break against human bedrock.

The human-premium invariants (see ../future-of-work/)

Four properties make work resistant to automation and aggregation. Care has all four at maximum strength:

Trust - you are handing over your child, parent, or mind; the trust cost is enormous and transfers to the individual, not the platform.

Physical presence - most care must happen in a body, in a place, in real time. Bits cannot bathe an elder.

Accountability - liability and licensing demand a named, responsible human. You cannot sue an algorithm for negligence the way you can a licensed facility.

Empathy - the emotional labor of comforting, reassuring, and attending is the actual product, and it is the hardest thing to fake or automate.

Where the attention giants (Meta, Google, TikTok) win by scaling one-to-many and the marketplace giants (Amazon, Uber, DoorDash) win by aggregating supply and taking a cut, care does neither well. It is the clearest, most concrete example of the human-premium: the more a job requires trust, presence, accountability, and empathy, the more it stays human, local, fragmented, and offline - and the less any software playbook can capture it.

The reader's takeaway

When you find a huge market with no dominant aggregator, ask: how much of it is trust, physical presence, accountability, and empathy? The higher that fraction, the more the market will stay fragmented, and the more AI will show up as a supplement rather than a replacement. Care is the limit case - and therefore the best lens for understanding what stays human as everything else gets automated.

9Glossary

TermMeaning
Caregiver marketplaceA two-sided platform matching families to caregivers (e.g. Care.com). Aggregates discovery but rarely the ongoing paid relationship.
Human-premiumThe extra value of work that must be done by a human because of trust, physical presence, accountability, or empathy. Care is the archetype.
Labor-intensiveA service whose main cost is human hours (60-80% of revenue in care), so it does not gain operating leverage as it grows.
FragmentationA market split among many small local providers with no dominant player. Care is one of the largest fragmented markets that exists.
Trust costThe high bar of vetting required before handing over a dependent person. It is what makes discovery the hard step and keeps loyalty on the individual.
Aggregation resistanceThe tendency of a market to defy platform consolidation because value keeps leaking back to individual providers (disintermediation).
DisintermediationWhen buyer and seller meet on a platform, then transact directly off it - the structural leak that starves care marketplaces.
TeletherapyTalk therapy delivered over video/chat (BetterHelp, Talkspace). The most scalable form of one-to-one care because it removes geography.
LiabilityLegal responsibility for harm to the person being cared for. It mandates named, accountable, licensed humans and blocks pure automation.
AI-as-supplementUsing AI for the information work around care (triage, scheduling, admin, companionship) while humans keep doing the physical, high-trust core.
