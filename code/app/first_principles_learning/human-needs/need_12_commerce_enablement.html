Commerce Enablement

The merchant side of commerce: the durable human need to sell, earn, and provide for oneself and one's family - and the mechanisms that arm or own the seller today.

Need 12 of 13 · A meta-need (safety/provision + self-actualization) · See The needs framework · #1 Amazon vs #2 Shopify

0TL;DR

  • The axiom is old as markets themselves: people need to make a living by selling goods and services. From the market stall to the online storefront, the need to reach buyers and get paid never changes - only the mechanism does.
  • The whole game is one question: who owns the customer relationship? That single tension splits the industry into aggregators that own the demand and enablers that arm the seller.
  • #1 Amazon owns the demand. ~600M+ US visitors/month and ~180M+ Prime members mean sellers rent access via the 3P marketplace + FBA + ads. Huge reach, but you do not own the customer and you help fund your own competitor.
  • #2 Shopify arms the rebels. It gives merchants their own storefront + checkout + tools; the merchant owns the customer. Shopify monetizes payments + subscription, not the demand - the anti-aggregator.
  • The economics are brutal and opposite. On Amazon the blended take (referral + FBA + ads) can reach ~40-50% of a 3P seller's revenue. On Shopify a merchant pays a subscription + ~2.9% on payments - but must buy their own traffic.
  • Harsha's world: Walmart Marketplace + WFS (fulfillment) + Walmart Connect (retail media) is building the Amazon playbook as a genuine #3, and the seller-enablement/analytics layer on top is still fragmented and wide open.

1The need as an axiom

Under all of commerce there are two humans, not one. The consumer needs to buy (sustenance, shelter, entertainment - needs 1 through 11). But sitting across the table is the merchant who needs to sell, earn, and provide. That is the axiom of Need 12 - and it is genuinely a meta-need, because earning a living is what lets a person satisfy every other need in the stack.

On Maslow it lands in two places at once. It is a safety/provision need (income = food on the table, shelter, security for the family) and a self-actualization need (building a business, being your own boss, making a thing the world pays for). Both drivers are ancient and universal.

The axiom, stated plainly

People have always needed to make a living by selling goods or services to other people. The market stall, the shop on the high street, the mail-order catalog, the eBay listing, the Shopify storefront, the Amazon 3P SKU, the TikTok Shop clip - all the same axiom wearing a new mechanism. Whoever most cheaply connects a seller to a paying buyer wins the era.

The through-line of the mechanisms is a steady drop in the cost of reaching a buyer. A physical stall reached whoever walked past. A catalog reached whoever you could mail. A marketplace listing reaches a global audience for near-zero marginal cost - but the marketplace, not the seller, now owns the walk-past traffic. That inversion (the platform owning the footfall) is the central fact of modern commerce enablement.

2The demand flow (from the seller's view)

The consumer notes trace the buyer's journey. Here we flip it and trace the seller's pipeline. The step where a seller is weakest is the step someone else charges rent on:

NEED TO SELL -> Find demand (customers) -> List / build a store -> Fulfill (ship/deliver) -> Get paid -> Grow (repeat + retain)
  • Find demand. The hardest, most expensive step. A seller either rents an existing audience (Amazon, Walmart, Etsy, TikTok) or buys traffic to their own store (Google/Meta ads driving to Shopify). This is where power concentrates.
  • List / build a store. Cataloging, pricing, merchandising. On a marketplace this is a listing inside someone else's mall; on Shopify it is your own shop that you control end to end.
  • Fulfill. Warehousing, picking, packing, shipping, returns. Increasingly outsourced to the platform (FBA, WFS) - which deepens lock-in and improves the buyer promise (fast, free delivery).
  • Get paid. Payment processing, payout timing, fraud, chargebacks. A quiet but enormous profit pool (this is Shopify's real business, and Stripe's whole business).
  • Grow. Reviews, repeat purchase, email/SMS, ads. Requires knowing your customer - which you only do if you own the customer relationship.
The one tension that explains everything

Who owns the customer relationship? On a marketplace, the platform owns the buyer - it has the email, the payment method, the purchase history, the trust. The seller is a faceless SKU. On an independent storefront, the merchant owns all of that. Every strategic move in this industry is a fight over that ownership - because whoever owns the customer captures the durable margin and sets the take rate.

3#1 Amazon - how it wins for sellers (own the demand)

Amazon is #1 not because it is a nice place to be a seller, but because it owns the demand at a scale no one can match. ~600M+ US visits/month, ~180M+ US Prime members trained to start product searches on Amazon (not Google), and a trust/logistics flywheel (reviews + fast free shipping) that converts browsers into buyers. If you have a product and you want customers today, Amazon is the single largest pool of high-intent buyers on the planet.

Third-party (3P) is now the majority of the store. Roughly 60%+ of units sold on Amazon are from 3P sellers, not Amazon's own retail. Amazon flipped from being a store to being a mall that rents stalls - and the rent is where the money is.

What a seller rents (and pays for)

  • 3P Marketplace access - the right to list in front of that demand, for a referral fee (take rate) of typically ~8-15% of each sale (~15% in most categories).
  • FBA (Fulfilled by Amazon) - ship your inventory to Amazon; they store, pick, pack, ship, and handle returns and customer service. This is what earns the Prime badge, which is what drives conversion. Costs storage + per-unit fulfillment fees.
  • Ads (Sponsored Products / Brands) - because the search results are so crowded, being findable increasingly requires paying. Amazon's ads business is now a ~$50B+/year machine, and it is almost pure margin extracted from sellers.
The Amazon tradeoff (read this twice)

Massive reach, but you do not own the customer. Amazon keeps the buyer's email, identity, and purchase data. You cannot easily re-market to them; they are Amazon's customer who happened to buy your product.

You fund your own competitor. Amazon sees exactly which of your SKUs sell, then can source/launch a competing private-label item or steer the Buy Box. Your success is Amazon's market research, paid for by you.

The take creeps up. Referral fee + FBA + ads compound. A product that looks profitable at a 15% referral fee can be underwater once ads are the price of visibility (see section 6).

4#2 Shopify - the different angle (arm the rebels)

Shopify is the #2 by taking the exact opposite bet. Instead of owning the demand and renting it out, it arms the seller with everything needed to run their own store: themes, checkout, inventory, payments, shipping labels, apps, POS for in-person. The merchant points their own domain at it, drives their own traffic, and - critically - owns the customer relationship outright. Ben Thompson's phrase for this is the anti-aggregator: Shopify arms the rebels against the Amazon empire.

Shopify powers on the order of a few million merchants and something like ~10% of US e-commerce GMV - collectively a serious rival to Amazon, but as a fragmented federation of independent stores rather than one mall.

Why the model is different at its core

  • Shopify does not sell you demand. It gives you the shop; you must bring the shoppers (via Meta/Google/TikTok ads, SEO, email, brand). That is the price of owning your customer.
  • It monetizes payments + subscription, not the audience. Merchants pay a monthly plan ($39 to $2000+/mo) plus - the big one - Shopify Payments at roughly ~2.9% + 30c per transaction. Payments is now the majority of Shopify's revenue.
  • Merchant Solutions > Subscriptions. The take-rate on GMV (payments, capital, shipping, markets) has grown to be far larger than the subscription line - Shopify makes more when its merchants make more, which aligns incentives.
  • The merchant owns everything Amazon keeps: the customer email/data, the brand, the storefront experience, the ability to re-market and build loyalty.
The elegant part of the Shopify bet

Amazon and Shopify can both grow at once because they serve two different flavors of the same need. If your edge is a product and you want volume now, rent Amazon's demand. If your edge is a brand and you want to own your customers and margins, arm yourself with Shopify. Most serious sellers now do both - Amazon for reach, Shopify for the brand-owned relationship. That is why "who owns the customer" is the entire strategic question.

5Other players and the evolution

Walmart Marketplace + WFS + Walmart Connect (Harsha's world)

Walmart is running the Amazon playbook as a credible #3, and it is the most interesting story on the board because it is the challenger with real scale. The three pieces mirror Amazon's three profit engines exactly:

  • Walmart Marketplace (the 3P store). Third-party sellers list on Walmart.com to reach Walmart's ~120M+ weekly customers and ~250M+ monthly site visitors. Referral fees are broadly Amazon-comparable (~6-15% by category). It is younger and less saturated than Amazon, so seller acquisition - onboarding good sellers with good catalog - is the core growth lever (this is the job Harsha works on).
  • WFS (Walmart Fulfillment Services). Walmart's answer to FBA - store inventory in Walmart's network, get fast tags and Walmart handling of pick/pack/ship/returns. WFS is the lock-in + buyer-promise engine, exactly as FBA is for Amazon.
  • Walmart Connect (retail media). The ads business - sponsored search + display monetizing that same seller base. It is a multi-billion-dollar, fast-growing, high-margin line, and it is the strategic reason the 3P marketplace exists: more sellers -> more ad demand -> more profit.
Why Walmart's angle is genuinely differentiated

Walmart has something Amazon spent 20 years and billions trying to buy: ~4,600 US stores within ~10 miles of ~90% of Americans. That turns into store-fulfilled delivery, in-store pickup/returns for online orders, and a grocery-anchored, weekly-habit customer base. For a seller, Walmart Marketplace offers Amazon-like reach that is less crowded (lower ad costs, more Buy Box opportunity today) but with a smaller (still huge) audience. The pitch to a seller is "the growth headroom of an earlier-stage Amazon, backed by the largest retailer on Earth."

The rest of the board

  • Etsy - the niche/handmade/vintage marketplace. Owns a specific kind of demand (crafted, personal, gift-y) rather than everything. Higher take rate (~6.5% + payments + ads/offsite ads that can push effective take well higher), defensible because the buyer intent is distinct.
  • eBay - the original C2C/SMB marketplace; still strong in used, collectibles, parts, and auctions. Owns the "long tail and pre-owned" slice of demand.
  • TikTok Shop / social commerce - the newest mechanism: demand created by content/discovery rather than search. The buyer is not looking for your product; a video makes them want it. Blurs the ad and the store into one. Fast-growing, take rate ~ high single digits and climbing.
  • Stripe / the payments layer - arms the "get paid" step for everyone building their own commerce (including many Shopify-adjacent and headless setups). The pure enabler: takes ~2.9% + 30c and never competes with the seller.

The evolution, in one line: from marketplaces that own demand (Amazon, Walmart, Etsy, eBay) toward a spectrum that also includes tools that empower sellers (Shopify, Stripe) and content that manufactures demand (TikTok Shop). The tension between owning demand and empowering the seller is the axis the whole industry rotates around.

6Unit economics (the two models are opposite)

The clearest way to feel the difference is to run $100 of sales through each model.

Amazon 3P: the compounding take

On a $100 sale (FBA + ads)Approx cost to seller
Referral fee (take rate)~$15 (about 8-15%; ~15% typical)
FBA fulfillment + storage~$10-20 (size/weight dependent)
Advertising (cost of being findable)~$10-20 (rising every year)
Blended take to Amazon~$40-50 of every $100 (~40-50%)
The number that shocks new sellers

Between referral + FBA + ads, Amazon can capture 40%+ (often closer to half) of a 3P seller's revenue. The seller is left to cover cost-of-goods, shipping-in, returns, and overhead out of the other ~50-60%. It is a fantastic deal for reach and a punishing one for margin - and the ads slice is what tipped it from "high rent" to "the landlord takes half."

Shopify: subscription + payments (you pay for tools, then buy your own traffic)

On a $100 sale (Shopify store)Approx cost to merchant
Shopify Payments (processing)~$2.9 + 30c (~2.9%)
Subscription (amortized per sale)fraction of a % at scale ($39-$2000+/mo flat)
Your own ad spend to get the visitorvaries wildly (this is the real cost - see below)
Direct take to Shopify~3% + a small subscription

Shopify Payments is the business. The subscription gets a merchant in the door; the ~2.9% payments take on rising GMV is where most revenue and growth come from. Merchant Solutions (payments + capital + shipping + Markets) now dwarfs the Subscriptions line. It is a "make money when our merchants make money" model - the enabler's alignment.

The catch that balances it out

Shopify's ~3% looks far cheaper than Amazon's ~40-50% - but that comparison hides the demand cost. On Amazon the ~40-50% includes access to the buyers. On Shopify the ~3% excludes demand entirely; the merchant must go buy every visitor via Meta/Google/TikTok ads, and customer-acquisition cost can easily exceed what Amazon's take would have been. You are not paying less; you are paying differently - and in exchange you own the customer.

7How it is playing out

  • Marketplaces vs independent storefronts is now a "both" world. Serious brands run Amazon (for reach) and a Shopify store (for owned relationships + margin) in parallel. Pure-marketplace sellers are increasingly fragile; pure-DTC sellers struggle to find demand cheaply. The winning posture is a portfolio across owned + rented demand.
  • The ads-tax on sellers keeps rising. Retail media (Amazon Ads ~$50B+, Walmart Connect multi-billion and growing fast, plus Instacart, Target, etc.) is the fastest-growing profit pool in commerce. It is functionally a rising tax on sellers to remain visible on shelves they used to reach organically. Expect the blended take to keep creeping up.
  • Social commerce is manufacturing demand, not just capturing it. TikTok Shop and live-shopping create want from content. This is a genuinely new mechanism for the "find demand" step - the first real challenge to search-driven discovery in a decade.
  • AI tools for sellers are the next wave. Listing generation, pricing, ad-bid optimization, demand forecasting, catalog cleanup, agentic storefront ops. Both platforms (Amazon's and Shopify's AI, Walmart's seller tools) and third parties are racing here.
  • Walmart is deliberately building the Amazon triangle (3P + WFS + Walmart Connect) to convert its physical scale into a rival profit engine. Seller acquisition is the top of that funnel - more good sellers -> more selection -> more shoppers -> more ad demand. It is the flywheel Amazon proved, run by the one company with the store footprint to differentiate it.
The whitespace worth noticing (Harsha's edge)

The seller-enablement and analytics layer is still fragmented. A multi-channel seller today juggles Amazon Seller Central, Walmart Seller Center, a Shopify admin, a TikTok Shop dashboard, plus bolt-on tools for ads, repricing, inventory, and reporting - none of which talk cleanly to each other. Whoever gives sellers a single, cross-platform "run my whole business" cockpit (list once, price everywhere, one P&L, AI-optimized ads across channels) is arming the rebels one level up. For a marketplace trying to win sellers, being the easiest and most profitable place to operate - not just the biggest audience - is an under-exploited lever.

8The pattern it exemplifies

Commerce enablement is the cleanest example of the eternal aggregator-vs-enabler split. There are exactly two winning strategies for serving the seller's need, and they are mirror images:

OWN the demand (aggregator)ARM the seller (enabler)
ExemplarsAmazon, Walmart, Etsy, eBay, TikTok ShopShopify, Stripe, BigCommerce
What it sells the sellerAccess to a captive audienceTools to run their own store
Who owns the customerThe platformThe merchant
How it makes moneyTake rate + fulfillment + ads (high take)Subscription + payments (low take, buy own traffic)
Seller's tradeoffReach now, thin margin, no ownershipOwnership + margin, but must find demand
Power dynamicPlatform commoditizes the sellerSeller keeps the brand and the data
The pattern in one line

OWN the demand and rent it to sellers (Amazon, Walmart) vs ARM the seller and let them own the customer (Shopify). This is aggregation theory applied to the supply side: aggregators concentrate demand and commoditize suppliers; enablers give suppliers independence and monetize the toolkit. Every company serving Need 12 is somewhere on that line - and the strategic question is always the same: who ends up owning the customer relationship?

9Glossary

TermMeaning
3P marketplaceThird-party marketplace: a platform where independent sellers list, using the platform's audience and rules (Amazon 3P, Walmart Marketplace). Contrast 1P, where the platform buys and resells the goods itself.
FBA / WFSFulfilled by Amazon / Walmart Fulfillment Services: the seller ships inventory to the platform, which stores, picks, packs, ships, and handles returns - unlocking the fast-shipping badge (Prime / Walmart tags) that drives conversion, and deepening lock-in.
Take rateThe share of a sale the platform keeps. On marketplaces this is the referral fee (~8-15%); blended with fulfillment + ads it can reach ~40-50% of a seller's revenue.
Retail mediaAdvertising sold by a retailer to its own sellers to be visible on its site (Amazon Ads, Walmart Connect). The fastest-growing, highest-margin profit pool in commerce; effectively a rising tax on sellers.
AggregatorA platform that owns the demand/customer relationship and commoditizes suppliers beneath it (Amazon, Walmart, Google).
EnablerThe anti-aggregator: a platform that arms suppliers with tools to run their own business and own their own customers (Shopify, Stripe).
Own-the-customerWhether the platform or the merchant holds the buyer's identity, email, payment method, and purchase history - the single most valuable asset in commerce, because it enables re-marketing, loyalty, and margin.
Social commerceSelling where demand is manufactured by content/discovery rather than search (TikTok Shop, live shopping). The buyer did not seek the product; a video created the want.
GMVGross Merchandise Value: the total value of goods sold through a platform. The headline scale metric; the platform's revenue is take-rate x GMV.
Buy BoxThe default "Add to Cart" winner on a product page when multiple sellers offer the same item. Winning it (via price, fulfillment speed, seller metrics) captures the vast majority of sales for that listing.
