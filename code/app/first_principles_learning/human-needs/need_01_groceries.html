Need: Groceries / Raw Food

The oldest, most repeated purchase in human life - and how a razor-thin, high-frequency, trust-driven business is served today.

Need note 01 · Sustenance / physiological (Maslow tier 1) · Captured 2026-07-11 · See The needs framework

0TL;DR

  • Groceries is the most durable need there is - people buy raw food roughly weekly, forever. It never churns, never goes out of fashion, and demand is inelastic.
  • It is a low-margin, high-frequency, trust + convenience + price business. Net grocery margins are ~1-3 percent, so you win on volume and operations, not per-item profit.
  • #1 Walmart wins on economies of scale: EDLP (everyday low prices), enormous supplier leverage, ~90 percent of the US within ~10 miles of a store, and those stores now double as delivery/pickup hubs.
  • #2 Costco wins on a different axiom: it makes its profit from the annual membership fee, not the product margin - so it can sell food near cost and still earn.
  • The whole category is going omnichannel: same-day pickup and delivery run out of existing stores, private label defends margin, and retail media (ads) is becoming the real profit engine.
  • Two clean ways to win the same need: scale-and-EDLP (Walmart) vs membership-loyalty (Costco). Same axiom, two mechanisms.

1The need as an axiom

Sustenance is Maslow tier 1 - physiological, non-negotiable, permanent. A caveman and a billionaire both eat several times a day. Unlike almost every other need, groceries has three properties that shape the entire industry:

  • Permanent: the need predates civilization and will outlive every current company. There is no substitute for eating.
  • High frequency: a typical household shops for groceries ~1-2 times per week - roughly 50-100 shopping trips a year, for a lifetime. Compare that to buying a car (once every ~6 years) or a house (a few times ever).
  • Inelastic and large: US grocery spending is roughly $1.5 trillion+ per year. People cut back at the margin in a downturn (trade down to cheaper brands) but they do not stop buying food.
Why frequency changes everything

Because the customer comes back ~weekly, the grocery relationship is the single most valuable habit in retail. Whoever owns the weekly trip owns a recurring, defensible, data-rich relationship - which is why grocery is a beachhead for pharmacy, fuel, financial services, and now advertising.

2The demand flow for groceries

Every need travels the same pipeline. For groceries the chain is unusually compressed - the customer already knows what they want, so discovery and trust matter less, while convenience, price, and fulfillment matter enormously.

NEED (hungry / restock)

  -> Discovery (which store? usually habit, not search)
  -> Trust (is it fresh? safe? priced fairly?)
  -> Decision (basket assembled)

  -> Payment (thin margin, high volume)
  -> Retention (habit, loyalty, membership, price memory)

The key insight

Groceries is not won at Discovery (like search) or Trust (like reviews). It is won at Fulfillment and Retention: get fresh food to the customer cheaply and reliably, then make the weekly trip a habit. That is an operations and real-estate game, not a marketing game.

3#1 Walmart - how it wins

Walmart is the largest grocer in the US (roughly a quarter of US grocery sales) and the largest company in the world by revenue (~$650 billion+ annually, more than half of it groceries). Its win is a textbook economies-of-scale flywheel.

The scale flywheel

  1. Huge volume -> supplier leverage. Walmart buys more from suppliers than anyone, so it negotiates the lowest cost of goods. A supplier cannot afford to lose Walmart.
  2. Low cost -> EDLP (everyday low prices). Instead of high-low promo cycles, Walmart advertises consistently low prices. This trains customer trust ("I do not need to wait for a sale").
  3. Low prices -> more customers -> more volume. The flywheel spins: more scale means even better costs, means even lower prices.

The physical moat

Roughly 90 percent of the US population lives within ~10 miles of a Walmart (~4,600 US stores). That footprint, built over decades, is nearly impossible to replicate. Crucially, in the omnichannel era those stores flipped from a cost into a weapon:

Stores as fulfillment centers

Every Walmart is now a mini-warehouse ~10 miles from most Americans. Online orders are picked from store shelves for same-day pickup and delivery. Amazon has to build fulfillment centers; Walmart already had 4,600 of them, staffed and stocked. This is why Walmart's e-commerce and delivery grew so fast - it bolted digital onto existing concrete.

The new margin layers

  • Walmart Marketplace: a third-party seller platform (like Amazon's) - higher-margin than selling groceries itself.
  • Walmart Connect (retail media): selling ads to brands who want placement in front of Walmart's shoppers. This is a multi-billion-dollar, very high-margin business layered on top of low-margin groceries.
  • Walmart+: a membership (free delivery, fuel discounts) - a direct answer to Amazon Prime and to Costco's loyalty.

4#2 Costco - the different angle

Costco serves the exact same need but with an inverted business model. It does not try to out-scale Walmart on assortment or price-per-item across the board. Instead it changes where the profit comes from.

Profit from the fee, not the margin

Costco marks up products only ~11-15 percent (vs 25-40 percent+ at typical retailers) and often makes near-zero net profit on the goods themselves. Its operating profit comes almost entirely from the annual membership fee (~$65-130/year), which is nearly pure margin. Members renew at ~90 percent. The goods are the bait; the membership is the business.

The mechanics of the Costco model

  • Curated, limited SKUs: a typical Costco stocks only ~4,000 SKUs vs ~30,000-50,000 at a supermarket. Fewer items -> larger orders per item -> even deeper supplier discounts -> faster inventory turns. It sells more of less.
  • The "treasure hunt": a rotating set of one-off deals (electronics, seasonal, luxury goods next to bulk food) makes shopping feel like a hunt, driving repeat visits and impulse spend.
  • Bulk / warehouse format: no fancy shelving, products sold on pallets, minimal labor per dollar of sales - which keeps the cost structure low enough to price near cost.
  • Kirkland Signature (private label): a premium-quality own-brand that customers trust as much as national brands, deepening loyalty and margin.
  • Loyalty as a moat: because you already paid to get in the door, you rationalize shopping there to "get your money's worth" - a powerful psychological lock-in.

5Other notable players / how it is evolving

  • Kroger - the largest pure-play US supermarket chain (~2,700 stores). Competes on scale like Walmart but grocery-focused, and has built its own sizable retail-media business (Kroger Precision Marketing). Its attempted merger with Albertsons was blocked in 2024 on antitrust grounds - a sign of how consolidated the category already is.
  • Instacart - the aggregation layer that sits on top of grocers. It does not own stores; it owns the app, the shoppers, and increasingly the ad inventory. It turned into a profitable advertising and software company (powering retailers' own delivery), showing that even in groceries the aggregator monetizes via ads.
  • Amazon / Whole Foods - Amazon bought Whole Foods (2017) to get physical grocery footprint and learn perishables. Despite Amazon's e-commerce dominance, groceries remain stubbornly physical and local, and Amazon is still a modest grocery share - proof the fulfillment problem is genuinely hard.
  • Aldi (and Lidl) - hard-discounters using an extreme version of the Costco SKU logic: ~1,500-2,000 mostly private-label SKUs, tiny stores, minimal labor, rock-bottom prices. Fast-growing and forcing everyone else on price.
  • Online grocery penetration - still only ~12-15 percent of US grocery sales (vs 20 percent+ for retail overall). Food is heavy, perishable, low-margin, and last-mile is expensive, so it digitized slower than any other category. This is why the winners fulfill from local stores rather than distant warehouses.

6Unit economics

Groceries has some of the thinnest margins in all of retail. Understanding the numbers explains every strategic move in the category.

Why margins are so brutal

Food is perishable (spoilage = pure loss), heavy and bulky (expensive to ship), commoditized (a gallon of milk is a gallon of milk), and price-transparent (shoppers know what things should cost). There is nowhere to hide. That is precisely why grocers chase margin elsewhere: private label, membership fees, and retail media.

7How it is playing out / where it is heading

  • Omnichannel is the default. The line between "online" and "in-store" is dissolving. The winning model is one system: shop online, fulfill from the nearest store, pick up in the parking lot or get same-day delivery.
  • Same-day, from stores. Distant fulfillment centers lose to local stores for fresh food. Walmart's ~4,600 stores and Kroger's ~2,700 become the last-mile network. Proximity beats warehouse automation for perishables.
  • Delivery economics are still hard. Last-mile delivery of a low-margin basket often loses money per order. Grocers offset it with membership fees (Walmart+, Instacart+), delivery fees, and by using it to sell ads and higher-margin items.
  • Private label keeps growing. In inflationary times shoppers trade down to store brands, which happen to be higher-margin - a rare win-win for the retailer.
  • Retail media is the real story. The most important shift is that grocers are becoming advertising businesses wearing a grocery costume. Walmart Connect, Kroger Precision Marketing, and Instacart Ads turn shopper data into high-margin ad revenue that subsidizes the thin-margin food business. The food gets you the audience; the ads make the money.
The reader's takeaway

Hold the need fixed (people buy food weekly, forever) and watch the mechanism evolve: the frontier is not "better shelves" but who owns the local last-mile and who monetizes the shopper's attention. The grocer that best fuses cheap fulfillment with a profitable ad network wins the next decade.

8The pattern it exemplifies

Groceries is the cleanest illustration of a recurring first-principles lesson: the same durable need can be won two completely different ways.

PatternChampionThe bet
Economies of scale + EDLPWalmartBe the biggest, buy cheapest, pass savings on, win on volume and footprint.
Membership / loyalty economicsCostcoSell goods near cost, profit from the fee, lock in loyal high-value members.
  • Scale wins by pushing cost down for everyone - broad reach, low prices, thin margins made up in volume.
  • Membership wins by changing where the profit lives - the product is bait, the recurring fee is the business, loyalty is the moat.
  • Both are valid. The lesson: when a need is huge and commoditized, do not just fight on price - find a different profit surface (a fee, an own-brand, an ad network) that competitors cannot easily copy.
The transferable idea

In any brutal, low-margin, high-frequency category, the winners either (a) out-scale everyone until cost itself is a moat, or (b) relocate the profit to a defensible layer (membership, private label, advertising). Groceries shows both playbooks running side by side on the exact same axiom.
