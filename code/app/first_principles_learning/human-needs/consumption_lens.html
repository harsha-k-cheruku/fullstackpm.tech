The Consumption Lens

A first-principles taxonomy of how humans consume - the act that connects a need (demand) to a mechanism (a company).

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/human-needs · Companion to The needs framework · Cross-refs ../future-of-work/ and ../economics-of-ai/

0TL;DR

  • Consumption is spending finite resources - money, time, attention - to satisfy a need. It is the ACT that connects a need (demand) to a mechanism (a company). Every purchase is a need being met through a type of consumption.
  • Your intuitive buckets - physical goods, digital, services, survival/care - are useful but blurry (a gym is a service plus a physical place). They are starting points, not first principles.
  • Underneath sit ~7 real dimensions that DEFINE any act of consumption: atoms vs bits, consumable vs durable vs access, discretionary vs not, good vs service, ownership vs access, frequency, and who pays.
  • The axes predict the economics. Bits scale to infinity (winner-take-all); atoms carry marginal cost and logistics (thin margins); labor does not scale (fragmented); survival is inelastic and third-party-paid (regulated, incumbent-heavy).
  • The megatrend the lens reveals: atoms -> bits -> services-as-bits. First we digitized information goods (music, books, film). Now AI is digitizing SERVICES (labor -> software). Physical and survival resist longest.
  • Consumption type is destiny for a business's margin, scalability, and moat - and the arrow of history digitizes consumption from bits outward, with atoms and survival last.

1What consumption IS (the first principle)

Strip away the marketing and every purchase is the same act: a human spends a finite resource to satisfy a need. The three finite resources are money, time, and attention - and they are genuinely finite (you get 24 hours and a bounded wallet, and attention is scarcer than both). Consumption is how you convert those resources into need-satisfaction.

The connecting act

In the needs framework we said: needs are axioms, companies are mechanisms. Consumption is the ACT that connects the two. A need is latent demand; a company is a mechanism; consumption is the transaction where the need reaches through the mechanism and gets satisfied. need (demand) -> consumption (the act) -> mechanism (the company).

So every purchase can be read two ways: which need is being satisfied? (the demand question, answered by the needs framework) and which type of consumption is being used to satisfy it? (the supply-side question, answered by this note). This note is the second lens. It matters because - as we will see - the type of consumption dictates the economics of whatever company serves it.

2The 4 intuitive buckets (your own starting point)

Ask anyone how they spend and they will reach for four folk categories. They are worth writing down because they are where intuition starts - and because seeing them blur is what forces us to dig for the real axes.

BucketExamples
Physical / goodsFood, cars, clothes, furniture, gadgets - things made of matter that you take home.
DigitalStreaming entertainment, audio/music, e-books, software, social apps, games.
ServicesMassage, travel, spa, gym, haircut, consulting, cleaning - someone doing something for you.
Survival / careHealthcare, dentist, insurance, medicine - the stuff you buy to stay alive and safe.
Why the buckets are blurry

A gym is a service (classes, trainers) plus a physical presence (the building, the machines) plus a subscription (access). Which bucket?

Travel is a service (the airline flies you) plus an experience plus physical fuel and seats. Which bucket?

A meal at a restaurant is a physical good (the food) plus a service (the cooking and serving). Which bucket?

The buckets overlap because they are surface labels, not underlying properties. To predict economics we need dimensions that do not blur - axes every purchase can be scored on independently.

3The real first principles - the ~7 dimensions that DEFINE consumption

These are the axes. Any act of consumption is a point in this space; the four folk buckets are just popular neighborhoods within it. Score a purchase on all seven and you have described it precisely - and, as section 5 shows, you have also predicted its economics.

#DimensionThe two polesWhy it matters
1Atoms vs BitsPhysical / rival / marginal cost > 0  vs  digital / non-rival / near-zero marginal costThe single most predictive axis. Bits can be copied and distributed for almost nothing; atoms cost money every single time.
2Consumable vs Durable vs AccessUsed-up once vs owned over time vs rented / subscribedSets how often the customer comes back and how revenue recurs (a sandwich vs a fridge vs Netflix).
3Discretionary vs Non-discretionarySkippable want vs survival needDetermines price elasticity. Non-discretionary demand barely bends with price; discretionary demand collapses in a downturn.
4Good vs ServiceA finished object vs someone's ongoing labor / timeA good is decoupled from its maker; a service is bottlenecked by human hours. This decides scalability.
5Ownership vs AccessBuy the asset vs rent / stream / subscribeBuy the car vs Uber; buy the DVD vs Netflix. Shifts capital, commitment, and who carries the asset risk.
6FrequencyOne-time vs recurring vs continuousFrequency is the heartbeat of a business - it sets lifetime value, habit, and how deep the relationship goes.
7Who paysSelf-paid vs third-party (insurance / employer / government)When the user is not the payer, price signals break and whole markets warp - see healthcare.
Note the independence

These axes are (mostly) orthogonal. Netflix is bits + access + discretionary + recurring + self-paid. A hospital stay is atoms-plus-labor + non-discretionary + one-time-ish + third-party-paid. Scoring on independent axes is exactly what the folk buckets cannot do.

4The 4 buckets are just CLUSTERS on those axes

Now the buckets stop being mysterious. Each is a recurring cluster - a common combination of axis values. That is why they feel real (the combinations genuinely recur) and why they blur (a real purchase can sit between clusters).

The reframe

You did not have four categories - you had four clusters in a 7-dimensional space. The gym feels blurry because it is literally a point between the Services cluster and the Access/Physical clusters. Once you think in axes, the blur disappears: you just read off its coordinates.

5The axes PREDICT the economics (the powerful part)

Here is the payoff for doing the work of finding real axes: the axes are not just descriptive, they are predictive. The coordinates of a purchase tell you the shape of the business that serves it - its margins, its scalability, and its moat.

Bits (digital) -> zero marginal cost -> winner-take-all

If it is bits, the second copy costs almost nothing to make and nothing to ship. So the marginal cost of one more user approaches zero, which means you can scale infinitely. That drives three consequences: monetize with ads or subscription (not per-unit price), tend toward winner-take-all (one great product can serve the whole planet), and reward aggregation (own the demand relationship and commoditize supply below you).

Atoms (physical) -> marginal cost > 0 + logistics -> thin margins

If it is atoms, every unit costs real money to make and move. You compete on economies of scale (spread fixed costs over volume) and win on operational excellence, but margins stay thin because matter is not free and logistics is hard. This is the Walmart/Amazon-retail world: enormous revenue, tight percentages.

Services (labor) -> does NOT scale like software -> fragmented

If it is labor, output is linear in human hours - two haircuts need twice the barber-time. Labor does not copy like bits, so services stay fragmented, local, and hard to aggregate. There is no global monopoly on haircuts. This held true for all of history... until AI turns some services into software (services-as-software) and breaks the linearity - see section 6.

Survival -> inelastic + third-party payer -> weird, regulated economics

If it is non-discretionary and someone else pays, price signals break. Demand is inelastic (you do not skip chemotherapy because it got pricier), the third-party payer warps incentives, and the result is regulated, incumbent-heavy markets with opaque pricing. Healthcare is the archetype.

The one-line law

Marginal cost and scalability are set by atoms vs bits and good vs service; elasticity and market structure are set by discretionary vs not and who pays. Give me those four coordinates and I can sketch the business.

6THE megatrend the lens reveals (the payoff)

Look at the axes over time and one arrow dominates history:

ATOMS  ->  BITS  ->  SERVICES-AS-BITS

Step 1: digitize INFORMATION GOODS   (music, books, film, mail)   [done]
Step 2: digitize SERVICES/LABOR        (via AI: labor -> software)  [now]
Step 3: physical + survival            (atoms + human trust)        [last]

Step 1 already happened. Anything that was really information wearing a physical costume got converted to bits: records and CDs became streaming, books became e-books and audio, film became downloads and streams, letters became email. The moment the underlying good was information, atoms were just packaging - and packaging loses to zero-cost distribution.

Step 2 is happening now. AI digitizes the thing that resisted for all of history: labor. A service used to be linear in human hours, which is why services never scaled. When an AI can do the drafting, the analysis, the support, the coding - the service turns into software, and software scales like bits. This is services-as-software, and it is the Future-of-Work thesis viewed from the demand side. (See ../future-of-work/ and ../economics-of-ai/.)

Why bits beat atoms

Distribution at zero cost. A bit can be copied perfectly and delivered anywhere instantly for nearly nothing. An atom must be manufactured and physically moved every single time. Once a need can be served in bits, the bit-based mechanism out-distributes and out-scales the atom-based one - so it wins the moment quality is good enough.

Why services were the last frontier

Labor did not scale. You could not copy a doctor, a lawyer, or a tutor the way you copy a song file. Human hours were the hard ceiling on every service business, which kept them local and fragmented. AI is the first technology that lets service output be reproduced without reproducing the human hours - which is why services are only now joining the digitization wave.

What resists longest

Physical and survival. You cannot download a haircut, a hip replacement, or a house. Atoms resist because matter must still be moved, and survival/care resists because it carries human trust and bodily risk that people are slow to hand to software. These are the last dominoes - which is exactly why they are the durable, defensible corners of the economy.

7Access is eating ownership (a secondary trend)

Running alongside the big arrow is a quieter shift on dimension 5: buy -> subscribe/rent, across category after category.

CategoryOwnership (old)Access (new)
MusicOwn CDs / MP3sSpotify subscription
Film / TVOwn DVDsNetflix streaming
CarsOwn the vehicleUber / car-sharing
SoftwareBuy a perpetual licenseSaaS subscription
Tools / storageOwn the server / the drillCloud / rental

Why access is winning: for the customer, lower commitment (no big upfront outlay, no asset to maintain or resell) and always up to date (the latest catalog, the latest version). For the business, continuous revenue (recurring beats one-time), a durable relationship, and the ability to ship improvements continuously. This is why the subscription business model quietly conquered software, media, and increasingly physical goods - it aligns with both sides' incentives.

Note the interaction

Access rides on top of bits. It is easiest to rent-not-own when the thing is already digital (you cannot half-own a stream). So the access trend accelerates wherever the atoms-to-bits trend has already landed - the two reinforce each other.

8How to use the lens

For any product, run the checklist: which dimensions define it? Score it on the seven axes. Those coordinates predict its margin structure, scalability, moat type, and whether AI will digitize it.

ProductCoordinatesWhat the lens predicts
NetflixBits / access / discretionary / recurring / self-paidZero marginal cost -> scalable, subscription-monetized, winner-take-most. Moat = content + scale + habit.
A haircutAtoms-adjacent / service / labor / one-time-recurring / self-paidLinear in human hours -> non-scalable, local, fragmented. Moat = location + relationship. Hard for AI to digitize (it is atoms on your head).
HealthcareSurvival / service + atoms / non-discretionary / third-party-paidInelastic + payer-warped -> regulated, incumbent-heavy, opaque pricing. Resists digitization (human trust + bodily risk).
The one question that does the work

Where does this purchase sit on atoms vs bits and good vs service? That pair alone tells you whether the business scales like software, grinds like retail, or stays fragmented like local labor - and therefore where AI is about to arrive.

9The pattern it exemplifies

Two durable truths fall out of the lens:

  • Consumption type is destiny for business economics. You do not get to choose your margin structure or scalability by willpower - they are handed to you by the axes of the thing you sell. Bits scale; atoms grind; labor fragments; survival gets regulated. The coordinates come first, the business shape follows.
  • The arrow of history digitizes consumption from bits outward. Information goods went first, services are going now (via AI), and atoms + survival go last (matter and human trust are the hard boundary). If you want to know what gets disrupted next, find what just became expressible in bits.
How this note fits the library

The needs framework gives you the demand side (what humans permanently want). This note gives you the supply-side act (how they spend to get it) and shows that the type of consumption predicts the mechanism's economics. Together they let you read any company: which need, served through which type of consumption, with which resulting economics - and whether AI is coming for it. 10Glossary

TermMeaning
ConsumptionSpending a finite resource (money, time, attention) to satisfy a need; the act that connects demand to a mechanism.
Atoms vs bitsPhysical matter (costs money per unit, must be moved) vs digital information (copyable and distributable at near-zero cost).
Rival vs non-rivalRival: my use prevents yours (a sandwich). Non-rival: we can both use it at once (a song file).
Marginal costThe cost to produce/deliver one more unit. High for atoms, near-zero for bits.
DiscretionaryA skippable want whose demand is elastic (bends with price); opposite of non-discretionary survival demand.
Durable vs consumableDurable: owned and used over time (a fridge). Consumable: used up in one go (a meal).
Ownership vs accessBuying an asset outright vs renting/streaming/subscribing to its use (own a car vs Uber).
Third-party payerWhen the user is not the one paying (insurer, employer, government), breaking normal price signals and warping the market.
Services-as-softwareAI converting labor-bottlenecked services into scalable software, breaking the linear-in-human-hours ceiling.
AggregationOwning the demand/discovery relationship (favored by bits) and commoditizing the supply beneath you.
