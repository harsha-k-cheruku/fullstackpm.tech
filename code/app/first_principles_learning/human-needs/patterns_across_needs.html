Patterns Across All the Needs

The capstone: fifteen needs, six archetypes. What recurs when you look at the whole board at once.

Synthesis of all 15 need notes · The needs framework · Captured 2026-07-11

0TL;DR

  • 15 needs collapse into ~6 repeating business archetypes. Once you see them, every company is a remix of a handful of moves.
  • The single most common move: aggregate demand -> commoditize supply -> bolt on advertising. It shows up in search, social, retail, food, and marketplaces alike.
  • Almost every need is contested by two opposite mechanisms - e.g. access vs ownership, scale vs membership, own-demand vs arm-the-seller.
  • Moats rhyme: network effects, economies of scale, switching costs, brand, proprietary data, and toll-road control cover nearly all of them.
  • Care and (half of) education are the outliers - the human-premium needs that resist aggregation and automation. The exceptions that prove the rule.

1The big picture

Read all fifteen notes back to back and the noise cancels out. Underneath the logos, three truths hold everywhere:

  1. Needs are constant; mechanisms churn. The need ("get from A to B", "know the answer", "be seen") never moves. The winning company is just whoever serves it best with today's technology - and it changes when technology changes.
  2. The same patterns recur. There are not 15 kinds of business; there are ~6, remixed across needs.
  3. Power flows to whoever owns the demand. In almost every need, the winner owns the customer relationship and forces suppliers to commoditize (aggregation theory).

2The master monetization pattern

The move that appears the most

Aggregate demand -> commoditize supply -> add an advertising / visibility tax.
Google (intent), Meta & Instagram & TikTok (attention), Amazon & Walmart (consumption + 3P ads), DoorDash & Uber (local demand + sponsored listings), Zillow (leads). Once you own the demand, suppliers must PAY YOU to reach it - and that ad layer is the highest-margin part of the business. It is the same move a dozen times.

Why it dominates: advertising is nearly pure margin, and it grows automatically as the demand base grows - so any business that has aggregated demand is structurally pulled toward selling it back to suppliers.

The stacking insight

The biggest companies stack archetypes. Amazon = Marketplace (2) + Scale (3) + Toll road (4, AWS). Walmart = Scale (3) + Marketplace (2) + Attention/ads (1, Walmart Connect). Google = Attention (1) + Toll road (cloud). Stacking archetypes is how a business escapes the ceiling of any one model.

4The recurring moats

Across all 15 needs, defensibility comes from the same short list:

  • Network effects - the product improves with users. (Meta, Uber, DoorDash, Visa, marketplaces.) Note: often local (Uber, DoorDash) rather than global.
  • Economies of scale - biggest player has the lowest unit cost. (Walmart, Costco, Amazon retail.)
  • Switching costs / lock-in - painful to leave. (Microsoft, Salesforce, Prime, CUDA.)
  • Brand / trust - commands a premium or the default. (Google "the verb", luxury, Apple.)
  • Proprietary data / process - a compounding, un-copyable asset. (Google, UnitedHealth, Epic EHR.)
  • Toll-road / ecosystem control - own a chokepoint everyone must cross. (Visa rails, Nvidia CUDA, TSMC, ASML.)
  • Credential / signaling - control a trusted certification. (Universities.)

5Two ways to win the same need

The most beautiful recurring pattern: almost every need is fought over by two opposite mechanisms, each internally coherent.

NeedMechanism AMechanism B (the opposite)

MobilityAccess / marketplace (Uber)Ownership / vertical integration (Tesla)
CommerceOwn the demand, rent to sellers (Amazon)Arm the seller, they own the customer (Shopify)

Why this matters

When you see a dominant mechanism, ask "what is the coherent opposite?" - that is usually where the #2 lives and where the next disruptor attacks. The opposite is not wrong; it is a different set of trade-offs serving the same axiom.

What the matrix shows

Attention/Ads and Marketplace are the most common columns - the internet's two default business models. Care has only one weak mark (a thin marketplace) - it is the least aggregated need on the board. And the biggest need (Commerce) lights up the most columns because its winners stack archetypes.

7Mechanism shifts in progress

The board is not static. Where a new technology is currently challenging the #1:

  • Knowledge: search -> answer-engines (Google vs OpenAI). The most active shift on the board right now.
  • Mobility: driver marketplace -> autonomous fleets (Uber vs Waymo/Tesla). Could flip asset-light into asset-heavy.
  • Productivity: tools-that-help -> agents-that-do (the seat-based model under pressure). Tie to the Future-of-Work note.
  • Education: expensive credentialed learning -> free AI tutors + skills-based hiring (unbundling the signal from the teaching).
  • Money: card rails -> embedded finance + stablecoins wrapping/threatening the rails.
The rule for predicting the next winner

Hold the need constant. Ask: what new technology now serves it better? That is where the next mechanism - and the next #1 - is born. Every shift above is the same need meeting a new tool.

8What stays human (the outliers)

Two needs refuse to follow the aggregation/automation playbook, and they teach the most:

  • Care - trust, physical presence, empathy, and liability keep it human, labor-intensive, and local. Only the least-physical slice (mental-health apps) scaled like software. It is the living proof of the human-premium invariants from the Future-of-Work note.
  • Education (the credential half) - AI is collapsing the cost of learning, but the trusted signal (a degree employers believe) stays sticky and human-gatekept.
The unifying lesson

The forces that made the giants - cheap distribution, aggregation, automation - hit a wall exactly where trust, physical presence, empathy, and accountability are the product. Those are the same invariants your other notes flagged as the durable human premium. The exceptions map the boundary of the whole system.

9Synthesis

The whole board in one paragraph

Human needs are the fixed axioms; companies are the churning mechanisms that serve them. There are only about six ways to build a business - attention aggregator, marketplace, scale retailer, toll road, subscription/lock-in, vertical integrator/brand - and the giants simply stack them. The most common single move is to aggregate demand and then tax the suppliers who must reach it (advertising). Almost every need is contested by two opposite mechanisms, and the winner shifts whenever a new technology serves the old need better. The only needs that escape this gravity are the deeply human ones - care, and the credentialing half of education - where trust and presence are the product. Learn the six archetypes and the two-mechanism duality, and you can read any company, in any industry, on sight.

That is the payoff of the whole collection: not 15 stories, but one pattern language for how businesses serve what humans permanently want.
