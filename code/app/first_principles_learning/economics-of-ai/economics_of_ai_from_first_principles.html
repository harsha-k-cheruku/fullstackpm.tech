The Economics of AI / Compute from First Principles

Where the money and the compute go when intelligence gets cheap.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/economics-of-ai · Captured 2026-07-11 · Sits on economic axioms; base axiom imported from ../ai/ and ../llms/

0TL;DR

  • This is a distinct layer built on economic axioms (scarcity, marginal cost, moats), taking "intelligence is getting cheap" as its base axiom.
  • Training = one-time capital cost, concentrated in a few labs. Inference = recurring cost that scales with usage - and agents are inference-hogs.
  • Center of gravity shifts decisively toward inference. Inference $/token falls roughly an order of magnitude per year.
  • Build for where cost is going, not where it is - things uneconomical today are trivial in ~18 months.
  • Value accrues to those who own the context and rent the intelligence; models commoditize, domains and trust do not.

1Where it sits (a genuinely new layer)

This is your meta-principle in action: a distinct first-principles topic that sits on economic axioms (scarcity, marginal cost, comparative advantage, moats) more than on the LLM axioms - even though it is tightly connected. It imports one axiom from the AI/LLM layer ("intelligence/generation is getting cheap") and builds an economy on top of it.

2The axioms

  1. Value concentrates in what is scarce. When something becomes abundant, its price falls and the scarce complements rise. (Cheap intelligence -> scarce verification, judgment, trust, data.)
  2. Marginal cost drives price over time. As the marginal cost of generation approaches zero, you cannot durably charge for generation itself - only for the scarce wrapper around it.
  3. Moats come from what cannot be copied - proprietary data, feedback loops, distribution, switching costs, trust - not from the model (which everyone can rent).
  4. Comparative advantage still holds. Even when AI does everything better, humans specialize where their relative disadvantage is smallest (the invariants) and trade the rest to machines.
  5. Capex vs opex split. Training is lumpy capital; inference is metered operating cost. Business models differ sharply depending on which one you live in.
  6. Cost curves fall predictably. Compute/efficiency improvements make today's uneconomical thing tomorrow's default. Design for the curve, not the snapshot.

3The mechanisms that follow

4Training vs inference (the central split)

Why inference wins the volume war

Agents are inference-hogs: one agent task = dozens of model calls in a loop, and "reasoning"/test-time-compute models deliberately spend MORE inference for better answers. As agents proliferate, aggregate inference demand dwarfs training. The center of gravity shifts decisively toward inference.

The cost curve rule

Inference $/token has been falling roughly an order of magnitude per year. Build for where cost is going, not where it is - design agents that will be cheap in ~18 months, and things that look uneconomical today become defaults soon.

5Where value accrues

  • Own the context, rent the intelligence. The model is a commodity input you rent; your edge is the domain data, workflow, and trust wrapped around it.
  • Thin wrappers die; deep wrappers win. A prompt with no moat gets crushed by the next model release; a tool that owns a domain's data + workflow + verification + trust captures the value of cheap intelligence.
  • The moat is proprietary data + feedback loops. Models commoditize; your domain-specific eval-set, your users' data, and your distribution do not. A product that learns from its own usage compounds.
  • Price for outcomes, not tokens. Value = (work displaced or enabled) x (trust it is correct). Pricing power lives in the verification/trust layer, not the compute.

6Investment implications

  • Be an inference renter, not a model trainer. Ride falling inference cost + rising capability; do not try to fund a frontier lab.
  • Build appreciating assets. Agents/workflows you build today improve for free as models improve underneath - you are long a rising tide you do not pay for.
  • Accumulate proprietary data + trust early. These are the durable moats; start the feedback loop now.
  • Design for the cost curve. Build things that are marginal today because they will be cheap soon; you get a head start on the market.
One line

Training is a rich-lab capex race; inference is the exploding, falling-cost opex layer where the rest of us live. Rent the intelligence, own the context, price for outcomes, and build for where the cost curve is heading.
