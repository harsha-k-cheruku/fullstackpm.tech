Computation as a Substrate

Computation is not "what computers do." It is the substrate-independent process of transforming information by following rules - and it may be the deepest layer of all, the one that even the other four substrates run on top of. Added as the honest 5th.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/foundations · Captured 2026-07-12 · The process running under Information, Evolution, and AI

0TL;DR

  • Computation = rules + state + iteration. Transform some stored information by mechanically applying rules, step after step. That is all it is - and it needs no electronics.
  • It is substrate-independent. The same computation can run on transistors, gears, neurons, DNA, dominoes, or people with pencils. The process is what matters, not the material.
  • Universality (Church-Turing): there exists a single kind of machine that can simulate any other computer. Computation is one universal thing, not many separate tricks.
  • It may be the deepest substrate. Evolution IS a computation; information is what computation operates ON; networks compute collectively; and physics itself may be computational ("it from bit"). The other four arguably run on this one.
  • But it has hard, provable limits. Some things are undecidable (the halting problem), some functions are uncomputable, and many computable things are intractable (P vs NP). Not everything can be computed, and not everything computable can be computed in time.
  • It is physical. Erasing information costs energy (Landauer's limit) - which is why computation and thermodynamics are the same story from two directions.
  • The honest caveat: "everything is computation" is a powerful lens that can overreach. We add it as the 5th substrate and flag where treating it as literal truth becomes dogma.

1What computation actually is

Forget silicon. Strip computation to its irreducible core and you get three ingredients:

The three ingredients

1. State - some stored information (symbols on a tape, marks on paper, voltages, the arrangement of anything).

2. Rules - a fixed, finite set of instructions of the form "if you see this state, do that" (read, write, move, transform).

3. Iteration - apply the rules over and over, each step's output becoming the next step's input.

Run that loop and you have computation: information transformed, mechanically, into new information. No understanding, no meaning, no electricity required.

state = initial_information
WHILE not done:
    look at current state
    apply a rule   -> produce new state   (mechanical, no "understanding")
# result: information transformed into other information

This is the essence of Alan Turing's 1936 abstraction, the Turing machine: an imaginary device with an infinite tape of cells, a head that reads/writes one symbol, and a tiny table of rules. Absurdly simple - and yet anything any computer can ever do can be done by this. The power was never in the hardware; it was in the idea of mechanical rule-following over state.

Why "substrate-independent" is the key word

A computation is defined by the pattern of rule-following, not the stuff doing it. The same algorithm runs on transistors, brass gears (Babbage), neurons, DNA strands, water pipes, or a room of people passing notes. Speed differs wildly; the computation is identical. This is the exact same "substrate independence" you met in Evolution as the Meta-Algorithm - and it is not a coincidence (see §3).

2Universality: the one-machine-to-rule-them-all result

The deepest fact about computation is universality. Turing proved you can build a single machine - a universal Turing machine - that takes, as input, a description of any other machine plus that machine's input, and then faithfully simulates it.

The Church-Turing thesis

Independently, Alonzo Church (lambda calculus) and Alan Turing (machines) arrived at the same class of computable functions. The thesis: every effectively-calculable function can be computed by a Turing machine. Different formalisms - Turing machines, lambda calculus, recursive functions, your laptop, a quantum computer - all compute exactly the same set of things. There is one notion of "computable," not many.

Two consequences that sound like science fiction but are plain math:

  • All general-purpose computers are the same computer. Your phone, a 1970s mainframe, and a supercomputer differ only in speed and memory, not in what is possible. Each can, in principle, simulate the others. This is why a program is portable at all.
  • Software is a machine described in data. Because a universal machine reads other machines as input, the line between "hardware" and "program" dissolves - a program is just a machine written down. (This is also why an LLM, itself a program, can run programs it was handed in a prompt.)

Universality is why computation is a candidate substrate and not just a topic: it is a single, closed, well-defined thing that shows up identically everywhere it appears.

3Why it may be the deepest substrate of the five

Here is the argument that computation belongs underneath the other four foundations - the reason a purist would call it the 1st, not the 5th:

SubstrateIts relationship to computation
EvolutionEvolution literally is a computation - an algorithm (variation + selection + retention) run on a physical substrate. We already called it "the meta-algorithm." An algorithm is a computation.
InformationInformation is what computation operates on; computation is what transforms information. They are two halves of one coin - static (information) vs dynamic (computation).
NetworksA network of simple parts following local rules is a distributed computer. Ant colonies, brains, markets, and cellular automata all compute collectively - emergence is computation you did not design.
EnergyComputation has an irreducible energy cost (Landauer, §5), and some physicists argue physical law itself is computational. Energy and computation bound each other.
The strong claim: "it from bit"

Physicist John Wheeler's slogan "it from bit" - and the "digital physics" of Konrad Zuse, Ed Fredkin, and Stephen Wolfram - push the idea to its limit: the universe itself is a computation, reality is information being transformed by rules, and physics is the software. Whether or not that is literally true (nobody knows), it explains why the computational lens keeps working everywhere: if everything is running an algorithm, of course "algorithm" describes everything.

Why we still list it 5th, not 1st

Ordering is a presentation choice, not a truth claim. We keep Information, Energy, Evolution, and Networks first because they are more concrete and independently observable; Computation is the most abstract and the most contested as a literal foundation. It is the deepest and the least certain - so it goes last as the capstone that ties the substrates together, with its caveats attached (§6-7).

4The hard limits: what computation cannot do

A substrate is only understood at its boundaries. Computation's limits are not engineering problems - they are provable, permanent walls. This is the most important and least-known part.

Undecidability - the halting problem

Turing proved (1936) that no program can exist that decides, for every possible program + input, whether that program will eventually halt or loop forever. Not "we haven't found one" - it is impossible, by a clean self-reference contradiction (ask the halting-checker to analyze a program built to do the opposite of whatever it predicts).

Why this matters beyond computer science

The halting problem is the computational sibling of Godel's incompleteness: there are true things no formal system can prove, and there are questions no algorithm can answer. It puts a hard ceiling on what analysis, prediction, and even AI can ever guarantee. Some questions are not hard - they are closed.

Uncomputable functions

Because there are uncountably many possible functions but only countably many possible programs, almost all functions are uncomputable - there is simply no algorithm for them. Most of mathematical possibility is forever out of reach of any machine, anywhere, ever.

Intractability - computable but not in time (P vs NP)

Even when something is computable, it may take longer than the age of the universe. Complexity theory sorts problems by how their cost grows with size:

  • P - solvable in "reasonable" (polynomial) time. Tractable.
  • NP - a proposed answer can be checked quickly, but finding it may be brutally slow.
  • The P vs NP question (is finding as easy as checking? - the field's biggest open problem, widely believed "no") is exactly the "verification is cheaper than generation" asymmetry you met in Trust & Verification and Future of Work. That asymmetry is not a business quirk - it is a suspected law of computation.
The three walls, stacked

Uncomputable (no algorithm exists) → undecidable (no algorithm can always answer yes/no) → intractable (an algorithm exists but is too slow to run). A lot of real-world difficulty is one of these three wearing a disguise. Recognizing which wall you have hit tells you to stop searching for a clever trick and start approximating.

5Computation is physical: it costs energy

Computation feels abstract, but running it in the real world obeys thermodynamics. Landauer's principle (1961): erasing one bit of information must dissipate a minimum amount of energy as heat (about kT ln 2 joules). Logically irreversible operations - throwing information away - have an unavoidable thermodynamic price.

The bridge to the Energy substrate

This is the exact hinge between two foundations: information erasure = entropy increase = heat. It is why data centers are, physically, heat factories; why energy now gates AI (cross-ref Energy & Thermodynamics and Economics of AI); and why reversible and quantum computing are researched (compute without erasing, dodge the tax). Computation and thermodynamics are one subject seen from two sides.

Practical upshot: there is a floor on how cheap thinking - human or machine - can ever get. Intelligence is not free; it is a physical process that must be powered and cooled.

6Why we added it (honesty about the seams)

You asked the right question - "are the four substrates the only ones, and the right ones?" The honest answer was no: the four were a curated complexity-science basis, and Computation was the strongest thing missing, quietly smuggled in via Evolution ("the meta-algorithm") and Information (compression). Adding it explicitly does three things:

  • Closes the real gap - the dynamic "process" layer now has its own floor, distinct from the static "information" layer.
  • Documents the choice - a collection that names its own seams is more honest than one that hides them (cross-ref Epistemology & the Method).
  • Names the overlap instead of pretending it away - Computation partly contains Evolution and pairs with Information. The five substrates span reality well but are not perfectly orthogonal, and saying so is the point.
The test any 6th substrate must pass

Before adding another layer, it must clear five bars: universal (runs under many domains), independent (true even if the topics above never existed), generative (higher layers are its consequences), substrate-independent (same pattern across media), and orthogonal (adds something the existing five do not already cover). Computation passes the first four strongly and the fifth partially - which is exactly why it earns a place, with caveats.

7Edge & breaking cases

The lens can overreach ("computational imperialism")

"Everything is computation" is seductive and partly a choice of description. Calling a rock "computing its own trajectory" adds nothing; the lens is useful only when rules + state + iteration genuinely illuminate the system. Beware treating a metaphor as a metaphysical fact - the exact seductive-coherence trap flagged in the synthesis method.

Is the brain / mind literally a computer?

Powerful as a model, but contested. Brains are analog, embodied, and noisy; whether cognition is fully captured by computation (or needs something more) is an open question. Use it as a productive lens, not a settled verdict.

Analog, quantum, and hypercomputation

  • Analog computing uses continuous quantities, not discrete steps - practically different, but still within the computable (per Church-Turing) as far as we know.
  • Quantum computers change what is tractable (speed/complexity) for some problems, but not what is computable - they cannot solve the halting problem either. They move the P-vs-NP-style walls, not the undecidability wall.
  • Hypercomputation (machines beyond Turing) is purely theoretical and may be physically impossible.
The master caveat

Computation tells you what is possible in principle and what is walled off forever. It does not tell you it is the whole of reality. Hold it as the deepest known process-substrate - immensely generative - while staying honest that "the universe IS a computer" is a hypothesis, not a proof.

8How to use it

Treat any process - a mind, a market, a protein folding, an organization, an AI - as a computation and run four questions:

The four-question lens

1. What is the state, and what are the rules? Name the information being held and the transformations applied. If you cannot, "computation" is just a metaphor here - drop it.

2. Which wall am I hitting? Is this hard because it is intractable (too slow), undecidable (no yes/no algorithm), or uncomputable (no algorithm at all)? The answer tells you whether to optimize, approximate, or give up and measure.

3. Is generation or verification the bottleneck? If checking is far cheaper than producing (the P/NP asymmetry), build the workflow around cheap verification (cross-ref Trust).

4. What does it cost to run? Every real computation burns energy and time. Follow the Landauer/thermodynamic cost to find the true limit (cross-ref Energy).

The payoff

Most "why is this so hard?" questions resolve into which wall you have hit. Computation gives you the vocabulary to tell an engineering problem (push harder) from a provable impossibility (stop, and change the question). Knowing the difference is the whole value of the substrate.

TermMeaning

Turing machineTuring's minimal abstraction of a computer: a tape of state + a head + a rule table. Anything computable can run on it.
UniversalityThe existence of one machine that can simulate any other computer given its description as input.
Church-Turing thesisEvery effectively-calculable function is computable by a Turing machine; all reasonable models of computation are equivalent.
