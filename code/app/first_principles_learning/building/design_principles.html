Design Principles from First Principles

DRY, YAGNI, KISS, SOLID - usually taught as commandments to memorize. Here they are derived instead: every one is a consequence of a single fact - code will be changed, and change is where the cost and the bugs live. Understand the root and you never need to memorize the rules.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/building · Captured 2026-07-12 · Cluster 1 of the Builder's Atlas; builds on Coding Fundamentals

0TL;DR

  • The one axiom: software is not built once - it is changed continuously. Most cost, risk, and bugs come from changing code, not writing it. Every principle exists to make change safe and cheap.
  • DRY - one fact, one place, so a change happens once. (But do not over-apply it to things that merely look alike.)
  • KISS & YAGNI - keep it simple; don't build for imagined futures. Complexity you add speculatively is pure cost with no payoff.
  • SOLID - five rules that all reduce to: high cohesion, low coupling, so a change touches one place and cannot ripple.
  • Composition over inheritance - assemble behavior from small parts rather than deep rigid hierarchies.
  • These are heuristics, not laws. They trade off against each other; the skill is knowing which to apply when - not chanting all of them always.

1The root: you are optimizing for change, not for now

Beginners think the goal is "make it work." It is not - making it work once is easy. The real goal is make it stay easy to change, because requirements shift, bugs appear, and features get added for years. A codebase is a living thing that is edited far more than it is created.

The axiom every principle falls out of

The dominant cost of software is the cost of change, and the dominant risk is that a change breaks something far away. So the entire discipline of design is: structure code so that (a) any given change is localized to one place, and (b) changing one place cannot silently break another. Read that twice - every principle below is just a tactic for those two goals. This is high cohesion + low coupling restated as an economic argument.

2DRY - Don't Repeat Yourself

If the same piece of knowledge lives in two places, a change must be made in both - and someday you will update one and forget the other, producing a bug. So each piece of knowledge should have one authoritative home. Change happens once; drift becomes impossible.

The DRY trap: coincidental duplication

DRY is about knowledge, not text. Two pieces of code that look identical today but represent different concepts that happen to coincide should not be merged - the day one needs to change and the other does not, your "DRY" abstraction becomes a straitjacket. Wrong abstraction is more expensive than duplication. Rule of thumb: duplicate until the third occurrence makes the shared concept undeniable, then extract. (This is why over-eager DRY collides with YAGNI.)

3KISS & YAGNI - simplicity and restraint

KISS ("Keep It Simple") - the simplest solution that works is usually the best, because simplicity is low complexity, and complexity is the enemy (see the Atlas axiom). Cleverness is a cost paid by every future reader.

YAGNI ("You Aren't Gonna Need It") - do not build for hypothetical future requirements. Speculative flexibility is complexity added now for a payoff that usually never comes.

Why YAGNI is deeper than it looks

The future is uncertain (an epistemic fact). Most predicted requirements never arrive, or arrive differently than imagined. So building for them front-loads certain cost against uncertain benefit - a bad bet. Worse, speculative abstractions calcify and make the real future change harder. Build for what is real now; keep the code simple and changeable so you can adapt cheaply when the real future actually shows up. YAGNI is a bet on optionality over prediction.

4SOLID, derived (not memorized)

Five object-oriented principles. Do not memorize the acronym - watch how all five are the same two goals (localize change, prevent ripples) in five costumes:

LetterPrincipleWhat it really saysWhich goal
SSingle ResponsibilityA piece should have one reason to change. If two unrelated things can force edits here, split it.Localize change (cohesion)
OOpen/ClosedAdd new behavior by adding new code, not editing working code. Extend, don't modify.Prevent ripples
LLiskov SubstitutionA subtype must be usable anywhere its parent is, without surprises. Honor the contract.Prevent ripples (interfaces)
IInterface SegregationMany small, focused interfaces beat one fat one, so users depend only on what they use.Low coupling
DDependency InversionDepend on abstractions (promises), not concrete implementations, so the guts can swap freely.Low coupling
The compression

All of SOLID collapses to: each piece does one thing (S), you extend rather than edit (O), substitutions honor their contracts (L), dependencies are narrow (I) and point at abstractions (D). If you deeply understand cohesion, coupling, and interfaces from the previous note, you could have invented SOLID yourself. That is the whole point of first-principles learning: the rules become derivable, not memorizable.

5Composition over inheritance

Inheritance ("a Dog is-a Animal") creates deep, rigid family trees where a change to a parent silently affects every descendant - high coupling by design. Composition ("a Car has-an Engine") assembles behavior from small, swappable parts you plug together. Prefer it: composed systems are looser, more flexible, and far easier to change - which is the whole game.

6When principles collide (the real skill)

The mark of an amateur is applying every principle everywhere; the mark of a builder is knowing they trade off:

  • DRY vs YAGNI/KISS. Aggressively removing duplication adds abstraction (complexity). Sometimes a little duplication is simpler than the abstraction that would remove it. Simplicity often wins.
  • Flexibility vs simplicity. Open/Closed and Dependency Inversion add indirection to buy future flexibility - which YAGNI says you may never need. Add flexibility when the change is likely, not merely possible.
  • Abstraction vs directness. Every abstraction is a layer to learn. Past a point, "clean" architecture becomes so indirect that following one request means opening ten files. Over-engineering is as real a failure as under-engineering.
The meta-principle

Principles are heuristics that point at the goal (cheap, safe change), not the goal itself. When two collide, return to the root question: which choice makes this system easier to understand and change, given what is actually likely to happen? That judgment - not the acronyms - is the craft.

7Principles in the AI era: design becomes the bottleneck

The inversion

When code is cheap to write (AI), the constraint shifts from writing to designing and maintaining. An AI can generate a thousand lines in seconds - and a thousand lines of tangled, high-coupling code is a liability, not an asset. So in the AI era these principles matter more, not less: they are how you keep an AI-accelerated codebase from becoming an unmaintainable pile faster than ever before.

  • YAGNI vs AI's eagerness. LLMs love to add "helpful" extra abstraction and options. Ruthlessly cut what the task did not require.
  • You set the structure; AI fills it. Define the interfaces and responsibilities (S, I, D); let the AI implement inside them. Good design makes AI output better and easier to verify.
  • Review for coupling. The failure mode of AI code is hidden dependencies and duplicated knowledge. Your principles are the checklist.

8Edge & breaking cases

  • Cargo-culting. Applying a principle without knowing the complexity it fights is superstition. If you cannot name the change it makes safer, you may not need it here.
  • Premature abstraction. The most common over-engineering: building the flexible, "SOLID" version before you understand the problem. You usually need to see a thing change 2-3 times before you know the right seams.
  • Throwaway code. A one-off script or a prototype you will delete tomorrow does not need SOLID. Match the rigor to the code's expected lifespan.
  • Principles are OO-flavored. SOLID grew up in object-oriented code; the spirit (cohesion, coupling, contracts) is universal, but the literal letter maps loosely onto functional or data-oriented styles.

9How to use it

The two questions that replace all the acronyms

1. "When this changes, how many places must I edit?" More than one for a single fact → you have a DRY/cohesion problem.

2. "If I change this, what might break that I'm not looking at?" Anything → you have a coupling problem. Introduce an interface / invert the dependency.

Design for the change that is likely; keep everything else as simple as possible until reality tells you otherwise.
