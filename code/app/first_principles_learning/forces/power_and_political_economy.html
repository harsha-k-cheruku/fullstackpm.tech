Power & Political Economy from First Principles

The collection already named the distribution blind spot - who abundance reaches. This note names the deeper one: power - who steers the machine in the first place. Distribution is the output; power is the hand on the lever.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/forces · Captured 2026-07-12 · The steering layer under economics, technology, and the state

0TL;DR

  • Power is the ability to get outcomes you want - often by making others act. It is not a substance you hold but a relationship: it lives between people.
  • Power is the flip side of dependence. I have power over you to exactly the degree you need something only I control. Cut the dependence and the power evaporates.
  • Sources: control of a scarce resource, control of a chokepoint, information asymmetry, network position (hubs), legitimacy/authority, and force.
  • Power concentrates by default. Increasing returns, network effects, compounding capital, and data flywheels all reward the already-large - "the rich get richer" is structural (preferential attachment), not moral.
  • Countervailing forces push back: competition, regulation/antitrust, decentralizing tech, and revolution/exit - an endless pendulum between concentration and dispersal.
  • Three ways to coordinate humans: the state (hierarchy/authority), the market (price/exchange), and the firm (managed hierarchy inside the market). Coase: firms exist to dodge transaction costs.
  • Data and compute are the new power base. Whoever owns the scarce inputs - chips, energy, data, models - owns leverage over everyone downstream. That is why the AI age concentrates power.
  • Use it: follow the dependence and the chokepoint to find who really has power; ask cui bono (who benefits); and never let yourself depend on a single chokepoint you do not own.

1What power fundamentally is

Strip away the flags, titles, and money and power reduces to one thing: the ability to bring about the outcomes you want - frequently by getting other people to act as you wish, even against their own preference. Max Weber's classic line: power is the probability that an actor can carry out their will despite resistance.

The crucial move is to stop thinking of power as a possession ("he has power") and start thinking of it as a relationship. Power always exists between parties, and its size is set by the weaker party's alternatives, not the stronger party's strength.

Power is the flip side of dependence

Here is the whole subject in one sentence: I have power over you to exactly the degree that you need something only I control. Power and dependence are the same relationship read from opposite ends. The boss has power over the worker who has no other income; the worker with three job offers has stripped that power away. The drug dealer has power over the addict; the sober person walks past. To find power, find the dependence. To destroy power, kill the dependence - build alternatives, substitutes, or exits.

This reframing is liberating and diagnostic. It tells you that power is rarely about brute capacity and almost always about the structure of options around a relationship. The monopolist is powerful not because it is large but because you have nowhere else to go. Remove the "nowhere else" and the largeness stops mattering.

It also explains why power is often invisible until tested. A dependence you never notice - the one supplier, the one platform, the one protocol - confers quiet, standing power that only becomes obvious the day it is used against you.

2Sources of power

If power is control over something others depend on, then the sources of power are just the different things people can be made to depend on. There are six recurring ones.

SourceThe dependence it exploitsExample
Scarce resourceYou need the thing; only they have enough of itOil, water, land, capital, rare talent
Chokepoint / bottleneckEverything must pass through the one narrow point they holdA canal, a payment rail, an app store, a key patent
Information asymmetryThey know something you need to know and you cannot verify it yourselfThe expert, the insider, the platform that sees all the data
Network positionThey sit where the connections converge; routing runs through themA hub, a broker, a market maker, a standard
Legitimacy / authorityYou obey because you accept their right to commandElected office, a judge, a respected brand, a religion
ForceThey can impose costs on you directly if you refuseArmies, police, the ability to fire, to sanction, to exclude

Network position deserves special emphasis because it is the most underrated. In Networks & Emergence we saw that scale-free networks grow a small number of dominant hubs. A hub does not need to own a scarce resource or wield force - its power comes purely from where it sits. Everyone must route through it, so it can tax, filter, or cut off flow. The broker between two groups that cannot reach each other directly holds power by structure alone; this is why intermediaries and platforms are so durably powerful.

Legitimacy is the cheapest power

Force works but is expensive, unstable, and breeds resistance - you must guard every subject. Legitimacy is force made unnecessary: when people accept your right to command, they police themselves and obedience becomes nearly free. That is why every durable power converts raw dominance into accepted authority as fast as it can - a conqueror crowns himself king, a monopoly rebrands as a public utility, a founder becomes a "visionary." Legitimacy is force that no longer has to show up. When it erodes, the naked force underneath suddenly has to do all the work again - and usually cannot.

Real power almost always stacks several sources. A state fuses force, legitimacy, and control of chokepoints (borders, courts, currency). A dominant platform fuses network position, information asymmetry, and scarce data. The more independent sources one actor stacks, the harder they are to dislodge, because you must break every one.

3Power concentrates

Left alone, power does not stay spread out - it clumps. This is not primarily a story about greed; it is a story about feedback loops. Several distinct mechanisms all point the same direction: toward the already-large.

MechanismWhy it rewards the already-large
Increasing returns to scaleBigger means cheaper per unit; the low-cost giant undercuts and absorbs rivals.
Network effectsEach new user makes the product more valuable, pulling in the next user - a self-feeding pull toward one winner.
Compounding capitalReturns on capital accrue to those who already have capital; wealth grows multiplicatively (see r > g).
Data flywheelsMore users produce more data, which trains better models, which win more users - a loop that only the leader can spin fast.
Preferential attachment: why the rich get richer

All four mechanisms are instances of one deep pattern from network science: preferential attachment. New links attach preferentially to nodes that already have many links - the popular get more popular because they are already popular. This is the exact engine that produces scale-free networks and their dominant hubs in Networks & Emergence, and it is why "the rich get richer" is a structural outcome, not a moral one. Absent friction, any advantage compounds into dominance. The tail is fat (see Risk, Uncertainty & the Tails) precisely because power obeys a power law.

In business, the durable version of this concentration is a moat - a structural advantage that protects returns from competition (see the ../businesses/ notes on moats). Network effects, economies of scale, switching costs, and proprietary data are all moats and all forms of preferential attachment. The concept is identical; only the vocabulary changes across disciplines.

In the AI economy (see ../economics-of-ai/), the data flywheel is the sharpest version yet: the leading model attracts the most usage, usage generates the most data and revenue, which funds the most compute, which trains the best next model. Each turn widens the gap. This is why AI markets tend toward a handful of frontier players rather than a long, flat field of equals.

4The countervailing forces

If concentration were the only force, everything would have collapsed into a single owner long ago. It has not, because concentration provokes its own opposition. Four forces push back toward dispersal.

  • Competition - the most basic dispersing force. High profits attract entrants; entrants erode the incumbent's returns. Concentration is self-limiting if entry stays possible - which is exactly what moats and chokepoints are designed to prevent.
  • Regulation / antitrust - the state deliberately breaking up or constraining concentrated private power. Antitrust exists precisely because markets do not reliably self-correct once a moat is deep enough. It is society overriding preferential attachment by force of law.
  • Decentralizing technology - new tech that removes a chokepoint or cheapens a scarce input. The printing press broke the clergy's monopoly on scripture; the internet broke gatekept distribution; open-source models can erode the data-and-compute moat. Technology giveth concentration and technology taketh it away.
  • Revolution / exit - when the dependence becomes intolerable and legitimacy collapses, the dominated either overthrow the powerful (voice pushed to its limit) or leave for an alternative (exit). Both destroy power by destroying the dependence it rested on.
The pendulum, not the destination

There is no stable end state. Concentration and dispersal are locked in an endless pendulum: advantage compounds until it provokes competition, regulation, disruptive tech, or revolt; those forces disperse power until a new advantage begins compounding again. History is not a march toward monopoly or toward equality - it is this oscillation. Carlota Perez's technological surges show the rhythm: a new technology installs by concentrating vast power in a few pioneers, then diffuses as it is regulated, standardized, and commoditized - only for the next technology to restart the cycle. The question is never "will power concentrate?" but "where are we in the swing?"

5State vs market vs firm

Step back from who has power to how humans coordinate at all. Getting millions of strangers to cooperate on complex tasks is not automatic - and there are essentially three mechanisms for it, each a different way of allocating the power to decide.

MechanismCoordinates byPower lives inGood at
StateHierarchy / authority - command backed by force and legitimacyOffice and lawPublic goods, rules, defense, things markets under-supply
MarketPrice / voluntary exchange - decentralized signals, no one in chargeNobody (dispersed)Allocating scarce goods, discovering value, innovation
FirmManaged hierarchy inside the market - command within, exchange withoutOwnership and managementTasks too costly to coordinate by repeated market transactions

The state and the market are the two poles - pure hierarchy versus pure exchange. The firm is the fascinating hybrid: a little island of central planning floating in the market's ocean of prices. Inside a company, you do not negotiate a price with a colleague to get a report; a boss simply directs it. Why do these islands of authority exist at all inside a price system?

Coase's theory of the firm

Ronald Coase's 1937 answer: firms exist to avoid transaction costs. Using the market is not free - you must search for partners, negotiate, write contracts, monitor, and enforce. When those costs of coordinating by repeated market transactions are high, it is cheaper to pull the activity inside a firm and coordinate it by command instead. So the boundary of the firm sits exactly where the two costs balance: do it in-house until in-house coordination costs more than buying on the market, then buy. This single idea explains make-vs-buy, outsourcing, vertical integration, and why firms grow to a size and then stop.

The same logic answers the bigger question of why some things are states, some markets, and some companies. It is a governance choice about which coordination mechanism has the lowest total cost for a given task. National defense has enormous free-rider and transaction-cost problems, so it lands in the state. Groceries coordinate cheaply through prices, so they land in the market. Building an aircraft requires tight, ongoing coordination that prices handle badly, so it lands inside firms. When technology lowers transaction costs (platforms, smart contracts, marketplaces), activities migrate out of firms and back into markets - which is why gig platforms can replace in-house workforces. The boundaries are not fixed; they move as coordination costs move.

6Data & compute as the new power base

Every era has a defining scarce input that confers power over everything downstream: land in the agrarian age, capital and machines in the industrial age. In the current age it is data, models, and compute. The party that owns these owns leverage over everyone who must build on top of them.

The logic is pure Section 1 - power as dependence. If your business, your research, or your government service needs a capability that only a handful of actors can provide (a frontier model, a hyperscale data center, a unique dataset), then those actors have power over you, whether or not they ever choose to use it. They set the price, the terms, the access, and the rules - and they can revoke them.

The new stack of chokepoints

The AI supply chain is a ladder of dependencies, and whoever controls a rung taxes everyone above it:

Data (the fuel) -> Compute (the engine) -> Models (the product) -> Applications (the surface everyone sees).

Most attention goes to the flashy top of the ladder, but the durable power sits at the bottom rungs - the scarce, capital-intensive, hard-to-replicate ones. This is why the data flywheel of Section 3 is so decisive: it is preferential attachment applied to the single most strategic input of the age. See ../economics-of-ai/ for the economics of that flywheel.

There is also a subtler power here: control of information means control of what others can verify. As Trust & Verification argues, when producing convincing information becomes cheap, the ability to verify becomes the scarce, valuable thing. Whoever owns the model owns the default account of reality that flows to millions - a profound information asymmetry (Section 2) operating at civilizational scale. Power over data is power over belief, and power over belief is the softest, deepest power of all.

7Geopolitics of compute

Because compute is now a strategic input, its supply chain has become a geopolitical battlefield. The same first principle - control a chokepoint others depend on - now operates between nations, not just firms.

Three inputs have emerged as the strategic chokepoints of the AI age:

  • Chips - the most acute chokepoint. Advanced AI chips depend on an astonishingly narrow supply chain: a handful of designers, one dominant fabricator, and a single company that makes the extreme-ultraviolet lithography machines the fabs require. Each of those is a global bottleneck of exactly the kind in Section 2.
  • Energy - compute at scale is bounded by power. Data centers need gigawatts, and the ability to generate and deliver cheap, reliable electricity becomes a hard constraint on how much intelligence a nation can produce. Energy is upstream of compute.
  • Talent - the small pool of people who can build frontier systems. Talent is mobile, which makes it both a chokepoint and a target of competition; nations compete to attract and retain it.
Export controls and sovereignty

When a nation controls a chokepoint, it can weaponize the dependence - this is exactly what export controls on advanced chips are: using control of a bottleneck to deny a rival the input it needs. The rival's response is predictably Section 4: build a domestic alternative to remove the dependence. This is the drive for compute sovereignty - the doctrine that a nation must own its critical AI inputs rather than depend on a potential adversary for them, the modern echo of controlling your own oil or food supply. The whole contest is Section 1 played at the level of states: everyone racing to hold chokepoints others need while eliminating their own dependence on chokepoints others hold.

8Edge & breaking cases

Power's failure modes are where the clean model gets ugly - and they are remarkably consistent across states, firms, and markets.

  • Power corrupts (the principal-agent problem) - whenever someone (an agent) acts on behalf of someone else (a principal), the agent has power and their own interests, which diverge from the principal's. Managers enrich themselves at shareholders' expense; officials serve donors over citizens. Delegated power reliably drifts toward the agent's benefit unless actively checked, because the agent controls the information and the levers.
  • Rent-seeking - using power to capture a larger slice of existing wealth rather than to create new wealth. Lobbying for a subsidy, erecting a licensing barrier, or extracting a toll adds nothing to the pie; it just redirects it. Rent-seeking is the parasitic use of power, and it is often more profitable than production, which is why the powerful drift toward it.
  • Regulatory capture - the referee bought by a player. Regulation (Section 4) is supposed to check concentrated power, but the regulated industry has the most money, information, and focus, so over time it captures its own regulator - writing the rules, staffing the agency, defining "safe." The countervailing force gets quietly converted into a moat that entrenches the incumbent it was meant to constrain. Watch for incumbents that welcome regulation: it often means the rules will burden entrants more than themselves.
  • Too-big dynamics - past a point, an actor becomes so central that its failure threatens the whole system ("too big to fail"), so it can privatize gains and socialize losses. Size buys not just market power but a public backstop, which further entrenches it - concentration feeding on the fear of its own collapse.
The deepest trade-off: efficiency vs resilience

Underneath all of these is one unavoidable tension. Concentration is efficient - one big optimized actor, no duplication, maximal economies of scale. Dispersal is resilient and fair - many actors, no single point of failure, no one able to dominate. You cannot maximize both. A concentrated system is a highly optimized machine with a single point of failure and a single hand on the lever; a dispersed one is messier, slower, and far harder to break or capture. Every real choice about power - antitrust, platform design, supply chains, governance - is really a choice about where to sit on this dial. There is no free lunch, only a trade you must make consciously rather than by default.

9How to use it

The point of a power lens is to see the levers that titles and press releases hide. Three habits do most of the work.

Three moves for reading any situation

1. Follow the dependence to find who really has power. Ignore titles and org charts. Ask: what does each party need, and who controls the scarce version of it? Trace the chokepoint - the one supplier, the one platform, the one approval - because that is where power actually sits, regardless of who appears to be in charge.

2. Ask cui bono - who benefits? When a rule, a narrative, or a "neutral" standard appears, ask who gains from it. Power is usually exercised through arrangements that look natural or fair. Following the benefit reveals the hand behind the design (and exposes rent-seeking and capture).

3. Own the chokepoint, or avoid depending on one. Strategically, there are only two safe positions: be the chokepoint others depend on, or make sure you never depend on a single chokepoint you do not control. Cultivate alternatives, second sources, and exits - multi-home, keep your data portable, hold a credible walk-away. Optionality (from Risk & the Tails) is not just financial; it is the antidote to someone else's power over you.

Run these together and a scene resolves quickly: the dependence shows you who holds the lever, cui bono shows you who designed the room, and the chokepoint rule tells you what to do about your own exposure. None of it requires knowing anyone's intentions - power is structural, so you read it from the structure.
