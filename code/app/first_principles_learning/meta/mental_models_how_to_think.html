Mental Models & How to Think

Every other note in this collection is a model of some slice of the world. This one is the note about the notes: what a model actually is, how to hold many of them at once, and how to pick the right one at the right moment - the practical operating system for thinking, and an index into everything else here.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/meta · Captured 2026-07-12 · The how-to-think companion that ties the whole collection together

0TL;DR

  • A mental model is a compression: a lossy, reusable representation of how some part of the world works. You already think through models whether you name them or not - the only choice is whether they are good ones, held consciously.
  • No single model is enough. Wisdom is a latticework (Munger) of many models drawn from many disciplines. The man with only a hammer sees nails everywhere; the skill is knowing WHICH model to apply WHEN.
  • Invert, always invert. Solving a problem backwards - listing the ways to fail and avoiding them - is often easier and more reliable than chasing brilliance. Avoiding stupidity beats seeking cleverness.
  • Follow the incentives. "Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome." Incentives are the single most reliable predictor of behavior - and when the measure becomes the target, the measure breaks (Goodhart).
  • Watch the biases. Confirmation, availability, sunk cost, anchoring, Dunning-Kruger, and motivated reasoning quietly corrupt every model you run. Each has a known counter.
  • Models are maps, not the territory. A model that explains everything explains nothing. Hold them loosely, match them to the problem, and slow down on the one-way doors.

1What a mental model is

A mental model is a compressed representation of how some part of the world works. It throws away almost all the detail and keeps only the structure you need to predict, decide, or explain. "Supply and demand," "compound interest," "natural selection," "incentives" - each is a tiny portable machine you carry in your head and point at reality.

A model is a compression

This is not a metaphor - it is literally the same operation as in Information Theory. A good model, like a good compression, discards the noise and keeps the signal: it is small, reusable, and lets you reconstruct or anticipate a large space of situations from a few rules. And like all lossy compression, it necessarily throws away real detail - which is exactly why every model is wrong in some regime and useful in another. The art is knowing what got compressed out.

The crucial point: you think through models whether you know it or not. There is no model-free perception of a complex world - your brain is a prediction engine running compressed representations constantly. The only question is whether your models are (a) good compressions of reality, and (b) consciously chosen rather than absorbed by accident from whoever shouted loudest. Making them explicit is the whole game: named models can be inspected, compared, and swapped; invisible ones just run you.

2The latticework idea (Munger)

Charlie Munger's central claim about thinking: you cannot get by on one model, or even the handful your profession happens to teach you. Wisdom is a latticework of many models from many disciplines, hung together so that a fact learned in physics reinforces one in biology reinforces one in economics.

The man with only a hammer

"To the man with only a hammer, every problem looks like a nail." A person who knows only one model - only economics, or only psychology, or only engineering - will force every situation into that single frame, and be confidently wrong whenever the situation belongs to a different discipline. The specialist's greatest strength becomes their blind spot. The fix is not depth alone but breadth held in tension: enough big models from enough fields that the right one is available when the problem calls for it.

Two skills define the latticework thinker, and the second is harder than the first:

  1. Acquisition - collect the big, load-bearing models from the major disciplines (the ones that come up again and again). This collection is, in effect, one attempt at that acquisition.
  2. Selection - know which model to apply when. A room full of models is useless if you reach for the wrong one; the master skill is matching the model to the shape of the problem in front of you, and often running several at once to triangulate.

Why "latticework" and not "list"? Because the models cross-brace each other. Second-order thinking sharpens your use of incentives; ergodicity reshapes how you read expected value; evolution and network effects turn out to be the same selection-plus-feedback structure wearing different clothes. The models compound - which is exactly why this note doubles as an index into the rest of the collection.

4Inversion, deep

"Invert, always invert" - the mathematician Carl Jacobi's advice, adopted by Munger as a core discipline. Many problems that are hard to solve forwards become easy backwards. Instead of asking "how do I succeed?", ask "how would I guarantee failure?" - then simply refuse to do those things.

Avoid stupidity, don't seek brilliance

Munger's version: "It is remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent." Forward-thinking chases the small, uncertain set of brilliant moves; inversion eliminates the large, knowable set of dumb ones. You almost always know more about what will ruin a plan than about what will make it soar - so subtract the failure modes first. Want a happy marriage? List everything that reliably destroys marriages and don't do it. Want a healthy portfolio? Enumerate the ways investors blow up, and avoid each.

Inversion is the thinking-toolkit sibling of via negativa from Risk, Uncertainty & the Tails: improvement by removal. Under uncertainty, cutting sources of fragility is more robust than adding cleverness, precisely because failure modes are more knowable and more stable than success recipes. Inversion also pairs with pre-mortems (Section 7): before you start, assume the plan already failed and ask why - then fix those causes in advance.

5Incentives

If you keep only one model from this note, keep this one. "Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome" (Munger). Incentives are the single most reliable predictor of behavior - more reliable than stated intentions, values, or org charts. When you cannot understand why a person, company, or system behaves as it does, you have almost always failed to find the incentive.

Follow the payoff

People, firms, and even algorithms flow toward what is rewarded and away from what is punished - often unconsciously, often against their own stated beliefs. This is why "incentive-caused bias" is so dangerous: a smart, honest person will genuinely believe the conclusion that pays them, without any conscious dishonesty. Before you diagnose stupidity or malice (see Hanlon, Section 6), check the incentive structure. Change the payoffs and the behavior follows; leave the payoffs and the behavior returns no matter how many memos you write.

Incentives are the engine underneath much of the collection. They drive the competitive races in Game Theory & Moloch, where individually rational responses to incentives produce collectively terrible equilibria. And they carry a built-in failure mode - Goodhart's Law: "when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Optimize a proxy hard enough and the system games the proxy while the real goal rots. This is the beating heart of the alignment problem in AI from First Principles: an optimizer given the wrong incentive will pursue it with terrifying literalness.

6The biases that corrupt thinking

Every model you run executes on flawed hardware. Cognitive biases are systematic errors in how the mind processes evidence - and they do not go away when you learn about them, so the defense is a set of concrete counters you build into your process. A short field guide to the worst offenders:

BiasWhat it doesThe counter
Confirmation biasYou seek and favor evidence that fits what you already believe.Actively hunt disconfirmation; ask "what would change my mind?" before looking.
Availability biasYou judge likelihood by how easily examples come to mind, not by base rates.Reach for the actual base rate; distrust the vivid, recent, or dramatic case.
Sunk cost fallacyYou keep pouring in because of what you already spent, not future value.Ask only "given where I am now, what is the best move?" Past cost is gone.
AnchoringThe first number or frame you see drags all later estimates toward it.Estimate independently first; consider multiple starting points; question the anchor.
Dunning-KrugerThe less you know, the more you overrate how much you know.Seek expert critique; map the edges of your competence; assume unknown unknowns.
Motivated reasoningYou reason toward the conclusion you want, then rationalize it.Check the incentive (Section 5); argue the other side; separate "want" from "is."

Two razors belong in this section as first-pass filters for interpreting others:

  • Occam's razor - among competing explanations, prefer the one requiring the fewest assumptions. Not "the simplest is always true," but "start there, and demand extra evidence for extra complexity."
  • Hanlon's razor - "never attribute to malice what is adequately explained by incompetence" (and, we'd add, by incentives). Most apparent conspiracies are just uncoordinated people responding to their payoffs.

7How to actually get smarter

Knowing about models and biases changes almost nothing on its own - insight does not survive contact with a busy day. What works is process: small, repeatable habits that force better thinking whether or not you feel like it.

The practices that compound

Decision journals. Before a real decision, write down what you expect to happen, why, and how confident you are. Later, compare. This is the only cure for hindsight bias - it freezes what you actually believed so you can calibrate over time.

Pre-mortems. Assume the project has already failed, then ask "why?" Inversion applied to planning - it surfaces the failure modes while you can still prevent them, and gives quiet doubters permission to speak.

Steelmanning. Before attacking an opposing view, state its strongest version - stronger than its own advocates would. If you can only beat the weak version (a straw man), you have learned nothing.

Thinking in bets. Reframe beliefs as wagers with odds: "how sure am I, and what would I stake?" This converts fuzzy conviction into calibrated probability and separates decision quality from outcome luck.

Surround ideas with disconfirmation. Deliberately seek the evidence and people most likely to prove you wrong. Comfort is the enemy of calibration.

One-way vs two-way doors

Match your rigor to reversibility. Most decisions are two-way doors - cheap to reverse, so decide fast and learn from the result; agonizing over them wastes your scarcest resource. A few are one-way doors - hard or impossible to undo (quitting, selling, launching publicly, anything with ruin potential). Slow down on those: gather more information, run a pre-mortem, demand a margin of safety. The classic error is spending one-way-door caution on two-way-door decisions and vice versa. Speed on the reversible; patience on the irreversible.

8The reflexive warning

A note about mental models has to turn its own tools on itself, or it fails its own test. The most important thing to know about models is their limit.

The map is not the territory

Every model in this collection is a map - a compressed, lossy, deliberately incomplete representation. It is never the territory itself. When the map and the ground disagree, the ground wins, every time. The failure mode is falling in love with an elegant model and then editing your perception of reality to fit it, rather than the reverse. This is the through-line back to Epistemology & First Principles: knowledge is provisional, and a model earns its keep only by predicting, never by being pretty.

Two corollaries follow, and they are what keep a latticework from becoming a cage:

  • A model that explains everything explains nothing. If your favorite framework can absorb any fact - if no observation could ever count against it - it has stopped being a model and become a faith. Unfalsifiable is not the same as true; it is the same as empty. The best models are the ones that stick their necks out and could be wrong.
  • Hold them loosely. Use models as lenses to try on, not lenses fused to your eyes. Reach for several, notice where they disagree, and let the disagreement teach you. The goal is not to be a hedgehog who knows one big thing but a fox who holds many and swaps them fluidly. Your attachment should be to reality, not to any map of it.

9How to use it

A standing routine that turns this whole collection into a working thinking practice:

Five moves to run

1. Build the latticework. Keep acquiring the big models from many disciplines - use the toolkit table in Section 3 as your acquisition map, and go deep in the linked notes.

2. Match the model to the problem. When a problem lands, ask what shape it has and which one-liner fits. Run two or three models, not one; let their disagreement flag where you're unsure.

3. Invert. Ask "how would this fail?" and remove the failure modes first. Avoiding stupidity beats chasing brilliance - and it's easier.

4. Follow the incentives. Before diagnosing malice or stupidity, find the payoff structure. To change behavior, change the incentives - and watch for Goodhart when you set a target.

5. Keep a decision journal. Write predictions before outcomes, slow down on one-way doors, build a margin of safety, and calibrate over time. Process beats resolutions.

Notice the shape: acquire broadly, apply selectively, invert to protect the downside, follow incentives to predict behavior, and journal to close the feedback loop on your own thinking. None of it requires being the smartest person in the room - which, fittingly, is the entire point.
