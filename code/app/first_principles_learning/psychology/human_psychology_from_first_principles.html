Human Psychology from First Principles

The irreducible facts about the human mind, and the biases and behaviors that fall out of them.

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/psychology · Captured 2026-07-11 · Companions: ../llms/ and ../agents/

0TL;DR

  • The mind is an energy-conserving prediction engine, evolved to keep a social animal alive and reproducing - not to find truth or be happy.
  • It runs mostly on fast emotional shortcuts; deliberate reason is the expensive exception.
  • It treats belonging as survival, fears loss more than it loves gain, and rewrites its own story to stay coherent.
  • Most "irrational" behavior is one of seven axioms doing exactly its job in the wrong (modern) context.

1Caveat: this bedrock is softer than math

Unlike LLMs (which rest on mathematical axioms), psychology's foundations are softer and more contested. There is no universal-approximation-theorem-level certainty here. Treat the axioms below as "the best current bedrock" - well-supported and highly generative - not eternal proof.

2The 7 axioms

  1. The brain is an evolved survival-and-reproduction organ, not a truth-seeking one. Every drive exists because it helped ancestors pass on genes. The mind optimizes for fitness, not accuracy or happiness. (The ultimate "why" behind everything.)
  2. Energy and attention are scarce, so the brain is a miser. Thinking is metabolically expensive; the default is the cheapest option - habits, shortcuts, snap judgments. Effortful reasoning is the exception.
  3. The brain is a prediction machine. It predicts incoming sensation and attends mostly to the error (surprise). Experience is a controlled hallucination corrected by the senses - perception is constructed, not recorded.
  4. Humans are obligately social. Exclusion from the group meant death, so belonging, status, and reputation are treated as near-survival needs. Social pain runs on circuitry overlapping physical pain.
  5. Emotion is older and faster than reason, and it steers. Feelings are fast, pre-computed action signals (approach / avoid / freeze). Reason mostly arrives after, to justify. Emotion decides; logic explains.
  6. Behavior is driven by expected reward and the avoidance of loss - and loss looms larger. The brain runs on a reward-prediction signal (dopamine tracks surprise in reward, not reward itself). A loss hurts about twice as much as an equal gain feels good.
  7. The self is a story the brain maintains for coherence. We are driven to keep self-image and beliefs consistent; contradiction is aversive (cognitive dissonance), so we rationalize rather than re-examine.

3The mechanisms that fall out

Nearly every famous "cognitive bias" is one of the axioms turned into behavior:

AxiomMechanism it produces
1 - evolved for fitnessCravings for sugar / status / novelty that misfire in the modern world
2 - brain is a miserSystem 1 / System 2, heuristics, habits, stereotyping
3 - prediction machineConfirmation bias, optical illusions, expectation shaping experience
4 - obligately socialConformity, tribalism, shame, need for approval, in-group / out-group
5 - emotion firstMotivated reasoning, gut decisions, flooding under stress
6 - reward & loss aversionProcrastination, addiction, endowment effect, status-quo bias
7 - coherent self-storyCognitive dissonance, rationalization, sunk-cost fallacy

4The synthesis

One line

The human mind is an energy-conserving prediction engine, evolved to keep a social animal alive and reproducing - so it runs mostly on fast emotional shortcuts, treats belonging as survival, fears loss more than it loves gain, and rewrites its own story to stay coherent. "Rational" is the expensive exception it uses sparingly.

5Why it is useful (the payoff)

  • Persuasion / coaching: you do not win by being right (that fights axioms 5 and 7). You win by making the desired behavior low-effort (2), socially rewarded (4), and loss-framed (6) - and by letting people keep their self-story intact.
  • Self-understanding: most of your "irrational" moments are one of these seven axioms doing its job in the wrong context.
  • Designing for humans (a deck, a tool, an org): reduce cognitive load, provide social proof, frame around avoiding loss, and never force someone to publicly contradict their identity.

6The symmetry with LLMs

A neat parallel

Both an LLM and a human brain are prediction engines optimizing a proxy (likely / fitness) rather than truth. That is exactly why both hallucinate, both are steerable by framing, and both need external verification. Understanding one deepens the other.
