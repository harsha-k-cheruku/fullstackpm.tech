Cost Collapse & the Death of Friction

The deepest statement of what technology is: it drives the marginal cost of a fundamental input to zero - and the felt result is "everything, to everyone, everywhere, all the time."

Topic folder: first-principles-tutor/how-tech-changes-the-world · Captured 2026-07-11 · Unifies ../economics-of-ai/, ../future-of-work/, and the human-needs overlay

0TL;DR

  • The engine: an explosion happens when the marginal cost of a fundamental input collapses toward zero - things that were uneconomic don't just get cheaper, they become free, and whole new categories appear.
  • The digital era rode three curves at once: compute + storage + bandwidth (cloud needed all three + virtualization - not just "infrastructure").
  • The biggest ones are General Purpose Technologies (steam -> electricity -> computing -> internet -> AI); each makes one fundamental input abundant.
  • The felt result is the death of friction: technology drives space, time, scarcity, and skill toward zero -> "make [X] available to everyone, everywhere, all the time."
  • Cost collapse (supply side) and friction death (demand side) are the same machine seen from two ends.
  • Scarcity never dies - it migrates up to attention, trust, judgment, and energy. That is where the durable value goes.

1The engine: cost collapse

The core principle

An explosion happens when the marginal cost of a fundamental input collapses toward zero - because then things that were absurdly uneconomic don't merely get cheaper, they become effectively free, and entire categories that were impossible suddenly exist.

This is the difference between "a cheaper version of the old thing" and "a new thing." Nobody would photograph their lunch and mail it to 200 people when film + postage cost $20 each. At zero marginal cost, Instagram exists. The threshold-crossing - cost passing from "expensive" through "cheap" to "free" - is what turns a smooth curve into a discontinuous explosion. It is the sharpened form of the "cost collapse" catalyst from Why Companies Are Born and Die.

2The three curves of the digital era

The internet economy did not have one catalyst - it had a trinity of exponential cost curves falling together for ~50 years. You need all of them for a category to ignite.

InputThe curveUnlocked
ComputeMoore's Law (cost per FLOP falls)Everything computational
StorageKryder's Law (cost per GB falls)Databases, photos, "store everything"
BandwidthFiber / Nielsen's Law (cost per bit falls)Cloud, streaming, real-time everything
Why cloud needed all three (the correction)

Cloud/AWS is often mis-filed as "infrastructure critical mass." In truth it needed compute abundance AND the bandwidth collapse (renting a remote computer only makes sense if moving bytes to it is nearly free) AND virtualization (software slicing one machine into many rentable units). Streaming needed bandwidth + storage + compute. Social needed all three + the smartphone. Miss one leg and the model stays uneconomic - most big births are multi-catalyst.

3The GPT lineage (each abundance removes a constraint)

The biggest cost collapses are what economists call General Purpose Technologies: pervasive (spread to every sector), self-improving (cheaper/better for decades), and complement-spawning (trigger thousands of downstream inventions). The lineage - and the fundamental input each made abundant:

GPTMade abundant
SteamPortable mechanical power
ElectricityClean, distributable energy
Internal combustionCheap mobility of people/goods
ComputingCalculation / information processing
InternetDistribution / communication (bandwidth)
AICognition / intelligence
Why AI qualifies - with an asterisk

AI clears the GPT bar: it is a cost collapse in a fundamental input (cognition), it is pervasive, self-improving, and complement-spawning. "Intelligence too cheap to meter" rhymes with "electricity too cheap to meter." BUT: AI is a child of the last explosion, not a peer - it is built on the compute/storage/bandwidth trinity. It is best read as the killer app of abundant compute, i.e. the next layer: last era's mechanism (cheap compute) became this era's axiom (cheap cognition). That is the meta-principle in action.

4The productivity lag (the electricity lesson)

The most-missed part of the electricity story: it was invented in the 1880s but the productivity boom did not arrive until the 1920s - a ~40-year lag. Factories first just bolted electric motors onto their steam-era layouts (one giant central motor, overhead shafts). The payoff only came when they redesigned the factory around electricity: small unit motors at each station, enabling the assembly line.

The productivity paradox

A GPT's payoff lags because you must reinvent the workflow around it, not bolt it on (Paul David, "The Dynamo and the Computer"; Solow: "computers everywhere except in the productivity statistics"). We are now in the "bolt a chatbot onto the old workflow" phase of AI. The real explosion comes when work is redesigned around agents - which is exactly the Future of Work thesis. Expect a frustrating gap between capability and measured productivity.

5The felt result: the death of friction

Turn the cost collapse around and look at it from the human's side. What zero marginal cost feels like is the removal of friction between a person and what they want. Strip any technology to its essence and it is killing one of just four frictions:

FrictionCollapses toFelt result
Space (distance)everywherelocation stops mattering
Time (delay/latency)all the time / instantwaiting disappears
Scarcity (access/cost/quantity)to everyone / abundantgatekeeping disappears
Skill (capability/expertise)by anyone / democratizedyou no longer need to be a specialist
The one-line template

Every technology is some version of: make [X] available / accessible to everyone, everywhere, all the time. "Everyone/everywhere/all-the-time" is just the four frictions hitting zero. "[X]" is whatever fundamental thing is being freed.

"Make everything available to everyone" and "make everyone accessible to everyone" are the first two rows of a longer list. Each row is one X with its frictions collapsing:

The subtle one: broadcast

Row 8 flips row 2. Technology made you not just reachable by everyone but a potential broadcaster to everyone - it collapsed the friction of distribution. That is why "everyone is a media company now" and why the creator economy exists. The skill row (capability/tools) is the other quietly huge one: democratizing production, not just consumption.

7Two views, one machine

The unification

Cost collapse and the death of friction are the same phenomenon seen from two sides.

Supply side: the marginal cost of a fundamental input collapses toward zero (the GPT/curve view).

Demand / experience side: the felt result of zero marginal cost is "available to everyone, everywhere, all the time."

When it costs ~nothing to copy / move / serve one more unit, the natural equilibrium is universal + instant + everywhere access. Cost collapse is the engine; "everything to everyone all the time" is what it feels like from the ground.

8Scarcity migrates up (the deepest consequence)

Scarcity does not die when a friction collapses - it relocates to a higher layer:

  • When everything is available -> the scarce thing becomes attention (you can't consume it all).
  • When everyone is accessible -> the scarce thing becomes genuine trust / connection (noise drowns signal).
  • When all information is instant -> the scarce thing becomes judgment / curation (what's true? what matters?).
  • When cognition is cheap -> the scarce thing becomes verification (likely is not true) and energy (the physical substrate).
Why this closes the whole collection

This is why aggregators (who filter abundance into attention) win, and why the invariants - trust, verification, taste, presence - are the durable human premium (Future of Work). Every friction you kill hands value to the layer above it. Build where scarcity is migrating TO, not where it is collapsing FROM.

9What resists collapse - and the shadow side

Frictions that refuse to fall

  • Physical presence (atoms) - a massage, surgery, a hug. Bits are free; atoms are not.
  • Verification / trust - AI's abundance is plausibility, not truth; someone must still certify.
  • Energy - the physical substrate under all of it (see the full-circle below).
  • Positional goods (status) - the deep one: status cannot be made available to everyone without ceasing to exist. The moment everyone has the blue check / degree / luxury bag, it stops signaling. Technology can democratize the imitation of status but never status itself. The status spanner is definitionally immune to the "everyone" collapse.
The shadow side: some frictions were load-bearing

The machine is indiscriminate - it removes good frictions too. "Everyone accessible to everyone all the time" also equals harassment, surveillance, misinformation at light-speed, addiction, and the death of privacy and deliberation. Privacy was friction-to-access. Considered debate was friction-to-reaction. Editorial gatekeeping was friction-to-garbage. Removing them has real costs - a maturity point most techno-optimism skips.

The full circle: the first GPT now gates the latest

AI's explosion is currently bottlenecked by - electricity. Datacenter power, grid capacity, and energy cost are the hard ceiling on scaling AI compute. The oldest abundance (energy) has become the scarcity gating the newest (cognition). The arrow is not a clean ladder - it loops: each GPT frees an input, that abundance unlocks the next GPT, and the new frontier eventually collides with an old constraint. Cognition got cheap; now we rediscover that energy is the real substrate - which is why smart money in the AI era flows into power generation, not just chips.

10Using it to forecast the next explosion

The collapse is roughly monotonic - we rarely re-add friction voluntarily. So this becomes a forecasting tool:

The forecasting move

To find the next explosion, hunt for the remaining friction. Whatever is still local, slow, scarce, or skill-gated is the next candidate for collapse. Right now that points at: physical services (skill-gated, being attacked by AI = services-as-software), care (local + trust), verification/trust (the new scarcity), and energy (the substrate). Combine with the birth test: which friction is about to fall, via which catalyst, and can the incumbent survive it?
