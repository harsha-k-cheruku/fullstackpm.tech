# Article Idea: Building Gets Easy. Selling Gets Brutal.

**Status:** Backlog — captured 2026-03-11, updated 2026-03-11
**Type:** Blog article — Part 3 of trilogy (Productivity Paradox → Speed Drug → this)
**Priority:** Strong — connects directly to Speed Drug's donut machine + Productivity Paradox's platform capture

---

## Core Premise

AI is making building cheaper, faster, and more accessible than at any point in history.
That's not the problem.

The problem is what happens to *selling* when everyone can build.

Supply of digital goods is about to multiply several fold. Margins on software, content, tools, and services will compress toward zero. And anything that does manage to generate meaningful revenue will be noticed, cloned, or acquired by the platforms that already own distribution.

The builders win the sprint. The sellers lose the marathon. And the platforms win everything.

**The bilateral squeeze framing** is the sharpest version of this: building costs are racing toward zero from AI *and* market prices are being pressured toward zero from competition. Both ends of the margin are compressing simultaneously. That's faster and more structural than any prior tech wave.

---

## The Opening Hook — Creator Economy as the Mirror

> In 2012, any decent YouTube channel could monetize. By 2024, the middle class of creators had been squeezed out — platforms captured the value, algorithms picked the winners, and everyone else raced to the bottom on volume.
>
> B2B software is about to go through the same thing. Faster. And with less warning.

This parallel lands hard for a B2B audience because they've watched the creator economy from the outside and thought "that's a content problem, not my problem."

The point of the article: it was never a content problem. It was a supply/distribution/platform problem. And B2B builders are next.

## The Argument — Built Inductively

### 1. The Supply Explosion (Donut Machine, Again)

AI collapses the cost of production for digital goods the same way industrial machinery collapsed the cost of physical goods in the 19th century.

One person can now ship what used to take a team of five. Time-to-MVP has gone from months to weeks. The barrier to entry for software, content, design, and data products is approaching zero.

Jevons tells us what happens next: cheap production means more production, not the same production more cheaply. The supply of digital goods — apps, tools, templates, content, agents, APIs — is going to multiply several fold. Probably an order of magnitude within five years.

The donut machine running 100x production speed doesn't make anyone richer if everyone has a donut machine.

---

### 2. The B2B Version Is More Brutal Than the Creator Economy

**Important precision:** "selling gets harder" is not uniformly true. B2B enterprise selling to large organizations is arguably getting *easier* right now — buyers are desperate for AI tools and budgets are unlocking. The compression happens specifically in **consumer, prosumer, and SMB markets** where switching costs are low and ten identical alternatives appear within weeks of your launch. Naming this distinction makes the argument more credible and less pushback-able.

**The twist that makes B2B worse than creator economy:** the buyer is also getting AI-enabled.

- A B2B buyer in 2024 could evaluate 3-4 vendors in a buying cycle
- A B2B buyer in 2026 with AI can evaluate 15
- More options evaluated = more price pressure = faster commoditization

Supply multiplies AND evaluation cost drops simultaneously. That's a double squeeze the creator economy never had — creators couldn't make their audiences smarter at consuming content faster.

---

### 3. More Supply = Price Competition + Decision Fatigue + Margin Compression

When supply multiplies and demand doesn't expand proportionally:

- **Price competition** — undercutting becomes the default strategy when differentiation is hard to perceive
- **Decision fatigue** — buyers face more options than they can evaluate; they default to the cheapest, the most familiar, or the one the platform recommends
- **Decision cost** — the cognitive cost of choosing rises; "good enough and free" beats "excellent and $10/month"
- **Margin compression** — prices race toward the cost of production, which AI is driving toward zero

Physical goods went through exactly this. When manufacturing got cheap, commodity products (textiles, electronics, furniture) saw margins collapse. The survivors were either brands with genuine emotional differentiation (luxury) or platforms with distribution control (Amazon, Walmart). Everyone in the middle got squeezed out.

Software is about to experience the same thing — at 10x the speed.

---

### 4. Commoditization by Integration — The Pattern Already Has a Name

Every software platform cycle produces the same outcome: the standalone product becomes a free feature inside something bigger. AI compresses the timeline.

| Was a product | Became a feature | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| Zoom | Every OS shipped video calling | ~4 years |
| Calendly | HubSpot / Salesforce scheduling | ~3 years |
| Grammarly | Every text field got AI writing | ~3 years |
| Notion | Every tool got a docs layer | ~4 years |

With AI, a platform with distribution can now build the equivalent feature in weeks, not months. The window between "you found something that works" and "a larger player ships it as a free feature" is compressing from **3-5 years to 12-18 months**.

**The B2B AI tool lifecycle:**

```
Founder identifies pain point
    ↓
Builds AI-enabled point solution
    ↓
Gets early traction (the "hair on fire" buyers)
    ↓
Raises a round, scales GTM
    ↓
Salesforce / HubSpot / Microsoft / Google notices
    ↓
Ships native version inside existing product
    ↓
Buyer asks: "why am I paying $200/seat for this
             when my CRM does it for free?"
    ↓
Point solution either gets acquired or dies
```

### 5. The Diapers.com Playbook — Platforms Always Win at Scale

Diapers.com built a genuinely good business — fast delivery, loyal customers, good margins on baby products. Amazon noticed. Amazon dropped diaper prices below cost to match them. Diapers.com couldn't sustain the price war. Amazon acquired them in 2010 for $545M, then shut them down in 2017 once the customer relationships were absorbed.

**Important nuance:** platforms only absorb things that hit a certain scale threshold. There's a long tail of products that are too niche to be worth absorbing — the "too small for Amazon to care about" zone is actually getting *larger* as AI makes niche products viable with smaller teams. This is a genuine counterargument worth acknowledging: going narrow and weird enough may be a viable escape hatch.

But for anything that reaches real traction in a large market? The playbook runs.

---

### 6. The Three Moats AI Is Eroding

For the last 20 years, B2B software companies built moats from:

- **Data lock-in** — your data lives here, moving it is painful → AI is making data portability and migration easier
- **Workflow integration** — we're embedded in how your team works → well-resourced platforms can replicate this faster than ever
- **Network effects** — your clients/partners are also on this platform → still holds, but requires genuine community, not just shared software

What's left: **distribution, trust, and relationships.**

Which is exactly what the creator economy survivors figured out too — the creators who survived weren't the ones who made the best content. They were the ones who owned the relationship with their audience directly (email lists, communities, Patreon) rather than renting it from the platform.

The B2B equivalent: the companies that survive aren't the ones with the best AI features. They're the ones whose buyers would miss *them* — not the product, but the team, the relationship, the domain expertise embedded in the service layer.

---

### 7. The Counterargument Worth Addressing

AI also lowers the cost of *differentiation*, not just building. In theory, AI lets small teams ship more opinionated, more tailored, more specific products that serve narrower audiences better.

The question the article should wrestle with honestly: does AI-enabled differentiation outrun the noise floor it also creates?

Answer: probably not for most products, because the same AI that lets you differentiate lets your competitors differentiate too — and the platforms differentiate fastest of all. Acknowledging this makes the argument more credible, not weaker.

---

### 8. The "So What" — Distribution First, Build Second

**The key flip for PMs and builders:** AI inverts the typical build-vs-distribute priority.

Most PMs think: build first, distribution second.

The argument: that instinct is now backwards.

The question stops being "can I build this?" (yes, faster than ever) and becomes "do I have distribution for this?" A product you can build in a weekend with AI that ships to zero people is worth less than a product that takes a month but goes to 10,000 people who trust you.

The builders who win won't be the ones who build fastest. They'll be the ones who:
- Build *for* an audience they already own
- Solve problems where relationships matter more than features
- Find the spaces too small or too weird for platform players to care about
- Treat distribution as the product, not an afterthought

The donut machine metaphor closes this: everyone will have a donut machine. The question is whether you're selling donuts or whether you own the corner where people are hungry.
