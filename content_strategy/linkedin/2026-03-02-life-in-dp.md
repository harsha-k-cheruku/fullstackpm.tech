## Version 1: Main Post (Recommended)

---

Last week I published a framework for mapping AI products as sequences of deterministic (D) and probabilistic (P) steps.

Then I couldn't stop seeing it everywhere. Not in products. In life.

**Your morning routine?** D→D→D→D. Alarm off, brush teeth, make coffee. Same input, same output. You engineered it that way.

**Cooking?** Following a recipe is D. Seasoning to taste is P. The leap from cook to chef is the leap from all-D to being comfortable with P steps.

**Driving?** D→D→D on a clear day. Add rain, and every step gets a P modifier. Add construction, and your navigation goes from D to P.

**Job searching?** P→D→P→D. You control the D steps (polish resume, practice answers). You try to influence the P steps (interview chemistry, screener mood). Every piece of career advice is an attempt to convert a P step into something more D-like.

**Parenting?** P→P→P with no rollback. No staging environment. No A/B testing. Shipping to production daily with a sample size of one.

Here's the insight that made me write 2,500 words about this:

**Humans instinctively convert P steps into D steps.** That's what habits are. That's what learning is. That's what education, therapy, and "best practices" are — taking something probabilistic and making it predictable.

But some of life's best moments are the P steps we didn't convert. Falling in love. A spontaneous trip. A joke that lands perfectly.

The art of living — like the art of product management — is knowing which steps should be D and which should stay P.
